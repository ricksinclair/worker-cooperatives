# How to Open a Worker Cooperative in Wyoming

Wyoming offers a unique environment for worker cooperatives, combining exceptional tax advantages with flexible business formation laws. This guide provides a practical roadmap for forming a worker-owned cooperative in the Cowboy State.

---

## 1. Quick Summary: What Makes Wyoming Unique

Wyoming stands out as one of the most business-friendly states in the nation for several key reasons:

### Tax Advantages
- **No state income tax** for individuals or corporations - Wyoming ranks [#1 on the State Tax Competitiveness Index](https://taxfoundation.org/location/wyoming/)
- **No corporate income tax** or franchise tax
- **No personal income tax** on business income
- **Low sales tax** at 4% state rate (average combined rate of 5.56%)
- **Low property tax** with a 0.55% effective rate on owner-occupied housing
- No excise tax, estate tax, inheritance tax, or intangible tax

### Business Environment
- **Flexible LLC laws** with strong asset protection provisions
- **Strong privacy protections** - member names and addresses are not required on Articles of Organization
- **Small population** (approximately 580,000) with a close-knit business community
- **Streamlined formation** - online LLC filings are approved immediately
- **Low filing fees** - $100 for LLC formation, $60 minimum annual report

### Considerations
- **Limited local market** - smallest state population in the U.S.
- **Geographic isolation** - may affect distribution and supply chains
- **No dedicated worker cooperative statute** - but flexible laws accommodate cooperative structures
- **Limited cooperative development infrastructure** compared to coastal states

## 2. Best-Fit Legal Structures for Worker Coops in Wyoming

Wyoming does not have a dedicated worker cooperative statute. However, the state offers several flexible legal structures that can effectively accommodate worker-owned cooperative businesses.

### Option 1: Limited Liability Company (LLC) with Cooperative Operating Agreement

**Recommended for most worker cooperatives**

Wyoming's [LLC statute (Title 17, Chapter 29)](https://law.justia.com/codes/wyoming/title-17/chapter-29/article-1/section-17-29-110/) is among the most flexible in the nation, making it an excellent vehicle for worker cooperatives.

**Key advantages:**
- **Maximum flexibility** - operating agreements can establish one-member-one-vote governance
- **Strong asset protection** - robust charging order protections
- **Pass-through taxation** - profits and losses flow directly to members
- **Privacy** - member information not publicly disclosed
- **Simple formation** - immediate online approval

**How to structure cooperatively:**
- Operating agreement establishes democratic governance (one member, one vote)
- Membership tied to employment status
- Profit distributions based on labor contribution (patronage) rather than capital investment
- Buy-in and buy-out provisions for worker-members

**Limitations:**
- Does not receive automatic Subchapter T tax treatment (may elect; consult tax advisor)
- Must carefully draft operating agreement to establish cooperative principles
- May not be recognized as a "cooperative" for certain grants or programs

### Option 2: Wyoming Processing Cooperative (Title 17, Chapter 10, Article 2)

Wyoming's [Processing Cooperative Statute](https://law.justia.com/codes/wyoming/title-17/chapter-10/article-2/) was adopted in 2001 and offers a hybrid structure with unique features.

**Key features:**
- Organized as an **unincorporated association** similar to an LLC
- Allows both **patron and non-patron investment**
- **Pass-through taxation** on both patron and non-patron earnings
- Designed for flexibility in capital raising

**Governance provisions include:**
- [Board governance requirements (17-10-215)](https://law.justia.com/codes/wyoming/title-17/chapter-10/article-2/)
- Member voting and grouping provisions
- Allocation and distribution rules

**Best suited for:**
- Agricultural producer cooperatives
- Cooperatives seeking outside investment
- Businesses processing agricultural products

**Considerations:**
- Originally designed for agricultural cooperatives
- May require more complex governance structure
- Less commonly used than LLC structure

### Option 3: Corporation with Cooperative Bylaws

A standard Wyoming corporation can be structured with cooperative principles embedded in bylaws.

**When to consider:**
- Planning to seek venture capital or traditional investors
- Considering conversion to an ESOP in the future
- Operating in a highly regulated industry requiring corporate structure

**Limitations:**
- Corporate formalities required (annual meetings, board elections, minutes)
- Double taxation unless S-corp election is made
- Less flexibility than LLC structure

### Option 4: Nonprofit Corporation (for Limited Circumstances)

Under the [Wyoming Nonprofit Corporation Act](https://law.justia.com/codes/wyoming/title-17/chapter-19/), a cooperative may organize as a nonprofit if:
- Primary purpose is community benefit rather than member profit
- Members willing to forgo profit distributions
- Seeking foundation grants requiring 501(c)(3) status

**Limitations:**
- Cannot distribute profits to members
- Complex IRS requirements for tax exemption
- Limited applicability for most worker cooperatives

### Recommended Structure Comparison

| Feature | Cooperative LLC | Processing Cooperative | Corporation |
|---------|----------------|----------------------|-------------|
| Formation complexity | Low | Medium | Medium |
| Governance flexibility | High | Medium | Low |
| Outside investment | Difficult | Designed for it | Standard |
| Tax treatment | Pass-through | Pass-through | Double (unless S-corp) |
| Privacy | High | Medium | Low |
| Recognition as "cooperative" | By structure only | Statutory | By bylaws only |

## 3. Step-by-Step Formation Checklist

### Phase 1: Planning and Preparation (2-4 weeks)

**Step 1: Organize Your Founding Team**
- [ ] Identify founding worker-members (minimum 1 for LLC)
- [ ] Establish shared vision and mission
- [ ] Determine initial capital contributions
- [ ] Agree on governance principles

**Step 2: Conduct Business Planning**
- [ ] Develop comprehensive business plan
- [ ] Create financial projections
- [ ] Identify target market and competitive landscape
- [ ] Contact [Wyoming SBDC](https://wyomingsbdc.org/) for free business advising

**Step 3: Search for Name Availability**
- [ ] Search the [Wyoming Secretary of State Business Database](https://wyobiz.wyo.gov/business/filingsearch.aspx)
- [ ] Ensure name ends with "LLC," "L.L.C.," or similar designator
- [ ] Check for trademark conflicts at [USPTO](https://www.uspto.gov/trademarks)
- [ ] Consider reserving name for 120 days ($60 fee) if not ready to file

**Important naming notes:**
- Names starting with "A" (like "A-1 Construction LLC") require paper filing for manual review
- Name must be distinguishable from other registered entities
- Cannot include restricted terms like "Bank" without licensing

### Phase 2: Legal Formation (1-2 weeks)

**Step 4: Select a Registered Agent**
Wyoming requires every LLC to maintain a [registered agent](https://sos.wyo.gov/Business/Docs/HowToFindOrBecomeARegisteredAgent.pdf) with a physical address in the state.

Requirements:
- [ ] Must be a Wyoming resident individual (18+) OR
- [ ] A business entity authorized to transact business in Wyoming
- [ ] Must have a physical street address (no P.O. boxes)
- [ ] Must be available during business hours

Options:
- A founding member with a Wyoming address
- Commercial registered agent service ($25-150/year)
- Wyoming attorney

**Step 5: Draft Operating Agreement**
The operating agreement is critical for establishing cooperative principles. Include:

- [ ] **Membership provisions**
  - Who qualifies for membership
  - Buy-in requirements and process
  - Probationary period (if any)
  - Exit and buyout procedures

- [ ] **Governance structure**
  - One member, one vote principle
  - Board of directors or managers
  - Meeting requirements and quorum
  - Decision-making processes

- [ ] **Capital accounts**
  - Initial contributions
  - Retained patronage allocations
  - Internal capital account system

- [ ] **Profit distribution**
  - Patronage-based allocation formula
  - Retained vs. distributed earnings
  - Payment timing

- [ ] **Dissolution provisions**
  - Process for winding up
  - Distribution of assets

Consider using templates from:
- [Democracy at Work Institute](https://institute.coop/publications)
- [Sustainable Economies Law Center](https://www.theselc.org/)
- Cooperative-experienced Wyoming attorney

**Step 6: File Articles of Organization**

**Online filing (recommended):**
1. Visit [WyoBiz.wyo.gov](https://wyobiz.wyo.gov/Business/RegistrationInstr.aspx)
2. Create an account
3. Complete Articles of Organization form
4. Pay $100 filing fee (+$4 credit card fee)
5. Receive immediate approval

**Paper filing:**
1. Download [Articles of Organization form](https://sos.wyo.gov/forms/business/llc/llc-articlesorganization.pdf)
2. Complete and sign
3. Include signed Registered Agent Consent form
4. Mail with $100 check to:

   Wyoming Secretary of State
   Herschler Building East, Suite 101
   122 W 25th Street
   Cheyenne, WY 82002-0020

5. Processing time: 10-15 business days

**Step 7: Obtain Federal Employer Identification Number (EIN)**
- [ ] Apply online at [IRS.gov](https://www.irs.gov/businesses/small-businesses-self-employed/apply-for-an-employer-identification-number-ein-online)
- [ ] Free and immediate
- [ ] Required for bank accounts, hiring employees, tax filings

### Phase 3: State Tax Registration (1-2 weeks)

**Step 8: Register with Wyoming Department of Revenue**

Wyoming has no income tax, but if you sell taxable goods or services:

- [ ] Register for Sales and Use Tax License via [Wyoming Internet Filing System (WYIFS)](https://excise-wyifs.wy.gov/)
- [ ] Pay $60 one-time license fee
- [ ] Processing time: approximately 10 business days

**Registration required if:**
- Selling tangible personal property
- Providing taxable services
- Selling admissions

**Contact:** [Wyoming Department of Revenue](https://revenue.wyo.gov/)
- Phone: 307-777-5200
- Email: dor@wyo.gov

### Phase 4: Local Licensing and Permits (1-4 weeks)

**Step 9: Obtain Local Business Licenses**

Wyoming has [no statewide general business license](https://www.llcuniversity.com/wyoming-llc/business-licenses-permits/). Requirements vary by municipality.

- [ ] Check with your city clerk's office for local business license
- [ ] Obtain zoning permit if operating from physical location
- [ ] Apply for home occupation permit if home-based
- [ ] Secure building permits for any construction/renovation
- [ ] Obtain health permits if handling food
- [ ] Get fire department permits if applicable

**Major city contacts:**
- Cheyenne: [City of Cheyenne Business Resources](https://www.cheyennecity.org/)
- Casper: [City of Casper](https://www.casperwy.gov/)
- Laramie: [City of Laramie](https://www.cityoflaramie.org/)

**Step 10: Industry-Specific Licenses**
- [ ] Contractor licenses (issued locally, not by state)
- [ ] Food manufacturing: [Wyoming Department of Agriculture](https://agriculture.wy.gov/)
- [ ] Alcohol: [Wyoming Liquor Division](https://revenue.wyo.gov/liquor-division)
- [ ] Professional services: Various state boards

### Phase 5: Operational Setup (Ongoing)

**Step 11: Set Up Financial Infrastructure**
- [ ] Open business bank account with EIN and Articles
- [ ] Establish accounting system tracking patronage
- [ ] Set up payroll system (if paying wages)
- [ ] Consider capital account tracking software

**Step 12: Employment and Labor Compliance**
- [ ] Register for Wyoming unemployment insurance
- [ ] Obtain workers' compensation insurance
- [ ] Post required workplace notices
- [ ] Create employee handbook incorporating cooperative principles

**Step 13: Ongoing Compliance**
- [ ] File Annual Report by anniversary month (minimum $60)
- [ ] File sales tax returns (monthly, quarterly, or annually based on volume)
- [ ] Maintain registered agent
- [ ] Hold annual member meetings per operating agreement
- [ ] Maintain corporate records

## 4. Governance and Documents You Should Prepare

### Essential Formation Documents

#### 1. Articles of Organization
Basic formation document filed with Secretary of State. Includes:
- LLC name
- Registered agent name and address
- Organizer information
- Management structure (member-managed or manager-managed)

#### 2. Operating Agreement (Critical for Cooperatives)
The operating agreement transforms a standard LLC into a worker cooperative. Key sections:

**Membership and Ownership**
```
- Membership classes (worker-member, provisional member)
- Qualifications for membership
- Buy-in amount and payment terms
- Probationary period (typically 3-12 months)
- Capital account system
```

**Governance Structure**
```
- Democratic control: one member, one vote
- Board of directors composition and election
- Officer positions and responsibilities
- Meeting requirements (annual, quarterly, special)
- Quorum requirements
- Voting thresholds for different decisions
```

**Financial Provisions**
```
- Patronage allocation formula (based on hours worked, wages, etc.)
- Retained earnings policy
- Distribution timing and methods
- Reserve fund requirements
```

**Member Rights and Responsibilities**
```
- Work requirements
- Decision-making participation
- Access to financial information
- Confidentiality obligations
```

**Separation Provisions**
```
- Voluntary departure process
- Involuntary termination procedures
- Capital account redemption timeline
- Non-compete provisions (if any)
```

#### 3. Membership Agreement
Individual agreement signed by each worker-member covering:
- Acknowledgment of operating agreement terms
- Capital contribution commitment
- Work expectations
- Confidentiality provisions

#### 4. Patronage Allocation Policy
Document explaining how profits are allocated, including:
- Calculation methodology
- Qualification requirements
- Payment schedule
- Tax implications

### Governance Best Practices

**Board Structure Options:**
1. **All-member board** - Every worker-member serves on board (best for small cooperatives)
2. **Elected board** - Members elect representatives (better for larger cooperatives)
3. **Hybrid** - Combination with some reserved seats

**Committee Structure:**
- Finance/Audit Committee
- Membership Committee
- Personnel/HR Committee
- Strategic Planning Committee

**Meeting Cadence:**
- Annual membership meeting (required)
- Quarterly board meetings (recommended)
- Monthly operational meetings (as needed)

### Record-Keeping Requirements

Maintain the following records:
- [ ] Articles of Organization and amendments
- [ ] Operating agreement and amendments
- [ ] Member meeting minutes
- [ ] Board meeting minutes
- [ ] Financial statements
- [ ] Member capital account records
- [ ] Patronage allocation records
- [ ] Tax filings

## 6. Worker-Ownership Conversions

Converting an existing business to worker ownership can preserve jobs and maintain community businesses when owners retire or exit.

### Why Consider Conversion in Wyoming

- **Baby Boomer retirements** - Many business owners lack succession plans
- **Rural community preservation** - Keep businesses and jobs local
- **Tax benefits** - Section 1042 capital gains deferral available
- **Employee loyalty** - Reward long-term employees

### Conversion Process Overview

#### Step 1: Assess Feasibility
- Is the business profitable and sustainable?
- Are employees interested in ownership?
- What is fair market value?
- What financing is available?

**Resources:**
- [Become Worker Owned](https://www.becomeworkerowned.org/) - Free assessment tools
- Wyoming SBDC for business valuation assistance

#### Step 2: Choose Conversion Structure

**Direct Sale to Worker Cooperative**
- Employees form cooperative and purchase business
- Can be structured as asset or stock purchase
- Seller may provide financing

**Partial Conversion**
- Gradual transfer of ownership over time
- Hybrid structures (some employee ownership, some outside)

**Comparison with ESOP:**

| Factor | Worker Cooperative | ESOP |
|--------|-------------------|------|
| Size | Any size, often smaller | Usually 15+ employees |
| Governance | Democratic (one person, one vote) | Varies (trustee-controlled) |
| Cost to establish | Lower | Higher ($50,000-150,000+) |
| Tax benefits | Section 1042 available | Section 1042 + additional benefits |
| Ownership culture | Direct worker control | Retirement benefit focus |

#### Step 3: Structure the Transaction

**Asset Purchase:**
- Cooperative buys business assets
- Simpler structure
- May have tax advantages

**Stock/Membership Purchase:**
- Cooperative acquires ownership interests
- Maintains business continuity
- May qualify for Section 1042

#### Step 4: Finance the Conversion

**Seller Financing:**
- Owner carries note for portion of purchase price
- Common in cooperative conversions
- Aligns seller interest with business success

**Bank Financing:**
- SBA loans can finance acquisitions
- Requires down payment and collateral

**Member Equity:**
- Employee contributions to purchase price
- May be deducted from future wages

#### Step 5: Transition Operations

- Train workers in governance and ownership
- Establish management systems
- Develop cooperative culture
- Implement patronage accounting

### Tax Benefits for Sellers

**Section 1042 Capital Gains Deferral**
Business owners selling to worker cooperatives (or ESOPs) may defer capital gains taxes if:
- Sale is at least 30% of company to qualifying employee ownership
- Proceeds are reinvested in "qualified replacement property" within 12 months
- Company has been a C corporation (some limitations apply)

**Consult a tax professional** for specific guidance on eligibility and structuring.

### Conversion Resources

- [Democracy at Work Institute - Becoming Employee Owned](https://institute.coop/sites/default/files/COT_BecomingEmployeeOwned_FINALweb.pdf)
- [Project Equity](https://project-equity.org/) - National conversion assistance
- [Cooperative Development Institute](https://cdi.coop/) - Conversion support

## 7. Common Pitfalls in Wyoming

### Formation Pitfalls

**1. Assuming No Taxes Means No Tax Compliance**
- Wyoming has no income tax, but federal taxes still apply
- Must file federal returns and pay self-employment taxes
- Sales tax obligations if selling taxable goods/services
- Employment taxes if you have employees

**2. Inadequate Operating Agreement**
- Generic LLC operating agreements do not establish cooperative principles
- Must specifically address one-member-one-vote governance
- Patronage allocation provisions essential for Subchapter T treatment
- Exit and buyout provisions critical for member transitions

**3. Failing to Establish Clear Membership Criteria**
- Who qualifies for membership?
- What is the probationary period?
- How are capital contributions handled?
- Clear criteria prevent disputes

**4. Underestimating Capital Needs**
- Wyoming's small population limits local market
- May need more working capital for market development
- Member buy-ins alone rarely sufficient

### Governance Pitfalls

**5. Insufficient Member Education**
- Worker-owners need training in cooperative governance
- Financial literacy essential for informed decision-making
- Ongoing education budget recommended

**6. Confusing Governance and Management**
- Board sets policy; management handles operations
- Clear role definitions prevent conflict
- Especially important in small cooperatives where roles overlap

**7. Inadequate Meeting Discipline**
- Operating agreement meeting requirements must be followed
- Minutes must be kept
- Decisions outside proper process may be challenged

### Financial Pitfalls

**8. Improper Patronage Accounting**
- Must track patronage allocations by member
- Written patronage notices required for Subchapter T
- Consult cooperative-experienced accountant

**9. Undercapitalization**
- Cooperatives often underfunded at start
- Capital reserves essential for sustainability
- Plan for equipment replacement and growth

**10. Ignoring Multi-State Obligations**
- Wyoming tax advantages only apply to Wyoming activities
- Operations in other states trigger those states' requirements
- Common issue for remote service cooperatives

### Operational Pitfalls

**11. Geographic Isolation Challenges**
- Wyoming's low population density limits local markets
- Consider regional or online sales strategies
- Supply chain logistics may be more complex

**12. Limited Professional Support**
- Few Wyoming attorneys specialize in cooperatives
- May need out-of-state expertise
- Budget for professional services

**13. Neglecting Annual Report**
- Due first day of anniversary month
- Only $60 minimum fee, but failure results in dissolution
- Set calendar reminders

### Cultural Pitfalls

**14. Hiring Without Membership Track**
- All long-term workers should have path to membership
- Non-member employees can create two-tier workplace
- Clear policies on membership timeline

**15. Insufficient Conflict Resolution**
- Democratic workplaces have different conflicts
- Establish clear grievance procedures
- Consider mediation provisions in operating agreement

## 8. Frequently Asked Questions (FAQs) for Wyoming

### Q1: Does Wyoming have a specific worker cooperative law?

**A:** No. Wyoming does not have a dedicated worker cooperative statute like California or Colorado. However, Wyoming's flexible [LLC statute](https://law.justia.com/codes/wyoming/title-17/chapter-29/article-1/section-17-29-110/) allows you to structure an LLC with cooperative governance principles through the operating agreement. Wyoming also has a [Processing Cooperative statute](https://law.justia.com/codes/wyoming/title-17/chapter-10/article-2/) that, while designed for agricultural cooperatives, can be used for other types of cooperatives.

---

### Q2: What are the total costs to form a worker cooperative LLC in Wyoming?

**A:** Basic formation costs include:
- Articles of Organization: **$100** (online) or **$104** (with credit card fee)
- Name reservation (optional): **$60** for 120 days
- Registered agent: **$0** (if member serves) or **$25-150/year** (commercial service)
- EIN: **Free**
- Sales tax license (if needed): **$60**
- Annual report: **$60** minimum per year

Total first-year minimum: approximately **$160-320** (plus legal fees for operating agreement drafting, typically $1,500-5,000 for a cooperative-specific document).

---

### Q3: Do I need a registered agent in Wyoming?

**A:** Yes. All Wyoming LLCs must continuously maintain a [registered agent](https://sos.wyo.gov/Business/Docs/HowToFindOrBecomeARegisteredAgent.pdf) with a physical address in Wyoming. The agent can be:
- An individual Wyoming resident (such as a founding member)
- A business entity authorized to operate in Wyoming
- A commercial registered agent service

The agent receives legal documents and official correspondence on behalf of your cooperative.

---

### Q4: How does Wyoming's lack of income tax affect my cooperative?

**A:** Wyoming's absence of state income tax provides significant advantages:
- **No state corporate tax** on cooperative earnings
- **No individual state tax** on member patronage distributions
- **No state withholding** from worker-member wages (though federal withholding still applies)

However, you must still comply with:
- Federal income taxes (Form 1065 for partnerships/LLCs, potentially Subchapter T treatment)
- Self-employment taxes for members
- Sales and use taxes if selling taxable goods/services
- Employment taxes if paying wages

The federal tax treatment of your cooperative depends on its structure and elections. Consult a tax professional experienced with cooperatives.

---

### Q5: Can I form a cooperative with just one person in Wyoming?

**A:** Legally, yes - Wyoming allows single-member LLCs. However, a **single-member business is not a cooperative** in any meaningful sense. The cooperative model is based on collective ownership and democratic governance among multiple worker-owners.

For a viable worker cooperative:
- **Minimum practical number:** 2-3 founding worker-members
- **Ideal founding size:** 3-7 committed worker-members
- Plan for growth and new member recruitment

If starting alone, you can form an LLC structured to convert to cooperative governance as you add worker-members.

---

### Q6: What ongoing filing requirements exist for Wyoming cooperatives?

**A:** Wyoming has relatively minimal ongoing requirements:

**Required:**
- **Annual Report:** Due first day of your formation anniversary month; minimum $60 fee (based on Wyoming assets)
- **Registered Agent:** Must maintain continuously
- **Sales Tax Returns:** Monthly, quarterly, or annually if you have a sales tax license

**Recommended but not state-mandated:**
- Annual member meeting (should be in operating agreement)
- Updated member records
- Financial statements
- Patronage allocation notices (required for Subchapter T treatment)

Failure to file the annual report within 60 days of the due date results in administrative dissolution.

---

### Q7: Are there grants available for worker cooperatives in Wyoming?

**A:** Direct grants specifically for worker cooperatives are limited in Wyoming. Available resources include:

**State programs:**
- [Wyoming Business Council](https://wyomingbusiness.org/business/financing/grants/) startup grants (limited, specific criteria)
- [SBIR Match Grants](https://wyomingbusiness.org/business/financing/grants/sbir/) for federal SBIR/STTR recipients
- Business Ready Community grants (infrastructure, through local governments)

**Federal programs:**
- SBA loans (not grants, but favorable terms)
- USDA Rural Development programs (for rural cooperatives)
- USDA Value-Added Producer Grants (for agricultural cooperatives)

**National cooperative resources:**
- Cooperative Development Foundation grants
- Foundation grants (various, typically require 501(c)(3) fiscal sponsor)

The [Wyoming SBDC](https://wyomingsbdc.org/) can help identify applicable funding sources for your specific cooperative.

---

### Q8: How do I find a lawyer or accountant who understands cooperatives in Wyoming?

**A:** Cooperative-specific expertise may be limited locally. Options include:

**Finding professionals:**
- [USFWC Referral Directory](https://www.usworker.coop/resources/) - National network of cooperative-friendly professionals
- [DAWI Referrals](https://institute.coop/resources/referrals) - Attorneys, accountants, and developers
- Wyoming State Bar lawyer referral service (ask about business/cooperative experience)
- Wyoming SBDC may have local referrals

**What to look for:**
- Experience with LLC operating agreements
- Understanding of Subchapter T taxation
- Familiarity with patronage accounting
- Experience with cooperative governance

Many cooperative attorneys work remotely and can assist Wyoming cooperatives from other states. Expect to pay $200-400/hour for specialized cooperative legal work.

   - Use [Wyoming business search](https://wyobiz.wyo.gov/business/filingsearch.aspx)
   - Reserve name if needed ($60 for 120 days)

6. **Identify professional support**
   - Find cooperative-experienced attorney
   - Locate accountant familiar with patronage accounting
   - Engage Wyoming SBDC for business planning

### Medium-Term Actions (60-90 Days)

7. **Complete legal formation**
   - Finalize operating agreement with attorney
   - File Articles of Organization
   - Obtain EIN

8. **Set up operations**
   - Open business bank account
   - Establish accounting systems
   - Obtain necessary licenses and permits

9. **Launch and grow**
   - Begin operations
   - Onboard founding worker-members
   - Develop member education program
