# How to Start a Worker Cooperative in Florida

> **Disclaimer:** This guide provides general information and is not legal or tax advice. Formation requirements and fees change periodically. Always verify current requirements with official state sources and consult with a qualified attorney and accountant familiar with cooperative business structures before forming your worker cooperative.

---

## 1. Quick Summary

**What makes Florida unique for worker cooperatives:**

Florida does **not** have a dedicated worker cooperative corporation statute like California, Colorado, or some other states. Instead, worker cooperatives in Florida typically form using existing general business entity laws, most commonly as a **Limited Liability Company (LLC)** with a cooperative-style operating agreement.

Florida offers several advantages for cooperative businesses:
- **No state income tax** on individuals, which can benefit worker-owners
- **Business-friendly environment** with streamlined online filing through Sunbiz
- **Growing cooperative ecosystem**, particularly in South Florida with organizations like Catalyst Miami
- **Strong SBDC network** with over 40 offices providing free business consulting
- **Florida Center for Employee Ownership (FLCEO)** dedicated to promoting worker cooperatives, ESOPs, and EOTs

**Common entity choices for Florida worker cooperatives:**
1. **Limited Liability Company (LLC)** - Most popular choice; flexible operating agreement can incorporate cooperative principles
2. **Corporation** (Chapter 607) - Traditional corporate structure with cooperative bylaws
3. **Nonprofit Cooperative Association** (Chapter 619) - Limited to agricultural products and related activities
4. **Agricultural Cooperative Marketing Association** (Chapter 618) - For agricultural producers only

## 2. Best-Fit Legal Structures for Worker Cooperatives in Florida

### Overview of Available Structures

Since Florida lacks a dedicated worker cooperative statute, founders must adapt existing business entity laws to create cooperative governance and economics.

### Option A: LLC with Cooperative Operating Agreement (Most Common)

The **Limited Liability Company** is the most popular structure for worker cooperatives in Florida due to its flexibility.

**Advantages:**
- Operating agreement can be customized to include cooperative principles (one member, one vote; patronage-based allocations)
- Pass-through taxation avoids double taxation
- Limited liability protection for members
- Internal capital accounts can be structured for patronage accounting similar to the Mondragon model
- Fewer formalities than corporations
- Members can be classified as employees for purposes of employment benefits

**Key Considerations:**
- Florida does not require an operating agreement by law (per Section 605.0105), but it is essential for cooperative governance
- Some states restrict using "cooperative" in an LLC name; verify with the Division of Corporations
- Members of an LLC are not generally considered employees by default, which may provide flexibility regarding minimum wage, overtime, and workers' compensation requirements

**Relevant Statute:** [Florida Revised Limited Liability Company Act, Chapter 605](https://www.flsenate.gov/Laws/Statutes/2024/Chapter605)

### Option B: Corporation with Cooperative Bylaws

A traditional **for-profit corporation** under Chapter 607 can be structured with cooperative bylaws.

**Advantages:**
- Well-established legal framework
- Can issue stock to worker-members
- Clear separation between ownership and management roles

**Disadvantages:**
- More formalities (board meetings, minutes, etc.)
- Potential double taxation unless S-Corp election is made
- Less flexibility than an LLC

**Relevant Statute:** [Florida Business Corporation Act, Chapter 607](https://law.justia.com/codes/florida/title-xxxvi/chapter-607/)

### Option C: Nonprofit Cooperative Association (Chapter 619)

Florida Statutes Chapter 619 provides for **Nonprofit Cooperative Associations**, but this structure is **limited to specific agricultural activities**.

**Who can use this structure:**
- Three or more persons engaged in production, preserving, drying, packing, canning, bottling, shipping, or marketing of agricultural products
- Three or more existing associations

**Key Features:**
- Nonprofit status
- Bylaws must be adopted within 30 days of incorporation
- Majority vote required for bylaw adoption
- Two-thirds vote required for charter amendments

**Limitations:**
- Restricted to agricultural products as defined in Section 618.01
- Not suitable for general worker cooperatives in non-agricultural sectors

**Relevant Statute:** [Chapter 619, Florida Statutes](https://www.flsenate.gov/Laws/Statutes/2024/Chapter619/All)

### Option D: Agricultural Cooperative Marketing Association (Chapter 618)

For agricultural producers, Chapter 618 provides a cooperative structure specifically for marketing associations.

**Who can use this structure:**
- Three or more persons engaged in production of agricultural products
- Three or more existing associations

**Key Features:**
- Can engage in cooperative activity related to producing, marketing, or selling agricultural products
- Marketing contracts can require members to sell exclusively through the association
- Not considered in restraint of trade

**Relevant Statute:** [Chapter 618, Florida Statutes](https://law.justia.com/codes/florida/title-xxxvi/chapter-618/)

### Worker-Cooperative-Friendly Provisions Worth Noting

While Florida lacks specific worker cooperative provisions, the following aspects of Florida law support cooperative structures:

1. **LLC Operating Agreement Flexibility:** Section 605.0105 allows extensive customization of governance, voting, and profit allocation
2. **No State Income Tax:** Florida has no individual state income tax, simplifying patronage distributions to worker-members
3. **Protected Series LLCs:** Effective July 1, 2026, Florida will recognize Protected Series LLCs (Chapter 605), which may offer new structuring options for multi-location cooperatives

## 3. Step-by-Step Formation Checklist

### Step 1: Name Search and Reservation

**Search for name availability:**
- Use the [Sunbiz Entity Name Search](https://dos.fl.gov/sunbiz/search/) to check if your desired name is available
- Name must be distinguishable from existing entities
- For LLCs, name must include "Limited Liability Company," "L.L.C.," or "LLC"
- For corporations, name must include "Corporation," "Company," "Incorporated," or appropriate abbreviations

**Reserve your name (optional):**
- File a [Name Reservation](https://dos.fl.gov/sunbiz/forms/) for $25
- Reservation is valid for 120 days

**Note:** Verify with the Division of Corporations whether "cooperative" or "co-op" can be included in an LLC or corporation name in Florida.

### Step 2: Formation Filing

**For an LLC:**

1. **File Articles of Organization** online through [Sunbiz E-Filing](https://dos.fl.gov/sunbiz/start-business/efile/fl-llc/)
2. **Filing Fee:** $125
3. **Required Information:**
   - LLC name
   - Principal place of business address (Florida street address)
   - Mailing address
   - Registered agent name and Florida street address
   - Management structure (member-managed or manager-managed)
   - Effective date (if different from filing date)
   - Authorized signature

4. **Optional Additions:**
   - Certified Copy: $30
   - Certificate of Status: $5

**For a Corporation:**

1. **File Articles of Incorporation** through [Sunbiz](https://dos.fl.gov/sunbiz/forms/corporations/)
2. **Filing Fee:** $70
3. **Additional Fees:**
   - Certified Copy: $8.75
   - Certificate of Status: $8.75

**Filing Portal:** [Sunbiz.org - Division of Corporations](https://dos.fl.gov/sunbiz/)

**Contact Information:**
- New Filings: efile@dos.myflorida.com
- General Inquiries: corphelp@dos.myflorida.com

### Step 3: Registered Agent Requirements

Every Florida LLC and corporation must have a registered agent:

**Requirements:**
- Must be either:
  - An individual who is a Florida resident, OR
  - A business entity authorized to do business in Florida
- Must have a physical street address in Florida (P.O. Box not accepted)
- Must be available during normal business hours to receive legal documents
- Must sign the formation documents (electronic signature acceptable for online filing)
- An entity cannot serve as its own registered agent, but an individual associated with the business may serve

**Changing Registered Agent:**
- File a Statement of Change of Registered Agent through Sunbiz
- Fee varies; check current fee schedule

### Step 4: Obtain Federal Employer Identification Number (EIN)

**All worker cooperatives need an EIN for:**
- Opening a business bank account
- Filing federal tax returns
- Hiring employees
- Reporting payroll taxes

**How to Apply:**
- **Online (Free):** [IRS EIN Assistant](https://www.irs.gov/businesses/small-businesses-self-employed/apply-for-an-employer-identification-number-ein-online) - Immediate issuance
- **By Mail/Fax:** Form SS-4

### Step 5: State Tax Registration

**Florida Department of Revenue Registration:**

If your cooperative will sell taxable goods or services, collect rent, or engage in other taxable activities, you must register as a sales and use tax dealer.

**How to Register:**
1. **Online:** [Florida Business Tax Application](https://taxapps.floridarevenue.com/taxregistration)
2. **By Mail:** Complete Form DR-1

**What You Receive:**
- Certificate of Registration (Form DR-11) - must be displayed at your business location
- Florida Annual Resale Certificate (Form DR-13)
- Tax return forms

**Key Points:**
- Separate registration required for each Florida business location
- Sales tax returns typically filed quarterly for new businesses
- Filing frequency based on sales tax liability
- Returns due by the 20th of the month following reporting period

**Sales Tax Rate:** 6% state rate plus applicable county discretionary sales surtax (varies by county, typically 0.5% to 1.5%)

**Important Note:** Florida has no state corporate income tax for pass-through entities like LLCs. C-corporations pay a 5.5% corporate income tax on Florida taxable income over $50,000.

**Registration Portal:** [Florida Department of Revenue](https://floridarevenue.com/taxes/eservices/Pages/registration.aspx)

### Step 6: Local Business Licensing

Florida does not issue a universal statewide business license. Licensing requirements depend on your specific business activities and location.

**Business Tax Receipt (BTR):**
- Most Florida counties require a Business Tax Receipt (formerly called "occupational license")
- Required for nearly all businesses, including home-based operations
- Many cities require a municipal BTR in addition to county BTR

**Counties requiring BTR include:**
- Miami-Dade County
- Broward County
- Hillsborough County (Tampa area)
- Orange County (Orlando area)
- Palm Beach County
- Pinellas County
- Duval County (Jacksonville area)
- Lee County
- Polk County
- Brevard County

**How to Obtain:**
- Contact your county Tax Collector's office
- Contact your city's business licensing department if located within city limits
- Some counties (like Palm Beach) allow online applications

**State Professional Licensing:**
- Check the [Florida Licensing Portal](https://www.myfloridalicense.com/wl11.asp) for profession-specific requirements
- Florida Department of Business and Professional Regulation (DBPR) handles many professional licenses
- Florida Department of Agriculture and Consumer Services (DACS) handles others

**Additional Local Requirements May Include:**
- Certificate of Occupancy
- Certificate of Use
- Zoning approval
- Health department permits (for food-related businesses)
- Fire department permits

**State Licensing Portal:** [MyFloridaLicense.com](https://www.myfloridalicense.com/)

### Step 7: Annual Report Filing

**LLC Annual Report:**
- **Due:** January 1 - May 1 each year
- **Fee:** $138.75
- **Late Fee:** $400 (assessed after May 1)
- **Consequence of Non-Filing:** Administrative dissolution

**Corporation Annual Report:**
- **Due:** January 1 - May 1 each year
- **Fee:** $150 (for-profit); $61.25 (nonprofit)
- **Late Fee:** $400

**File Online:** [Sunbiz Annual Report Filing](https://services.sunbiz.org/Filings/AnnualReport/FilingStart)

## 4. Governance and Documents to Prepare

### Essential Governing Documents

#### For LLCs: Operating Agreement

While Florida law does not require a written operating agreement, it is **essential** for worker cooperatives. Your operating agreement should address:

**1. Cooperative Principles:**
- Statement of cooperative purpose and values
- Commitment to International Cooperative Alliance principles
- Definition of worker-member status

**2. Membership:**
- Eligibility requirements (e.g., minimum hours worked, probationary period)
- Buy-in amount and payment terms
- Member classes (if any): worker-members, non-worker members, investor members
- Process for admitting new members
- Grounds and process for termination of membership

**3. Voting Rights:**
- One member, one vote (regardless of capital contribution)
- Decisions requiring simple majority vs. supermajority vs. unanimous consent
- Quorum requirements
- Proxy voting policies
- Meeting procedures (notice, frequency)

**4. Capital Accounts and Patronage:**
- Initial capital contribution requirements
- Internal capital account system
- Patronage allocation formula (typically based on hours worked or wages earned)
- Retained patronage (written notice of allocation)
- Redemption of capital accounts upon departure
- Redemption schedule and priority

**5. Profit and Loss Allocation:**
- Allocation based on patronage (labor contribution) rather than capital investment
- Reserve requirements
- Distribution timing and approval process

**6. Management and Officers:**
- Management structure (member-managed vs. manager-managed)
- Board of Directors composition and election
- Officer positions and responsibilities
- Committees (e.g., membership committee, finance committee)
- Compensation policies

**7. Meetings:**
- Annual membership meeting requirements
- Special meeting procedures
- Board meeting frequency
- Record-keeping requirements

**8. Transfer Restrictions:**
- Prohibition on transfer of membership interests to non-members
- Right of first refusal
- Buyback provisions

**9. Dissolution:**
- Process for voluntary dissolution
- Distribution of assets upon dissolution (typically to other cooperatives or charitable purposes)

**10. Dispute Resolution:**
- Internal grievance procedures
- Mediation and arbitration provisions

#### For Corporations: Bylaws

If forming as a corporation, prepare bylaws that incorporate cooperative principles:

- Democratic voting (one share per member, or separate voting shares)
- Patronage dividend provisions
- Worker-member eligibility
- Board election procedures
- Meeting requirements

#### Additional Documents to Prepare

1. **Member Handbook:** Policies, procedures, job descriptions, workplace expectations
2. **Membership Agreement:** Individual agreement signed by each worker-member
3. **Patron Disclosure Notice:** Required for tax purposes to allocate patronage
4. **Employment Policies:** Even if members are owners, clear employment policies protect everyone
5. **Buy-Sell Agreement:** Terms for member departure, death, disability
6. **Conflict of Interest Policy:** Essential for cooperative governance

## 6. Worker-Ownership Conversions

### Overview

Converting an existing business to worker ownership is often more successful than starting a cooperative from scratch, particularly for established businesses with experienced workers. In Florida, conversions typically use either the **ESOP** or **worker cooperative** model.

### ESOP vs. Worker Cooperative Conversion

| Feature | ESOP | Worker Cooperative |
|---------|------|-------------------|
| **Best for** | Larger businesses (typically 20+ employees) | Smaller businesses (typically under 100 employees) |
| **Setup Cost** | $50,000 - $100,000+ | $10,000 - $30,000 typically |
| **Annual Costs** | Significant (annual valuations, trustee fees, administration) | Lower ongoing costs |
| **Tax Benefits** | Significant (seller 1042 rollover, corporate deductions) | Section 1042 rollover available for C-corps |
| **Governance** | Varies; can include voting rights | Democratic (one member, one vote) |
| **Regulation** | Heavily regulated (ERISA) | Less regulated |
| **Ownership** | Indirect (through trust) | Direct membership |

### The 1042 Rollover Option

Both ESOPs and qualified worker cooperatives can use the **Section 1042 rollover** for tax-advantaged sales:

**Requirements:**
- Must be a C-corporation
- Must sell at least 30% of stock to employees/cooperative
- Seller must hold the stock for at least 3 years prior to sale
- Seller must reinvest proceeds in qualified replacement property within 12 months

**Benefit:** Capital gains tax deferral on the portion rolled over into qualified domestic securities

### Conversion Process for Worker Cooperatives

**Phase 1: Assessment and Planning (3-6 months)**
1. Assess business viability and readiness for conversion
2. Evaluate employee interest and readiness
3. Conduct business valuation
4. Explore financing options
5. Consult with attorney and accountant experienced in cooperative conversions

**Phase 2: Legal Structuring (2-4 months)**
1. Determine appropriate legal structure (LLC or corporation)
2. Draft operating agreement/bylaws with cooperative provisions
3. Establish capital structure and buy-in requirements
4. Create membership agreements

**Phase 3: Financing and Transaction (3-6 months)**
1. Secure financing (seller note, bank loans, member contributions)
2. Execute purchase agreement
3. Transfer ownership
4. File necessary documents with state

**Phase 4: Transition and Training (6-12 months)**
1. Train worker-members on cooperative governance
2. Establish decision-making processes
3. Implement open-book management
4. Build cooperative culture

### Business Size Considerations

**Worker cooperative conversion is typically more attractive for:**
- Businesses with fewer than 100 employees
- Annual revenue between $500,000 and $10 million
- Owners seeking more democratic transition
- Situations where employees have limited capital for buy-in

**ESOP may be more attractive for:**
- Larger businesses (50+ employees)
- Higher value businesses
- Owners prioritizing maximum tax benefits
- Businesses where employees prefer indirect ownership

### Florida Resources for Conversions

- **Florida Center for Employee Ownership:** Succession planning guidance and service provider referrals
- **National Center for Employee Ownership:** "Is an ESOP Right for You?" seminars in Florida
- **Democracy at Work Institute:** Cooperative conversion technical assistance

### Succession Planning Context

According to estimates, over 4 million U.S. businesses owned by retiring baby boomers will change hands or dissolve in the next decade, and more than half of small business owners lack succession plans. Worker ownership conversion provides an alternative to selling to competitors or closing the business.

## 7. Common Pitfalls in Florida

### Pitfall 1: Assuming Florida Has Worker Cooperative Laws

**The Problem:** Some founders mistakenly assume Florida has a dedicated worker cooperative statute like California or Colorado.

**The Solution:** Understand that Florida worker cooperatives must use general business entity laws (typically LLC or corporation) and build cooperative principles into their governing documents.

### Pitfall 2: Inadequate Operating Agreement

**The Problem:** Forming an LLC without a comprehensive operating agreement that addresses cooperative governance, patronage allocation, and member rights.

**The Solution:** Work with an attorney familiar with cooperative structures to draft a thorough operating agreement covering all aspects of cooperative operations. Do not rely on generic templates.

### Pitfall 3: Confusing Chapter 719 with Worker Cooperative Law

**The Problem:** Chapter 719 of Florida Statutes is titled "Cooperatives" but applies only to residential housing cooperatives, not worker cooperatives.

**The Solution:** Recognize that Chapter 719 is irrelevant for most worker cooperatives. Focus on Chapter 605 (LLCs), Chapter 607 (corporations), or if agricultural, Chapters 618-619.

### Pitfall 4: Relying on Cooperative Structure Instead of Business Fundamentals

**The Problem:** Assuming the cooperative structure alone will make the business successful.

**The Solution:** Build a strong business model first. The cooperative structure is a governance and ownership model, not a substitute for viable products/services, market demand, and sound financial management.

### Pitfall 5: Undercapitalization

**The Problem:** Worker cooperatives often start with limited capital, particularly in communities with fewer resources.

**The Solution:**
- Plan for adequate capitalization from the start
- Explore member buy-ins, retained patronage, loans, and grants
- Build cash reserves for economic downturns
- Consider phased member buy-in schedules

### Pitfall 6: Unclear Roles and Decision-Making Processes

**The Problem:** Assuming all decisions must be made collectively or by consensus, leading to slow decision-making and frustration.

**The Solution:**
- Define clear roles and responsibilities in the operating agreement
- Establish decision-making authority levels (individual, committee, board, full membership)
- Use consensus only where appropriate; majority voting for most decisions
- Train members on effective meeting facilitation

### Pitfall 7: Failing to Plan for Succession

**The Problem:** Not planning for what happens when founding members leave, retire, or pass away.

**The Solution:**
- Include clear buy-sell provisions in governing documents
- Establish capital account redemption schedules
- Plan for knowledge transfer and leadership development
- Build systems that do not depend on any single person

### Pitfall 8: Ignoring Employment Law Compliance

**The Problem:** Confusion about whether worker-members are employees and what employment laws apply.

**The Solution:**
- Consult with an employment attorney about Florida and federal employment law obligations
- Maintain proper payroll, workers' compensation, and unemployment insurance as required
- Document member/employee status clearly
- Keep employment policies even for member-owned businesses

### Pitfall 9: Missing Annual Report Deadlines

**The Problem:** Failing to file the annual report by May 1 results in a $400 late fee and potential administrative dissolution.

**The Solution:**
- Calendar annual report deadline (January 1 - May 1)
- File early to avoid delays
- Keep registered agent information current

### Pitfall 10: Neglecting Local Licensing Requirements

**The Problem:** Assuming state formation is sufficient and overlooking county and city business tax receipt requirements.

**The Solution:**
- Research requirements in your specific county and city
- Obtain Business Tax Receipts as required
- Check for industry-specific permits and licenses

## 8. Frequently Asked Questions (Florida-Specific)

### Q1: Does Florida have a worker cooperative corporation statute?

**A:** No. Unlike California, Colorado, or some other states, Florida does not have a specific statute for worker cooperative corporations. Worker cooperatives in Florida typically form as LLCs with cooperative-style operating agreements or as corporations with cooperative bylaws. Some agricultural cooperatives may use Chapter 618 or 619, but these are limited to agricultural activities.

### Q2: Should I form my worker cooperative as an LLC or corporation in Florida?

**A:** Most worker cooperatives in Florida choose the **LLC** structure because:
- The operating agreement offers flexibility to incorporate cooperative principles
- Pass-through taxation avoids double taxation
- Fewer formalities than corporations
- Internal capital accounts can be structured for patronage allocation

A corporation may be preferable if you need to use the Section 1042 rollover for a conversion (requires C-corp status) or if your cooperative model fits better with stock ownership. Consult with an attorney to determine the best structure for your situation.

### Q3: Can I use "cooperative" or "co-op" in my Florida LLC name?

**A:** This is uncertain under Florida law. Some states restrict use of "cooperative" in LLC names. It is advisable to verify with the [Division of Corporations](https://dos.fl.gov/sunbiz/) before selecting your name. Even if the name is approved, you can still operate as a worker cooperative regardless of what your legal name includes.

### Q4: How are worker-members taxed in a Florida worker cooperative?

**A:** Tax treatment depends on your entity structure:
- **LLC taxed as partnership:** Income passes through to members on Schedule K-1. Members pay self-employment tax on their share unless they receive W-2 wages.
- **LLC taxed as S-Corp:** Members who work receive W-2 wages; additional distributions are K-1 income.
- **C-Corporation:** The corporation pays corporate tax; dividends/patronage distributions are taxed again to members.

Florida has **no state individual income tax**, so worker-members do not pay state income tax on their share of profits. However, federal taxes apply. Patronage dividends may receive favorable tax treatment if properly structured. Consult a CPA experienced with cooperative taxation.

### Q5: Do I need a Business Tax Receipt (occupational license) for my cooperative?

**A:** Most likely, yes. Most Florida counties require a Business Tax Receipt for any business operating within the county. If your cooperative is located within city limits, you may also need a city BTR. Check with your county Tax Collector and city business licensing office. Some counties (like Lake County) have eliminated this requirement.

### Q6: What annual filings are required for Florida worker cooperatives?

**A:** At minimum:
- **Florida Annual Report** (Sunbiz): Due January 1 - May 1; $138.75 for LLCs, $150 for corporations
- **Federal Tax Return:** Form 1065 (partnership), 1120 or 1120-S (corporation)
- **Payroll Tax Filings:** If you have employees/worker-members receiving wages
- **Sales Tax Returns:** If collecting sales tax (typically quarterly)
- **Business Tax Receipt Renewal:** Typically annual, varies by county

Failure to file the annual report by May 1 results in a $400 late fee and potential administrative dissolution.

### Q7: Are there organizations in Florida that can help me start a worker cooperative?

**A:** Yes, several organizations provide support:
- **Florida Center for Employee Ownership (FLCEO):** Statewide education and resources - [flceo.org](https://www.flceo.org/)
- **Catalyst Miami Worker-Owned Business Program:** Academy and incubator in South Florida - [catalystmiami.org/workerowned](https://www.catalystmiami.org/workerowned)
- **Florida SBDC Network:** Free business consulting statewide - [floridasbdc.org](https://floridasbdc.org/)
- **U.S. Federation of Worker Cooperatives:** National support and Co-op Clinic - [usworker.coop](https://www.usworker.coop/)

### Q8: How is patronage (profit sharing based on work) handled in a Florida worker cooperative?

**A:** Patronage allocation must be addressed in your operating agreement or bylaws. Typically:
1. **Determine patronage basis:** Usually hours worked or wages earned during the fiscal year
2. **Calculate each member's patronage percentage:** Member's patronage / Total patronage
3. **Allocate net income:** After expenses and reserves, allocate surplus based on patronage percentages
4. **Distribute or retain:** Some portion may be paid in cash; the rest is credited to internal capital accounts (retained patronage)
5. **Provide written notice:** Members must receive written notice of their patronage allocation for tax purposes

This differs from traditional profit-sharing based on capital investment. Consult with an accountant experienced in cooperative accounting.

### Planning Phase (Months 1-3)

- [ ] **Develop your business plan:** Market analysis, financial projections, operations plan
- [ ] **Decide on legal structure:** LLC vs. corporation based on your specific needs
- [ ] **Draft governance framework:** Decision-making processes, membership policies, patronage system
- [ ] **Consult professionals:**
  - Attorney experienced with cooperatives or business formation
  - CPA/accountant familiar with cooperative taxation
- [ ] **Plan your capitalization:** Member buy-ins, loans, grants, other funding

### Formation Phase (Months 3-4)

- [ ] **Reserve or search your business name** through [Sunbiz](https://dos.fl.gov/sunbiz/search/)
- [ ] **File formation documents:**
  - [Articles of Organization (LLC)](https://dos.fl.gov/sunbiz/start-business/efile/fl-llc/) - $125
  - Articles of Incorporation (Corporation) - $70
- [ ] **Prepare and adopt operating agreement or bylaws** with cooperative provisions
- [ ] **Obtain EIN** from [IRS](https://www.irs.gov/businesses/small-businesses-self-employed/apply-for-an-employer-identification-number-ein-online)
- [ ] **Register with Florida Department of Revenue** if collecting sales tax
- [ ] **Obtain Business Tax Receipt** from county and city

### Launch Phase (Months 4-6)

- [ ] **Open business bank account**
- [ ] **Set up accounting system** with internal capital accounts
- [ ] **Establish payroll** if paying W-2 wages
- [ ] **Obtain business insurance** (general liability, workers' compensation)
- [ ] **Conduct member orientation and training** on cooperative governance
- [ ] **Hold organizational meeting** and document in minutes

### Ongoing Operations

- [ ] **File annual reports** by May 1 each year
- [ ] **Hold regular membership meetings** as specified in governing documents
- [ ] **Maintain proper records** of meetings, decisions, and financial transactions
- [ ] **Provide annual patronage notices** to members
- [ ] **Continue member education** on cooperative principles and business skills
- [ ] **Connect with the cooperative community** through USFWC, NCBA CLUSA, and local networks

### Get Help

**You do not have to do this alone.** Florida has a growing ecosystem of support for worker cooperatives:

- **Florida Center for Employee Ownership:** [www.flceo.org](https://www.flceo.org/)
- **Catalyst Miami:** [www.catalystmiami.org/workerowned](https://www.catalystmiami.org/workerowned)
- **Florida SBDC Network:** [www.floridasbdc.org](https://floridasbdc.org/)
- **U.S. Federation of Worker Cooperatives:** [www.usworker.coop](https://www.usworker.coop/)
- **Democracy at Work Institute:** [institute.coop](https://institute.coop/)

Building a worker cooperative is a journey. Take it one step at a time, and lean on the support resources available to you.
