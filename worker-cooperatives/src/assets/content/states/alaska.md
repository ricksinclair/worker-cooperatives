# How to Open a Worker Cooperative in Alaska

A comprehensive guide to forming and operating a worker-owned cooperative business in the State of Alaska.

---

## 1. Quick Summary

**What makes Alaska unique for worker cooperatives:**

Alaska offers a distinctive environment for worker cooperatives, combining a **dedicated cooperative corporation statute** (Alaska Cooperative Corporation Act, AS 10.15) with significant tax advantages including **no state personal income tax** and **no state sales tax**. The state has a strong cooperative tradition, particularly in electric utilities, food cooperatives, and producer cooperatives serving rural and remote communities.

**Key distinguishing features:**
- **Alaska Cooperative Corporation Act (AS 10.15):** Comprehensive statutory framework for cooperative corporations, including provisions for membership, capital stock, patronage, and governance
- **No state personal income tax:** Pass-through entities like cooperative LLCs provide significant tax advantages for worker-members
- **No state sales tax:** Reduced compliance burden (though some localities impose sales taxes)
- **Strong cooperative infrastructure:** Electric cooperatives power approximately 90% of Alaska residents
- **Alaska Cooperative Development Center (ACDC):** State's only organization specializing in cooperative business development, offering free technical assistance through USDA grant funding
- **Biennial reporting:** Only required every two years, reducing ongoing compliance burden

**Common entity choices for worker coops in Alaska:**
1. **Cooperative Corporation** (AS 10.15) - Purpose-built statutory framework for cooperatives
2. **Limited Liability Company (LLC)** with cooperative operating agreement - Offers flexibility and pass-through taxation
3. **Standard Corporation** (C-Corp or S-Corp) with cooperative bylaws - Less common but viable for specific needs

## 2. Best-Fit Legal Structures for Worker Coops in Alaska

### Option A: Cooperative Corporation (Alaska Cooperative Corporation Act)

**Statutory Authority:** [Alaska Statutes Title 10, Chapter 15 - Alaska Cooperative Corporation Act](https://law.justia.com/codes/alaska/title-10/chapter-15/)

Alaska's Cooperative Corporation Act provides a comprehensive framework for forming and operating cooperatives. While originally designed with agricultural, utility, and consumer cooperatives in mind, the statute can accommodate worker cooperative structures.

**Formation Requirements (AS 10.15.335):**
- Three or more natural persons at least 19 years of age may act as incorporators
- Articles of incorporation must be signed and delivered to the Department of Commerce, Community, and Economic Development
- Name must contain the word "cooperative" or acceptable abbreviation
- Name must be distinguishable from all other registered entities in Alaska

**Key Provisions:**

**Membership (AS 10.15.020):**
- Bylaws establish qualifications for membership
- Membership may be limited to specific classes
- Provisions for member rights and obligations

**Capital Stock (AS 10.15.045):**
- Cooperatives may have capital stock or operate on a membership (non-stock) basis
- Membership stock provisions in AS 10.15.050

**Governance (AS 10.15.015):**
- Board of directors adopts initial bylaws
- Members may alter, amend, or repeal bylaws
- Bylaws contain provisions for regulation and management of cooperative affairs

**Liability Protection (AS 10.15.090):**
- Member liability limited to amount of investment in the cooperative

**Advantages:**
- Purpose-built cooperative statutory framework
- Clear guidance on membership and governance
- Established legal precedents for cooperative operations
- Required use of "cooperative" in name signals cooperative identity
- Limited liability protection for members

**Considerations:**
- Corporate formalities required (annual meetings, minutes, etc.)
- Potential double taxation unless S-Corp election made (where eligible)
- Statute originally designed for agricultural/utility cooperatives; may require adaptation for worker cooperative specifics

**Filing Fee:** $250

---

### Option B: LLC with Cooperative Operating Agreement

**Statutory Authority:** [Alaska Statutes Title 10, Chapter 50 - Alaska Revised Limited Liability Company Act](https://law.justia.com/codes/alaska/title-10/chapter-50/)

An LLC can be structured to operate as a worker cooperative through a carefully drafted operating agreement. This approach is increasingly common for smaller worker cooperatives.

**Key Operating Agreement Provisions for Cooperative LLCs:**

1. **One-Member-One-Vote:** Alaska LLC law (AS 10.50.095) permits operating agreements to establish any voting structure. Explicitly establish democratic voting regardless of capital contribution.

2. **Patronage-Based Distributions:** The operating agreement can specify that profits are distributed based on labor contribution (patronage) rather than capital investment.

3. **Membership Classes:** Define worker-member class with specific rights, obligations, and pathways to membership.

4. **Buyback Provisions:** Include terms for redeeming member interests upon departure.

5. **Democratic Governance:** Establish board election procedures, member meeting requirements, and decision-making protocols.

**Advantages:**
- Pass-through taxation (avoids double taxation) - especially valuable given Alaska has no state income tax
- Flexible governance structure
- Less formal requirements than corporations
- Lower ongoing compliance burden
- Well-suited for smaller worker cooperatives

**Considerations:**
- Cannot use "cooperative" in entity name without potential confusion or additional filings
- Must carefully draft operating agreement to achieve cooperative principles
- No dedicated statutory guidance for cooperative-specific issues
- Requires knowledgeable attorney for proper structuring

**Filing Fee:** $250

---

### Option C: Standard Corporation with Cooperative Bylaws

A traditional business corporation (C-Corp or S-Corp) can adopt bylaws incorporating cooperative principles, though this approach requires more customization.

**When this might be appropriate:**
- Planning to seek outside investment in the future
- Desire for well-established corporate law precedents
- Specific industry requirements favor corporate structure
- Converting existing corporation to worker ownership

**Filing Fee:** $250

---

### Worker Coop-Friendly Provisions Worth Noting

1. **No State Personal Income Tax:** Alaska does not levy personal income taxes, making pass-through entities (LLCs, S-Corps) particularly advantageous for worker-members receiving patronage distributions.

2. **No State Sales Tax:** The state does not impose sales tax (though some localities do), reducing compliance burden.

3. **Biennial Reporting:** Alaska requires entity reports every two years rather than annually, reducing administrative burden.

4. **Section 1042 Rollover Eligibility:** Selling business owners may defer capital gains when selling 30% or more of C-Corp stock to a qualified worker cooperative.

5. **Cooperative Name Protection:** The word "cooperative" in an entity name is protected for entities organized under the Cooperative Corporation Act.

## 4. Governance and Documents You Should Prepare

### Bylaws (for Corporations) or Operating Agreement (for LLCs)

These governing documents are critical for establishing your cooperative's democratic structure. While not filed with the state, they govern internal operations.

**Essential Provisions for Worker Cooperatives:**

#### Membership Classes and Rights
- **Worker-Member Class:** Define eligibility (e.g., minimum hours worked, probationary period, employment status)
- **Membership Share:** Amount and terms of initial capital contribution (if any)
- **Candidate/Probationary Period:** Typical range of 3-12 months before full membership
- **Pathways to Membership:** Clear criteria for becoming a worker-owner

#### Voting Structure
- **One-Member-One-Vote:** Explicitly state that voting power is not proportional to capital contribution
- **Quorum Requirements:** Minimum participation for valid meetings (commonly 50% + 1 or lower for larger coops)
- **Supermajority Requirements:** Decisions requiring more than simple majority (e.g., amendments, dissolution, major asset sales - often 2/3 or 3/4)
- **Proxy Voting:** Whether permitted and under what circumstances

#### Patronage and Profit Distribution
- **Patronage Definition:** How member labor contribution is measured
  - Hours worked
  - Compensation earned
  - Equal shares for all members
  - Hybrid approaches
- **Allocation Formula:** How net earnings are divided between:
  - Patronage distributions to members
  - Collective reserve account (indivisible reserves)
  - Retained earnings for operations
- **Written Notice of Allocation:** Procedure for documenting patronage credits (important for tax purposes)
- **Distribution Timing:** When and how distributions are paid (cash, equity credits, or combination)

#### Internal Capital Accounts
- **Individual Member Accounts:** Track each member's equity stake
- **Collective Reserve Account:** Undivided surplus belonging to cooperative as a whole
- **Interest on Capital:** Whether accounts earn interest (permitted under AS 10.15)
- **Redemption Terms:** How accounts are paid out upon member departure

#### Member Departure and Buyback
- **Voluntary Withdrawal:** Notice requirements (commonly 30-90 days)
- **Involuntary Termination:** Grounds and due process for removing members
- **Death or Disability:** Treatment of membership shares and capital accounts
- **Redemption Schedule:** Timeline for paying out departing member's capital account (often 3-5 years to protect cooperative cash flow)
- **Forfeiture Provisions:** Conditions under which member may forfeit some or all capital account

#### Board of Directors
- **Composition:** Number of directors, term lengths (often staggered 2-3 year terms)
- **Eligibility:** Whether non-members can serve (unusual for worker coops)
- **Election Process:** How directors are elected by members
- **Officer Roles:** President, Secretary, Treasurer duties
- **Committees:** Standing committees and their authorities

#### Meetings
- **Annual Member Meeting:** Required timing and notice (AS 10.15 requires member meetings)
- **Special Meetings:** How called, notice requirements
- **Board Meetings:** Frequency and procedures
- **Record Keeping:** Minutes and document retention requirements

### Additional Documents to Prepare

1. **Member Handbook:** Policies, procedures, expectations, workplace guidelines
2. **Membership Agreement:** Contract signed by each member outlining rights and obligations
3. **Patronage Policy:** Detailed explanation of how patronage is calculated and distributed
4. **Conflict of Interest Policy:** Especially important for democratic organizations with member-directors
5. **Buy-Sell Agreement:** Terms for member share transfers
6. **Employment Policies:** Distinct from membership policies (for non-member employees)
7. **Decision-Making Matrix:** Which decisions require member vote vs. board vs. management

### Sample Resources

- [Democracy at Work Institute - Resource Library](https://institute.coop/resources)
- [USFWC Resources Page](https://www.usworker.coop/resources/)
- [Alaska Cooperative Development Center](https://www.alaskacoops.com/)

**Recommendation:** Consult with an attorney experienced in cooperative law to customize these documents for your specific situation. The Alaska Cooperative Development Center can provide referrals.

## 6. Worker-Ownership Conversions

### Converting an Existing Business to Worker Ownership

Business succession through worker ownership is an increasingly important option, particularly in Alaska where many baby boomer business owners are approaching retirement. This approach can preserve jobs, maintain business legacy, and keep enterprises locally owned.

### Two Primary Paths: ESOP vs. Worker Cooperative

| Factor | Worker Cooperative | ESOP |
|--------|-------------------|------|
| **Best Fit** | Smaller businesses (<100 employees) | Larger businesses (typically 20+ employees) |
| **Setup Cost** | Lower ($10,000-$50,000 typical) | Higher ($50,000-$150,000+) |
| **Ongoing Cost** | Lower (no required annual valuations) | Higher (annual appraisals, trustee fees, administration) |
| **Governance** | One-member-one-vote | Voting typically proportional to shares |
| **Tax Treatment** | Pass-through (if LLC or S-Corp) | C-Corp can be 100% tax-exempt; S-Corp has limits |
| **Section 1042 Rollover** | Available for C-Corps | Available for C-Corps |
| **Regulation** | Minimal federal regulation | Heavy ERISA/DOL regulation |
| **Democratic Control** | Built into structure | Optional, requires specific plan design |

### Alaska ESOP Landscape

According to Alaska Business Magazine, there are approximately 24 employee-owned companies with ESOPs in Alaska. Notable examples include:
- **Alaska Mill & Feed / Alaska Garden & Pet:** Transitioned to ESOP in 2016
- **Alaska Rubber Group:** Converted to ESOP in 2006

These examples demonstrate that employee ownership transitions are viable in Alaska's economy.

### Section 1042 Rollover (Both Models)

Selling owners of C-corporations can defer capital gains taxes when selling 30% or more of company stock to either an ESOP or a qualified worker cooperative. The seller must reinvest proceeds in qualified replacement property (domestic operating company securities) within a specified period.

### Typical Worker Cooperative Conversion Process

1. **Feasibility Assessment**
   - Professional business valuation
   - Financial analysis and projections
   - Worker interest and readiness assessment
   - Legal structure analysis

2. **Transition Planning**
   - Timeline development (typically 1-3 years)
   - Financing structure design
   - Leadership succession planning
   - Communication strategy

3. **Legal Structure**
   - Entity conversion or new entity formation
   - Purchase agreement negotiation
   - Bylaws/operating agreement drafting
   - Tax planning

4. **Financing the Buyout**
   - Seller financing (most common - often 50-80% of purchase price)
   - Bank loans (SBA 7(a) particularly useful)
   - Worker capital contributions
   - Combination approaches

5. **Implementation**
   - Governance training for new worker-owners
   - Operational transition
   - Financial literacy education
   - Ongoing technical assistance

### Alaska-Specific Considerations

- **No State Income Tax:** Makes pass-through cooperative structures especially attractive for worker-members
- **Rural Business Considerations:** Many Alaska businesses serve rural or remote communities; cooperative structure can help maintain local ownership and employment
- **Seasonal Businesses:** Common in Alaska (tourism, fishing, construction); cooperative structures must address seasonal employment patterns
- **ACDC Support:** Alaska Cooperative Development Center can provide conversion technical assistance

## 7. Common Pitfalls in Alaska

### 1. Underestimating Alaska's Unique Operating Environment

Alaska's geography, climate, and demographics create unique business challenges that affect cooperative operations.

**Common Issues:**
- High shipping costs for supplies and inventory
- Seasonal workforce availability
- Limited local market size in rural areas
- Communication challenges in remote locations

**Solution:** Factor these realities into business planning. Consider how cooperative structure can address challenges (e.g., shared resources, flexible seasonal arrangements, member commitment to community).

---

### 2. Choosing Wrong Legal Structure

Not all cooperative statutes fit worker cooperatives equally well. Alaska's Cooperative Corporation Act (AS 10.15) was designed primarily for agricultural, utility, and consumer cooperatives.

**Solution:**
- Carefully evaluate whether cooperative corporation or LLC structure better fits your needs
- LLC with cooperative operating agreement often provides more flexibility for worker cooperatives
- Consult with attorney familiar with both cooperative and general business law
- Contact ACDC for guidance on structure selection

---

### 3. Inadequate Operating Agreement/Bylaws

Using generic templates or failing to address worker cooperative-specific issues creates legal and operational problems.

**Key Areas Often Overlooked:**
- Patronage calculation methodology
- Member buyout terms and timeline (critical for protecting cash flow)
- Dispute resolution procedures
- Decision-making authority levels
- Seasonal worker membership provisions

**Solution:** Work with an attorney familiar with worker cooperative law. Use resources from USFWC and DAWI as starting points, but customize for Alaska and your specific situation.

---

### 4. Forgetting Biennial Report and Initial Report

While Alaska's biennial (every two years) reporting is less burdensome than annual requirements in other states, missing deadlines leads to non-compliance.

**Key Deadlines:**
- **Initial Report:** Due within 6 months of formation (no fee)
- **Biennial Report for Cooperatives:** Due July 2; late fee applies after August 1
  - Fee: $100 ($110 with late fee)
- **Biennial Report for LLCs:** Due every two years by January 2
  - Domestic: $100; Foreign: $200

**Solution:** Set calendar reminders. Consider using a registered agent service that tracks compliance deadlines.

---

### 5. Inadequate Capitalization

Worker cooperatives often start undercapitalized because workers have limited personal wealth to invest, and traditional lenders may be unfamiliar with cooperative structures.

**Solution:**
- Develop realistic capital requirements before launch
- Consider phased membership buy-in over time (e.g., payroll deduction)
- Build collective reserves from retained earnings
- Explore USDA programs and cooperative-friendly lenders early
- Contact Alaska SBDC for loan readiness assistance

---

### 6. Conflating Ownership with Daily Management

New worker cooperatives often assume all decisions must be made collectively, leading to inefficiency and decision fatigue.

**Solution:** Clearly define in bylaws/operating agreement:
- Which decisions require full member vote (usually major strategic decisions)
- Which decisions are delegated to board of directors
- Which decisions belong to managers or committees
- Create decision-making matrix for clarity

---

### 7. Neglecting Member Education and Engagement

Being a worker-owner requires different skills than being an employee. Not all workers may be equally prepared for ownership responsibilities.

**Solution:**
- Establish clear expectations during candidacy period
- Provide ongoing governance and financial literacy training
- Create meaningful participation opportunities beyond annual meetings
- Budget for member education and development
- Use resources from USFWC and DAWI

---

### 8. Ignoring Registered Agent Requirements

Failure to maintain a registered agent results in non-compliance and potential dissolution.

**Alaska-Specific Notes:**
- Registered agent must be Alaska resident individual OR corporation authorized in Alaska
- LLCs, LLPs, and LPs cannot serve as registered agents
- Must have physical Alaska address (not P.O. Box)

**Solution:** Ensure continuous registered agent coverage. Consider professional registered agent service for reliability, especially if no member has stable Alaska physical address.

## 8. FAQs for Alaska

### Q1: Does Alaska have a specific law for worker cooperatives?

**Partially.** Alaska has the Cooperative Corporation Act (AS 10.15) which provides a comprehensive framework for cooperative corporations. However, this statute was designed primarily for agricultural, utility, and consumer cooperatives rather than specifically for worker cooperatives.

Worker cooperatives in Alaska typically either:
- Form under AS 10.15 and adapt the provisions to worker ownership context
- Form as an LLC with a cooperative operating agreement
- Form as a standard corporation with cooperative bylaws

The Alaska Cooperative Development Center can help you determine which structure best fits your needs.

---

### Q2: What are the tax advantages of forming a worker cooperative in Alaska?

**Significant advantages exist:**

- **No state personal income tax:** Worker-members receiving patronage distributions face no state income tax on those distributions
- **No state sales tax:** Reduced compliance burden (though some localities impose sales taxes)
- **Pass-through taxation:** LLCs and S-Corps avoid double taxation at entity level
- **Patronage deductions:** Properly structured cooperatives can deduct patronage distributions
- **Section 1042 rollover:** Sellers to qualified cooperatives may defer capital gains (C-Corps)
- **Corporate income tax:** Alaska does impose graduated corporate income tax (0-9.4%) on C-Corps

Consult a tax professional familiar with cooperative taxation for specific guidance.

---

### Q5: Can I form a worker cooperative as an LLC in Alaska?

**Yes.** While Alaska's cooperative statute applies to corporations, an LLC can be structured with cooperative principles through its operating agreement. This includes:
- One-member-one-vote provisions
- Patronage-based distributions
- Democratic governance structures
- Member capital accounts

The LLC structure offers pass-through taxation and operational flexibility, making it popular for worker cooperatives. However, it lacks the dedicated statutory guidance of the cooperative corporation and cannot use "cooperative" in the entity name without potential confusion.

### Q7: Are there successful cooperatives in Alaska I can learn from?

**Yes.** Alaska has a strong cooperative tradition:

- **Electric Cooperatives:** Power approximately 90% of Alaska residents, serving rural villages and urban areas
- **Food Cooperatives:** Operating in Sitka, Kodiak, and Fairbanks
- **Producer Cooperatives:** Supporting farmers (peony, oyster producers in Homer and Mat-Su)
- **Artist Cooperatives:** Shared storefronts in Sitka, Valdez, Juneau, and Anchorage

While dedicated worker cooperatives are still emerging in Alaska, the Alaska Cooperative Development Center is actively developing worker cooperative initiatives, including a childcare worker cooperative pilot program.

### Q8: Can I convert my existing Alaska business to a worker cooperative?

**Yes.** Options include:
- Converting existing corporation structure and adopting cooperative provisions
- Forming new cooperative entity to purchase business assets
- Restructuring LLC operating agreement to incorporate cooperative principles

The process typically involves business valuation, financing the ownership transition, and restructuring governance. Alaska's no-income-tax environment makes seller financing particularly attractive, as sellers can structure installment sales efficiently.

Contact the Alaska Cooperative Development Center or Alaska SBDC for conversion assistance and referrals to experienced professionals.
