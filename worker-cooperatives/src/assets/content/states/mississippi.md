# How to Start a Worker Cooperative in Mississippi

## Quick Summary

Mississippi offers a distinctive environment for worker cooperative formation, shaped by a rich history of cooperative economics rooted in the Civil Rights Movement and a growing solidarity economy movement. While the state does not have a dedicated worker cooperative statute, entrepreneurs can successfully form worker-owned businesses using flexible entity structures, particularly Limited Liability Companies (LLCs).

**What Makes Mississippi Unique:**

- **Deep cooperative roots**: Mississippi has a long tradition of cooperative development, particularly among African American farming communities. Organizations like the [Mississippi Association of Cooperatives](https://mississippiassociation.org/) and [Federation of Southern Cooperatives](https://www.federation.coop/) have supported cooperative development since the 1960s and 1970s.
- **Cooperation Jackson**: The state is home to [Cooperation Jackson](https://cooperationjackson.org/), a nationally recognized network of worker cooperatives in Jackson that has been building a solidarity economy since 2014.
- **Low formation costs**: Mississippi has among the lowest LLC formation fees in the nation ($50) and no annual report filing fee for domestic LLCs.
- **Flexible LLC laws**: The [Mississippi Limited Liability Company Act](https://www.sos.ms.gov/content/documents/pol_res/llcs/1revisedLLCact.pdf) allows significant flexibility in structuring operating agreements to reflect cooperative principles.
- **Strong CDFI presence**: Access to mission-aligned capital through organizations like [Hope Enterprise Corporation](https://www.hopecu.org/) and the [Renaissance Community Loan Fund](https://www.rclfms.com/).

**Common Entity Choices:**

| Structure | Best For | Key Consideration |
|-----------|----------|-------------------|
| LLC (most common) | Most worker coops | Maximum flexibility for one-member-one-vote governance |
| Corporation | Larger coops seeking outside investment | More formal structure with board requirements |
| Agricultural Cooperative | Farmer/producer coops | Specific statutes under MS Code Chapter 79-19 |

## Best-Fit Legal Structures for Worker Cooperatives in Mississippi

Mississippi does not have a specific worker cooperative statute like some states (e.g., California, Colorado). However, worker cooperatives can be effectively formed using existing business entity structures. The key is crafting governance documents that embed cooperative principles.

### 1. Limited Liability Company (LLC) - Recommended

The LLC is the most popular and flexible structure for worker cooperatives in Mississippi.

**Advantages:**
- **Voting flexibility**: Mississippi law allows LLCs to structure voting rights however they choose. You can explicitly establish "one member, one vote" regardless of capital contributions in your [Operating Agreement](https://www.zenbusiness.com/mississippi-operating-agreement/).
- **Pass-through taxation**: Profits and losses pass through to members, avoiding double taxation.
- **Limited liability**: Member-owners are protected from business debts and liabilities.
- **Operational flexibility**: Minimal formalities compared to corporations.
- **Low cost**: $50 filing fee with no annual report fee for domestic LLCs.

**Key Statutory Reference:**
Under the [Mississippi Limited Liability Company Act (MS Code 79-29-123)](https://law.justia.com/codes/mississippi/2017/title-79/chapter-29/article-1/section-79-29-123/), the operating agreement governs "the affairs of a limited liability company, the conduct of its business and the relations of its members." This flexibility allows you to incorporate cooperative principles directly into your operating agreement.

**Cooperative Provisions to Include:**
- One member, one vote (regardless of capital contribution)
- Patronage-based profit distribution
- Democratic governance procedures
- Member admission and exit processes
- Internal capital accounts

### 2. Corporation

Corporations offer a more formal structure with established legal precedent.

**Advantages:**
- Clear governance structure with board of directors
- Well-understood by banks and investors
- Established case law

**Disadvantages:**
- More formalities (annual meetings, minutes, bylaws)
- Potential double taxation (unless S-corp election)
- Less flexibility than LLCs

**Relevant Statute:**
[Mississippi Business Corporation Act (MS Code Chapter 79-4)](https://law.justia.com/codes/mississippi/2013/title-79/chapter-4/)

### 3. Agricultural Cooperative Marketing Association

For producer cooperatives in agriculture or aquatic products, Mississippi has specific cooperative statutes.

**Relevant Statutes:**
- [MS Code Chapter 79-19: Agricultural Cooperative Marketing Associations](https://law.justia.com/codes/mississippi/title-79/chapter-19/)
- [MS Code Chapter 79-21: Aquatic Products Marketing Association](https://law.justia.com/codes/mississippi/2015/title-79/chapter-21/general-provisions/)

Under MS Code 79-19-25, each share of stock entitles the holder to one vote in the management of the association. These statutes provide a specific framework for agricultural and fishing cooperatives.

### 4. Nonprofit Corporation

Some worker cooperatives organize as nonprofits, particularly those with a strong social mission.

**Considerations:**
- No private benefit to members (limits profit distribution)
- May qualify for tax exemption
- Suitable for coops focused on community benefit
- Governed by [MS Code Chapter 79-11](https://codes.findlaw.com/ms/title-79-corporations-associations-and-partnerships/)

## Step-by-Step Formation Checklist

### Phase 1: Planning and Preparation (Weeks 1-4)

- [ ] **Identify founding worker-owners** (minimum 2-3 recommended)
- [ ] **Define your cooperative's purpose and values**
- [ ] **Develop a business plan** including:
  - Market analysis
  - Financial projections
  - Governance structure
  - Member buy-in requirements
- [ ] **Consult with cooperative development resources**:
  - [Mississippi Association of Cooperatives](https://mississippiassociation.org/)
  - [Cooperation Jackson](https://cooperationjackson.org/)
  - [Mississippi SBDC Network](https://www.mississippisbdc.org/)

### Phase 2: Legal Formation (Weeks 5-8)

#### Step 1: Choose and Reserve Your Business Name

- [ ] **Search name availability** through the [Mississippi Secretary of State Business Search](https://www.sos.ms.gov/business-services-regulation)
- [ ] **Reserve your name** (optional but recommended)
  - Filing fee: $25
  - Valid for 180 days
  - File online through the [Secretary of State portal](https://www.sos.ms.gov/business-services/fees-forms-directory)

#### Step 2: Appoint a Registered Agent

Under the [Mississippi Registered Agents Act (MS Code 79-35)](https://www.sos.ms.gov/business-services/commercial-agents), every business entity must have a registered agent.

**Requirements:**
- Must have a physical Mississippi address (not a P.O. Box)
- Must be available during normal business hours (8am-5pm Mon-Fri)
- Can be an individual (18+) or a business entity authorized in Mississippi
- Must consent to serve as agent

**Options:**
- A member of the cooperative with a Mississippi address
- A commercial registered agent service (typically $49-$150/year)
- View [Commercial Registered Agents list](https://www.sos.ms.gov/business-services/commercial-agents)

#### Step 3: File Certificate of Formation (LLC) or Articles of Incorporation

**For LLC (Recommended):**
- [ ] File Certificate of Formation online at [Mississippi Secretary of State](https://www.sos.ms.gov/business-services-regulation)
- Filing fee: $50 (plus $3 online transaction fee)
- Processing: Immediate upon online submission

**Required Information:**
- LLC name (must include "LLC" or "Limited Liability Company")
- Registered agent name and address
- Principal office address
- Management structure (member-managed recommended for small coops)
- Organizer information

**For Corporation:**
- [ ] File Articles of Incorporation
- Filing fee: $50

#### Step 4: Draft Operating Agreement (LLC) or Bylaws (Corporation)

This is the most critical document for your worker cooperative. While not filed with the state, it governs your internal operations.

**Essential Cooperative Provisions:**

```
Key Operating Agreement Sections for Worker Cooperatives:

1. MEMBERSHIP
   - Eligibility requirements
   - Admission process
   - Probationary period
   - Buy-in requirements

2. GOVERNANCE
   - One member, one vote (regardless of capital)
   - Meeting requirements
   - Quorum and voting thresholds
   - Board/management committee structure

3. CAPITAL STRUCTURE
   - Initial capital contributions
   - Internal capital accounts
   - Retained earnings allocation

4. PATRONAGE DISTRIBUTION
   - How surplus is allocated
   - Relationship to hours worked or patronage
   - Distribution timing

5. MEMBER EXIT/TERMINATION
   - Voluntary withdrawal process
   - Involuntary termination process
   - Capital account payout terms
```

### Phase 3: Tax Registration (Weeks 9-10)

#### Step 5: Obtain Federal Employer Identification Number (EIN)

- [ ] Apply online at [IRS EIN Application](https://www.irs.gov/businesses/small-businesses-self-employed/apply-for-an-employer-identification-number-ein-online)
- Free
- Immediate issuance online
- Required for all business bank accounts and tax filings

#### Step 6: Register with Mississippi Department of Revenue

- [ ] Register for state taxes via [Taxpayer Access Point (TAP)](https://www.dor.ms.gov/business/sales-use-tax/register-taxes)
- Register for:
  - **Withholding Tax** (if you have employees)
  - **Sales and Use Tax** (if selling tangible goods)
  - **Corporate Income Tax** (if organized as C-corporation)

**Contact:** [Mississippi Department of Revenue](https://www.dor.ms.gov/) - (601) 923-7700

**Withholding Tax Information:**
- [MS DOR Withholding Tax Guide](https://www.dor.ms.gov/business/withholding-tax)
- Returns due 15th of the month following the pay period
- New employers typically assigned monthly filing frequency

#### Step 7: Register with Mississippi Department of Employment Security (MDES)

- [ ] Register for unemployment insurance at [MDES Employer Registration](https://mdes.ms.gov/employers/register-with-us/)
- Required if you have employees
- File Form UI-1 (Status Report)
- Contact: (601) 493-9427 or tax@mdes.ms.gov

### Phase 4: Ongoing Compliance (Annual)

#### Step 8: File Annual Report

- [ ] File online by April 15 each year through [MS Secretary of State Annual Reports](https://www.sos.ms.gov/business-services/annual-reports)
- **Domestic LLC filing fee: $0** (informational filing only)
- Domestic Corporation filing fee: $23.25
- Failure to file within 60 days of deadline may result in administrative dissolution

#### Step 9: Maintain Good Standing

- [ ] Keep registered agent information current (change fee: $10)
- [ ] Maintain required records and meeting minutes
- [ ] File all state and federal tax returns on time
- [ ] Conduct annual member meetings

## Governance and Documents You Should Prepare

### Essential Formation Documents

| Document | Purpose | Filed with State? |
|----------|---------|-------------------|
| Certificate of Formation (LLC) / Articles of Incorporation | Creates legal entity | Yes |
| Operating Agreement (LLC) / Bylaws (Corp) | Internal governance rules | No |
| Member Agreement | Individual member rights/obligations | No |
| Patronage Policy | How surplus is distributed | No |
| Board Resolutions | Formal decisions | No |
| Meeting Minutes | Record of decisions | No |

### Operating Agreement Key Provisions

Your operating agreement should address these cooperative-specific elements:

**1. Democratic Control (One Member, One Vote)**
```
Voting Rights: Each Member shall have one (1) vote on all matters
submitted to the Members, regardless of capital contribution or
ownership percentage. Voting rights are personal and non-transferable.
```

**2. Membership Classes**
Consider whether to have:
- Worker-members (voting, full rights)
- Probationary members (limited rights during trial period)
- Non-worker investors (if applicable, limited voting)

**3. Patronage Distribution**
Define how surplus (profits) will be allocated:
- Typically based on hours worked or wages earned
- Distinguish between cash distribution and retained capital
- Address timing of distributions

**4. Capital Accounts**
Establish internal capital account system:
- Initial buy-in requirements
- How retained patronage is credited
- Terms for redemption upon exit

**5. Decision-Making Thresholds**
Specify voting requirements for different decisions:
- Day-to-day operations (simple majority)
- Major decisions (supermajority, e.g., 2/3 or 3/4)
- Fundamental changes (unanimous or near-unanimous)

### Additional Policies and Procedures

- **Personnel/HR policies**: Work schedules, leave, discipline
- **Financial policies**: Banking, signing authority, expense reimbursement
- **Conflict resolution procedures**: Mediation, grievance process
- **Member education program**: Cooperative principles, governance training

## Worker-Ownership Conversions

Converting an existing business to worker ownership can preserve jobs, maintain local businesses, and create wealth-building opportunities for employees. This is increasingly important as Baby Boomer business owners approach retirement.

### Why Consider Conversion in Mississippi?

- Preserve legacy businesses and community assets
- Retain jobs in rural communities
- Address the "Silver Tsunami" of retiring business owners
- Build employee wealth and engagement
- Potential tax benefits for selling owners

### Conversion Options

#### 1. Worker Cooperative Conversion

**Best for:** Businesses with fewer than 100 employees and $500,000 - $10 million annual revenue

**Process:**
1. **Feasibility assessment**: Evaluate business viability and employee interest
2. **Valuation**: Determine fair market value
3. **Structure design**: Create membership classes, capital structure, governance
4. **Financing**: Arrange buyer financing (often seller notes + bank loans + member buy-in)
5. **Legal documentation**: Draft new operating agreement/bylaws
6. **Training**: Educate worker-owners on cooperative governance
7. **Transition**: Gradual transfer of ownership and management

**Advantages:**
- Lower conversion costs than ESOP (no trust setup, annual appraisals)
- Democratic governance
- Suitable for smaller businesses
- Flexible structure

**Resources:**
- [Becoming Employee Owned (Project Equity)](https://institute.coop/sites/default/files/COT_BecomingEmployeeOwned_FINALweb.pdf)
- [Become Worker Owned](https://www.becomeworkerowned.org/what-is-worker-ownership)

#### 2. Employee Stock Ownership Plan (ESOP)

**Best for:** Larger businesses (100+ employees) with higher revenues

**IRS Section 1042 Tax Benefits:**
Selling owners can defer capital gains taxes if:
- Selling at least 30% of business to ESOP or worker cooperative
- Reinvesting proceeds in Qualified Replacement Property within 12 months

**Considerations:**
- Setup costs: $50,000 - $80,000+
- Ongoing costs: Annual appraisals, trustee fees, administration
- More suitable for larger, more profitable companies

**Resources:**
- [National Center for Employee Ownership](https://www.nceo.org/)
- [Employee Ownership Expansion Network](https://www.eoxnetwork.org/)

### Conversion Steps Checklist

- [ ] Initial owner interest and feasibility conversation
- [ ] Preliminary business valuation

- [ ] Detailed feasibility study
- [ ] Select legal and financial advisors

## Common Pitfalls in Mississippi

### 1. No Dedicated Worker Cooperative Statute

**Challenge:** Mississippi lacks specific legislation for worker cooperatives, unlike states like California or Colorado.

**Solution:** Use the flexible LLC structure with a carefully drafted operating agreement that embeds cooperative principles. Work with an attorney familiar with cooperative law.

### 2. Limited Local Expertise

**Challenge:** Few attorneys and accountants in Mississippi have specific worker cooperative experience.

**Solutions:**
- Connect with [Cooperation Jackson](https://cooperationjackson.org/) for local network referrals
- Consult with national cooperative attorneys (many work remotely)
- Use resources from [U.S. Federation of Worker Cooperatives](https://usworker.coop/)

### 3. Misunderstanding LLC Taxation

**Challenge:** Member-owners sometimes misunderstand self-employment tax obligations and pass-through income.

**Solutions:**
- Each member-owner is subject to self-employment tax on their share of profits
- Consult a CPA familiar with multi-member LLCs
- Consider S-corp election for potential tax savings (consult professional)

### 4. Inadequate Capitalization

**Challenge:** Worker cooperatives often start with insufficient capital, especially in lower-income communities.

**Solutions:**
- Develop realistic financial projections
- Access CDFI financing through [Hope Enterprise Corporation](https://www.hopecu.org/) or [Renaissance Community Loan Fund](https://www.rclfms.com/)
- Consider phased member buy-in
- Explore [SSBCI loan programs](https://www.connectms.org/ssbci/)

### 5. Governance Growing Pains

**Challenge:** Moving from hierarchical to democratic management creates tension and confusion.

**Solutions:**
- Invest in governance training from the start
- Develop clear decision-making policies
- Establish conflict resolution procedures
- Connect with [Cooperation Jackson](https://cooperationjackson.org/) for solidarity economy training

### 6. Registered Agent Compliance

**Challenge:** Failing to maintain a valid registered agent can result in loss of good standing.

**Solutions:**
- Choose a reliable registered agent with a permanent Mississippi address
- Update agent information promptly if changes occur ($10 filing fee)
- Consider a commercial registered agent service for reliability

### 7. Missing Annual Report Deadline

**Challenge:** While there's no filing fee for domestic LLCs, missing the April 15 deadline can lead to administrative dissolution.

**Solutions:**
- Set calendar reminders for January 1 (filing opens) and April 1 (final reminder)
- File early in the year
- Assign responsibility to a specific member or officer

### 8. Insufficient Operating Agreement

**Challenge:** Using a generic operating agreement template that doesn't address cooperative principles.

**Solutions:**
- Customize your operating agreement with explicit cooperative provisions
- Include one-member-one-vote language
- Address patronage distribution, capital accounts, and member exit procedures
- Have it reviewed by someone with cooperative experience

## Frequently Asked Questions (FAQs)

### 1. Does Mississippi have a specific worker cooperative law?

No. Mississippi does not have a dedicated worker cooperative statute. However, worker cooperatives can be effectively formed as LLCs or corporations with operating agreements/bylaws that incorporate cooperative principles. The [Mississippi Limited Liability Company Act](https://www.sos.ms.gov/content/documents/pol_res/llcs/1revisedLLCact.pdf) provides sufficient flexibility to structure voting on a one-member-one-vote basis regardless of capital contributions.

### 2. How much does it cost to form a worker cooperative LLC in Mississippi?

The basic costs are among the lowest in the nation:
- **Certificate of Formation filing fee:** $50 (plus $3 online transaction fee)
- **Name reservation (optional):** $25
- **Registered agent:** $0 if a member serves, or $49-$150/year for commercial service
- **Annual report fee:** $0 for domestic LLCs
- **EIN:** Free from IRS

Total minimum startup cost: Approximately $53-$78

Additional costs may include attorney fees for operating agreement drafting and business planning assistance.

### 3. How are worker cooperative members taxed in Mississippi?

For LLCs (most common structure):
- **Federal:** Profits and losses pass through to members proportionally and are reported on individual tax returns. Members pay self-employment tax (Social Security and Medicare) on their share of profits.
- **State:** Mississippi has a graduated income tax. Members report their share of LLC income on their state returns.
- **Patronage distributions:** Distributions based on patronage (hours worked) are generally treated as compensation, not investment returns.

Consult a tax professional familiar with multi-member LLCs for your specific situation.

### 4. What is Cooperation Jackson and how can they help?

[Cooperation Jackson](https://cooperationjackson.org/) is a network of worker cooperatives and solidarity economy institutions in Jackson, Mississippi, founded in 2014. Their mission is to advance economic democracy through cooperative development. They offer:
- Cooperative incubation and development support
- Training through the Kuwasi Balagoon Center for Economic Democracy
- Networking with existing worker cooperatives
- Community organizing and mutual aid

They are an invaluable resource for anyone starting a worker cooperative in Mississippi, particularly in the Jackson area.

### 5. Can we access small business loans as a worker cooperative?

Yes. Worker cooperatives can access various lending sources:
- **CDFIs:** [Hope Enterprise Corporation](https://www.hopecu.org/) and [Renaissance Community Loan Fund](https://www.rclfms.com/) specifically serve Mississippi small businesses, including cooperatives.
- **SBA loans:** Worker cooperatives organized as LLCs can apply for SBA-backed loans.
- **SSBCI programs:** Mississippi's [State Small Business Credit Initiative](https://www.connectms.org/ssbci/) offers loan guarantees and other support.
- **National cooperative lenders:** [Shared Capital Cooperative](https://sharedcapital.coop/) provides financing specifically for cooperatives.

### 6. What happens if a worker-owner leaves the cooperative?

This should be addressed in your operating agreement. Typical provisions include:
- **Notice period:** Usually 30-90 days
- **Capital account redemption:** The departing member receives their internal capital account balance, often paid out over time (e.g., 2-5 years) to protect cooperative cash flow
- **Non-compete considerations:** May include non-compete or non-solicitation clauses
- **Voluntary vs. involuntary departure:** Different procedures for resignation versus termination

### 7. How do we handle the "one member, one vote" principle in Mississippi?

Include explicit language in your LLC operating agreement. Mississippi law allows you to structure voting rights however you choose. Sample language:

> "Each Member in good standing shall have one (1) vote on all matters submitted to a vote of the Members, regardless of the Member's capital contribution, ownership percentage, or internal capital account balance. Voting rights are personal to each Member and may not be transferred, delegated, or exercised by proxy except as explicitly provided in this Agreement."

### 8. Are there any agricultural cooperative laws we should know about?

Yes. If your cooperative involves agricultural production or marketing, you may consider organizing under [MS Code Chapter 79-19 (Agricultural Cooperative Marketing Associations)](https://law.justia.com/codes/mississippi/title-79/chapter-19/). This provides a specific framework for farmer and producer cooperatives, including provisions for one-vote-per-member governance. The [Mississippi Association of Cooperatives](https://mississippiassociation.org/) can provide guidance on agricultural cooperative formation.
