# How to Start a Worker Cooperative in New Jersey

## Quick Summary

New Jersey offers a compelling environment for worker cooperatives, combining historic cooperative legislation with strategic access to one of the nation's most developed cooperative ecosystems just across the Hudson River. The state's [Title 34:17 - Co-Operative Societies of Workingmen](https://law.justia.com/codes/new-jersey/title-34/) represents one of the oldest cooperative statutes in the country, dating back to 1881, though most modern worker cooperatives opt for the flexibility of the LLC structure.

**What makes New Jersey unique for worker cooperatives:**

- **Historic cooperative statute**: New Jersey's R.S. 34:17-1 et seq. specifically authorizes workers' cooperative societies, one of the earliest such laws in the United States
- **Proximity to NYC cooperative ecosystem**: Direct access to the [Worker Cooperative Business Development Initiative (WCBDI)](https://nyc-business.nyc.gov/nycbusiness/article/worker-cooperatives), the largest municipal worker cooperative development program in the U.S.
- **State employee ownership support**: Governor Murphy's administration has allocated [$6 million in funding](https://www.nceo.org/employee-ownership-blog/employee-ownership-efforts-move-forward-nj-mo) for conversions to ESOPs, worker cooperatives, and employee ownership trusts
- **NJ/NY Center for Employee Ownership**: [Rutgers University hosts](https://smlr.rutgers.edu/nj-ny-center-employee-ownership) a dedicated center providing education and resources on worker cooperatives
- **Strong CDFI network**: Multiple Community Development Financial Institutions including [New Jersey Community Capital](https://www.newjerseycommunitycapital.org/) and the [Cooperative Business Assistance Corporation](https://www.njeda.gov/premiercdfi/)
- **Robust small business infrastructure**: The [NJ Small Business Development Center network](https://njsbdc.com/) operates across the state with locations at Rutgers and other institutions

New Jersey's position in the New York-New Jersey metropolitan area means cooperative founders can leverage resources, technical assistance, and professional networks from both states while benefiting from New Jersey's lower costs and business-friendly environment.

## Best-Fit Legal Structures for Worker Cooperatives in New Jersey

### Option 1: Worker Cooperative Society (R.S. 34:17-1 et seq.)

New Jersey maintains a [dedicated statutory framework](https://law.justia.com/codes/new-jersey/title-34/) specifically for workers' cooperative societies under Title 34, Chapter 17 of the New Jersey Revised Statutes.

**Key features of the Worker Cooperative Society statute:**

- **Purpose**: Individuals may associate into a society for carrying on any lawful mechanical, mining, manufacturing, service, or trading business
- **Capital structure**: Capital stock divided into shares; no share issued for less than par value
- **Registered office requirement**: Must maintain a registered office and file notice of location with the Commissioner of Labor and Workforce Development and the county clerk
- **Share certificates**: No certificate issued until shares are fully paid
- **Transfer provisions**: Shares may be transferable by sale, will, or nomination upon death, subject to board consent
- **Annual reporting**: Board must annually report on the condition of the society, including capital stock amounts and shares issued

**Recent legislative developments**: Bills have been introduced to expand the permissible purposes of workers' cooperative societies to include services, and to direct the [New Jersey Economic Development Authority](https://www.njeda.gov/) to provide financial and technical assistance to cooperative societies in the same manner as other small businesses.

**Considerations:**
- Older statutory framework with less modern flexibility
- May require additional legal guidance to navigate
- Less commonly used than LLC structure
- Potentially beneficial for cooperatives seeking state recognition of cooperative status

### Option 2: Limited Liability Company (LLC) - Recommended

The LLC structure is the most popular choice for modern worker cooperatives in New Jersey due to its flexibility in governance and taxation.

**Why LLCs work well for worker cooperatives:**

- **Governance flexibility**: [New Jersey's LLC law](https://law.justia.com/codes/new-jersey/title-42/section-42-2c-11/) gives owners broad power to write rules governing operations and management
- **Democratic structure**: Operating agreements can be customized to establish one-member-one-vote governance
- **Pass-through taxation**: Default partnership taxation allows patronage dividends to flow through to members
- **Limited liability**: Protects member-owners' personal assets
- **No minimum member requirements**: Can start with any number of worker-members

**Formation requirements:**
- File Public Records Filing for New Business Entity (Certificate of Formation) with the [NJ Division of Revenue and Enterprise Services](https://www.njportal.com/dor/businessformation/home/welcome)
- Filing fee: $125 for for-profit entities
- Annual report: $75, due on the anniversary of formation

**Operating agreement essentials for cooperative LLCs:**

According to [New Jersey statute 42:2C-11](https://law.justia.com/codes/new-jersey/title-42/section-42-2c-11/), operating agreements define the governance and decision-making structure of the LLC. For worker cooperatives, include:

- Democratic voting provisions (one member, one vote)
- Membership eligibility and admission process
- Patronage allocation formula (typically based on hours worked)
- Internal capital account system
- Buy-out and redemption procedures
- Profit distribution mechanisms

**Resources for LLC operating agreements:**
- [Democracy at Work Institute templates](https://institute.coop/bylaw-and-operating-agreement-templates)
- [Sustainable Economies Law Center templates](https://www.theselc.org/templates) (including bilingual English/Spanish versions)
- [Start.coop Bylaws Starter Kit](https://www.start.coop/bylaws)

### Option 3: Cooperative Corporation

While less common, some worker cooperatives may choose to incorporate as a standard corporation and adopt cooperative principles through bylaws. This may be appropriate for:

- Larger cooperatives seeking external investment
- Cooperatives planning to convert to an ESOP in the future
- Situations where corporate structure is preferred by lenders

**Note**: New Jersey does not have a dedicated cooperative corporation statute like some states (California, Colorado, etc.), so corporate cooperatives operate under general corporation law with cooperative provisions in bylaws.

### Choosing the Right Structure

| Factor | Worker Cooperative Society | LLC | Corporation |
|--------|---------------------------|-----|-------------|
| Formation complexity | Moderate | Low | Moderate |
| Governance flexibility | Limited | High | Moderate |
| Tax treatment | Varies | Pass-through (default) | Double taxation (default) |
| State recognition as cooperative | Yes | No (by default) | No |
| Access to state programs | Potential advantages | Standard | Standard |
| Legal precedent | Limited | Extensive | Extensive |
| Recommended for | Traditional cooperative recognition | Most worker cooperatives | Larger cooperatives, ESOP path |

## Step-by-Step Formation Checklist

### Phase 1: Planning and Preparation

**Step 1: Develop your cooperative concept**
- [ ] Define your business model and value proposition
- [ ] Identify founding worker-members
- [ ] Research your industry and market
- [ ] Draft preliminary business plan
- [ ] Determine startup capital needs

**Step 2: Choose your legal structure**
- [ ] Review options above (LLC recommended for most)
- [ ] Consult with attorney familiar with cooperative law
- [ ] Consider tax implications with accountant

**Step 3: Search for business name availability**
- [ ] Use the [NJ Business Name Search](https://www.njportal.com/dor/businessnamesearch/) to check availability
- [ ] Search free of charge online at the Division of Revenue portal
- [ ] Ensure name is distinguishable from existing businesses
- [ ] Optional: Reserve name for 120 days ($50 fee, renewable)

> **Important**: Be exact when searching. Spacing and punctuation affect results. If you proceed with a name already in use, registration will be denied.

### Phase 2: Formation Filing

**Step 4: Obtain Federal Employer Identification Number (EIN)**
- [ ] Apply online at [IRS.gov](https://www.irs.gov/businesses/small-businesses-self-employed/apply-for-an-employer-identification-number-ein-online)
- [ ] Required before filing with New Jersey
- [ ] Free to obtain
- [ ] Needed for tax filings, bank accounts, and hiring

**Step 5: File formation documents with NJ Division of Revenue**

For LLCs:
- [ ] Complete Public Records Filing for New Business Entity (Certificate of Formation)
- [ ] File online through [NJ Business Formation portal](https://www.njportal.com/dor/businessformation/home/welcome)
- [ ] Pay $125 filing fee
- [ ] Receive NJ Business Entity ID

For Worker Cooperative Societies (R.S. 34:17):
- [ ] Prepare articles of association per statutory requirements
- [ ] File with Commissioner of Labor and Workforce Development
- [ ] File notice with county clerk where office is located
- [ ] Establish registered office in New Jersey

**Step 6: Appoint a registered agent**

[New Jersey requires](https://www.nj.gov/njfilm/assets/pdf/registering-to-do-business-in-NJ.pdf) all LLCs to maintain a registered agent who:
- [ ] Is at least 18 years old and a New Jersey resident, OR
- [ ] Is a business entity authorized to transact business in New Jersey
- [ ] Maintains a physical street address in New Jersey (P.O. Box alone insufficient)
- [ ] Is available during regular business hours

> **Note**: A member of the cooperative can serve as registered agent, or you can hire a professional registered agent service.

### Phase 3: State Tax Registration

**Step 7: Register for New Jersey taxes (NJ-REG)**

All businesses must register with the [NJ Division of Taxation](https://www.nj.gov/treasury/taxation/br1.shtml):

- [ ] Complete Form NJ-REG (Business Registration Application) online at [NJ Online Tax/Employer Registration](https://www.njportal.com/DOR/BusinessRegistration)
- [ ] No fee for NJ-REG filing
- [ ] Must register at least 15 days before engaging in business activity

**Information required for NJ-REG:**
- [ ] New Jersey Business Entity ID
- [ ] Federal EIN
- [ ] NAICS code for your business activity
- [ ] Sales tax collection information (if applicable)
- [ ] Anticipated first payroll withholding date
- [ ] Mailing address and business location(s)

**What you receive after registration:**
- New Jersey Tax ID number
- Certificate of Authorization to Collect Sales Tax (if applicable)
- [Business Registration Certificate (BRC)](https://www.nj.gov/treasury/revenue/busregcert.shtml) - required for public contracting and state grants/tax credits

**Contact for registration questions**: Division of Revenue Business Registration Services Office at 609-292-9292 (Option #1), Monday-Friday 8:30 AM - 4:30 PM

### Phase 4: Local Licensing and Permits

**Step 8: Obtain local business licenses and permits**

[New Jersey does not have a general state-level business license](https://business.nj.gov/licensing-and-certification-guide), but local requirements vary significantly:

- [ ] Contact your municipality for local licensing requirements
- [ ] Check zoning compliance for your business location
- [ ] Obtain Certificate of Occupancy if required
- [ ] Apply for health permits if handling food, healthcare, or cosmetic services
- [ ] Obtain building permits for any construction work
- [ ] Check signage permit requirements

**Finding your local requirements:**
- Visit [Business.NJ.gov Licensing Guide](https://business.nj.gov/licensing-and-certification-guide)
- Contact your municipal clerk's office
- Check county business development office

**Common permits by industry:**
- Food service: Health department permit, food handler certifications
- Retail: Sales tax certificate, mercantile license
- Professional services: State occupational licenses (varies by profession)
- Home-based businesses: Home occupation permit

### Phase 5: Operational Setup

**Step 9: Establish banking and financial systems**
- [ ] Open business bank account using EIN and formation documents
- [ ] Set up accounting system with internal capital accounts
- [ ] Establish payroll system for worker-members
- [ ] Consider cooperative-friendly financial institutions

**Step 10: Create governance documents**
- [ ] Finalize operating agreement or bylaws (see next section)
- [ ] Draft membership agreement
- [ ] Create member handbook
- [ ] Establish meeting procedures

**Step 11: File annual report**
- [ ] Note anniversary date of formation
- [ ] File annual report online with Division of Revenue
- [ ] Pay $75 annual report fee
- [ ] Due on exact anniversary of initial filing

## Governance and Documents You Should Prepare

### Essential Governing Documents

**1. Operating Agreement (for LLCs) or Bylaws (for corporations/cooperative societies)**

Your operating agreement or bylaws form the foundation of your cooperative's governance. Key provisions should address:

**Membership:**
- Eligibility requirements (employment status, probationary period)
- Admission process and member approval
- Membership buy-in amount and payment terms
- Rights and responsibilities of members
- Grounds and process for termination

**Democratic Governance:**
- One member, one vote principle
- Quorum requirements for meetings
- Types of decisions requiring member vote
- Supermajority requirements for major decisions
- Board of directors structure and election procedures
- Officer roles and responsibilities

**Financial Structure:**
- [Internal capital account system](https://institute.coop/sites/default/files/SampleWorkerCooperativeBylaws.pdf) (required for cooperative taxation)
- Patronage allocation formula
- Retained vs. distributed patronage
- Redemption of member capital upon departure
- Reserve fund requirements

**Operational Provisions:**
- Fiscal year designation
- Meeting schedules (annual, regular, special)
- Amendment procedures
- Dissolution provisions

**2. Membership Agreement**

A separate document from the member's perspective that includes:
- Rights and benefits of membership
- Financial obligations (buy-in, capital contributions)
- Work expectations
- [Consent to patronage distributions](https://institute.coop/sites/default/files/SampleWorkerCooperativeBylaws.pdf) (tax compliance)
- Confidentiality provisions
- Dispute resolution procedures

**3. Member Handbook/Policies**

Operational policies that may be easier to update than bylaws:
- Personnel policies
- Compensation structure
- Benefits administration
- Work schedules and time-off policies
- Performance evaluation procedures
- Grievance procedures

**4. Board and Committee Charters**

If your cooperative has a board of directors:
- Board responsibilities and authority
- Committee structures (finance, membership, etc.)
- Meeting procedures
- Conflict of interest policies

### Template Resources

- [Democracy at Work Institute - Sample Worker Cooperative Bylaws](https://institute.coop/resources/sample-worker-cooperative-bylaws)
- [ICA Model Bylaws for Worker Cooperatives](https://institute.coop/sites/default/files/ica-model-bylaws.pdf)
- [Sustainable Economies Law Center Legal Templates](https://www.theselc.org/templates)
- [Start.coop Bylaws Starter Kit](https://www.start.coop/bylaws)

### Working with Legal Professionals

While templates provide a starting point, working with an attorney familiar with cooperative law is strongly recommended. The governing documents should:
- Comply with New Jersey LLC or corporate law
- Properly establish cooperative tax treatment
- Reflect your cooperative's specific values and operations
- Address foreseeable scenarios and disputes

Consider attorneys experienced with cooperatives through:
- [CUNY Law Community Economic Development Clinic](https://www.law.cuny.edu/) (NYC, may assist NJ cooperatives)
- [TakeRoot Justice](https://takerootjustice.org/) (NYC-based, works with grassroots organizations)
- NJ/NY Center for Employee Ownership referrals

## Worker-Ownership Conversions

Converting an existing business to worker ownership is increasingly recognized as a solution to business succession challenges and wealth-building for workers.

### Why Consider Conversion in New Jersey?

- Over 150,000 to 300,000 businesses nationally become candidates for employee takeovers annually as baby boomer owners retire
- [New Jersey has nearly 90 ESOPs](https://www.njeda.gov/esop-assistance-program/) with $65 billion in plan assets
- State support through NJEDA's ESOP Assistance Program
- Lower costs and complexity compared to ESOPs for smaller businesses

### Conversion Pathways

**1. Direct Sale to Worker Cooperative**

The business owner sells the company directly to a newly formed worker cooperative:

**Advantages:**
- Can work for businesses of any size
- Lower ongoing administrative costs than ESOPs
- Direct democratic ownership
- Annual maintenance costs typically [$5,000 or less](https://becomingemployeeowned.org/st/nj/)

**Process:**
1. Business valuation
2. Feasibility assessment
3. Worker interest and capacity assessment
4. Cooperative formation
5. Financing arrangement
6. Transaction closing
7. Transition period with seller

**2. ESOP Conversion**

Employee Stock Ownership Plans work well for larger businesses (typically 20+ employees):

**NJEDA Support:**
- [ESOP Assistance Program](https://www.njeda.gov/esop-assistance-program/) covers 90% of feasibility costs up to $35,000
- Eligibility: 20+ full-time employees
- Stable, sustainable business structure
- Owner seeking succession planning

**3. Employee Ownership Trust (EOT)**

A newer model gaining traction:
- Trust holds shares on behalf of employees
- Employees benefit without individual share ownership
- May be simpler than ESOP for some situations

### Conversion Resources

**[NJ/NY Center for Employee Ownership](https://smlr.rutgers.edu/nj-ny-center-employee-ownership)**
- Free online educational program
- Understanding capital sources for conversions
- Distinguishing debt vs. equity capital

**[Democracy at Work Institute - Workers to Owners](https://institute.coop/our-work/workers-owners-conversions)**
- Technical assistance for conversions

**[Becoming Employee Owned](https://becomingemployeeowned.org/st/nj/)**
- New Jersey-specific information
- Initiative of Democracy at Work Institute

**[Project Equity](https://project-equity.org/)**
- National organization focused on conversions
- Assessment tools and resources

### Financing Conversions

Worker cooperative conversions typically use a combination of:

- **Seller financing**: Owner carries a note for portion of purchase price
- **Bank debt**: Traditional business loans, SBA loans
- **CDFI loans**: [New Jersey Community Capital](https://www.newjerseycommunitycapital.org/), CBAC
- **Cooperative loan funds**: [The Working World](https://www.theworkingworld.org/) (NYC-based)
- **Member equity**: Worker contributions to capital
- **Retained earnings**: Using business cash flow

### Conversion Considerations

**For Business Owners:**
- Preserve company culture and legacy
- Tax advantages (consult tax professional)
- Flexible transition timeline
- Workers motivated to continue success

**For Workers:**
- Assess financial capacity for buy-in
- Evaluate governance readiness
- Plan for transition period
- Build business management skills

## Common Pitfalls in New Jersey

### Legal and Formation Pitfalls

**1. Choosing the Wrong Legal Structure**

While New Jersey's historic R.S. 34:17 worker cooperative statute exists, it may not be the best choice for modern cooperatives. Many founders default to what seems obvious without considering:
- Tax implications
- Governance flexibility needs
- Access to legal expertise and resources

**Solution**: Consult with an attorney familiar with cooperative law before deciding. The LLC structure offers maximum flexibility for most worker cooperatives.

**2. Inadequate Operating Agreement/Bylaws**

[Research shows](https://wanderwellconsulting.com/common-cooperative-mistakes/) that cooperatives with poorly drafted governing documents face predictable problems:
- Unclear decision-making authority
- No process for handling departing members
- Inadequate patronage allocation provisions
- Missing internal capital account systems

**Solution**: Use [established templates](https://institute.coop/bylaw-and-operating-agreement-templates) and work with a cooperative-experienced attorney. Address foreseeable scenarios in writing before they become disputes.

**3. Failing to Properly Register for Taxes**

New Jersey requires registration [at least 15 days before](https://www.nj.gov/treasury/taxation/informationforvendors.shtml) engaging in business activity. Failure to register results in penalties.

**Solution**: Complete NJ-REG registration promptly after formation. Don't wait until you have revenue.

**4. Missing Local Licensing Requirements**

With no general state business license, founders may assume no licenses are needed. However, [municipalities have varying requirements](https://business.nj.gov/licensing-and-certification-guide) that can result in cease-and-desist orders.

**Solution**: Contact your municipality early in the planning process. Check zoning before signing a lease.

### Governance and Management Pitfalls

**5. Conflating Ownership with Day-to-Day Management**

A [common cooperative mistake](https://wanderwellconsulting.com/common-cooperative-mistakes/): assuming democratic ownership means every decision requires a vote or consensus.

**Solution**: Clearly define roles and responsibilities. Separate strategic decisions (member votes) from operational decisions (management authority). Create decision-making levels that follow organizational roles.

**6. Inadequate Management Skills**

Worker cooperatives need technically competent management just like any business. Democratic ownership doesn't eliminate the need for business acumen.

**Solution**: Invest in training. Use [NJSBDC free counseling](https://njsbdc.com/) services. Consider hiring experienced managers when needed.

**7. One Person "Going Rogue"**

Cooperatives can move slower than traditional businesses, leading some members to make unilateral decisions to "get things done," often at significant cost.

**Solution**: Establish clear authority boundaries. Create expedited decision-making processes for time-sensitive matters. Address frustrations openly in meetings.

### Financial Pitfalls

**8. Undercapitalization**

Worker cooperatives often struggle to secure adequate startup capital because:
- Workers have limited personal wealth to invest
- Traditional lenders don't understand cooperatives
- No outside equity investors (by design)

**Solution**:
- Be realistic about capital needs in business planning
- Explore [CDFI lending](https://www.njeda.gov/premiercdfi/) through NJEDA partners
- Consider seller financing for conversions
- Build relationships with cooperative-friendly lenders like [The Working World](https://www.theworkingworld.org/)

**9. Failing to Maintain Internal Capital Accounts**

For cooperative tax treatment, you must track each member's capital account. Failure to do so creates tax problems and member disputes.

**Solution**: Set up proper accounting systems from day one. Work with an accountant familiar with cooperative structures.

**10. Uneven Startup Contributions**

When founders contribute unequally, resentment can build if not properly addressed in governing documents.

**Solution**: Document all contributions. Create clear policies for how different contribution levels are treated. Consider "sweat equity" provisions.

### Cultural and Operational Pitfalls

**11. Treating Cooperative Structure as Your Product**

Your [business must succeed on its merits](https://wanderwellconsulting.com/common-cooperative-mistakes/), not just because it's a cooperative. Customers buy your product or service, not your governance structure.

**Solution**: Develop a strong value proposition independent of your cooperative identity. Meet market needs first.

**12. Not Planning for Founder Departure**

Cooperatives must outlast their founders. Without succession planning, the cooperative may not survive leadership transitions.

**Solution**: Build systems and documentation that don't depend on specific individuals. Develop leadership pipeline among members.

**13. Isolation from Support Networks**

New Jersey cooperatives may feel isolated without realizing the resources available across the Hudson River.

**Solution**: Connect with [NYC's cooperative ecosystem](https://nyc-business.nyc.gov/nycbusiness/article/worker-cooperatives). Join [US Federation of Worker Cooperatives](https://www.usworker.coop/). Attend regional cooperative events.

## Frequently Asked Questions (FAQs) for New Jersey

### 1. Does New Jersey have a specific worker cooperative statute?

Yes. New Jersey's [R.S. 34:17-1 et seq.](https://law.justia.com/codes/new-jersey/title-34/) (Title 34, Chapter 17 - Co-Operative Societies of Workingmen) dates back to 1881 and specifically authorizes workers' cooperative societies. However, most modern worker cooperatives in New Jersey form as LLCs because of greater flexibility in governance and well-established legal precedents. The LLC structure allows you to achieve cooperative governance through your operating agreement while benefiting from a more widely understood legal form.

### 2. How much does it cost to form a worker cooperative in New Jersey?

Basic formation costs include:
- **LLC filing fee**: $125 with [NJ Division of Revenue](https://www.njportal.com/dor/businessformation/home/welcome)
- **Name reservation** (optional): $50 for 120 days
- **NJ-REG tax registration**: Free
- **EIN**: Free from IRS
- **Annual report**: $75 per year

Additional costs to budget:
- **Legal fees**: Varies, but expect $2,000-$10,000+ for proper cooperative operating agreement
- **Accounting setup**: Varies
- **Local permits**: Varies by municipality
- Ongoing annual maintenance: Typically under $5,000 for worker cooperatives

### 3. Can a worker cooperative in New Jersey access the same support as other small businesses?

Yes. Worker cooperatives structured as LLCs can access all standard small business support programs, including:
- [NJSBDC free counseling](https://njsbdc.com/)
- [NJEDA financing programs](https://www.njeda.gov/financing-and-incentives/)
- SBA loans and programs
- Local economic development resources

Additionally, recent legislative efforts aim to ensure the [NJEDA provides](https://pub.njleg.gov/bills/2020/S2000/1905_I1.HTM) financial and technical assistance to cooperative societies in the same manner as other small businesses.

### 4. What resources are available for converting my existing New Jersey business to worker ownership?

New Jersey offers several conversion-specific resources:

- **[NJEDA ESOP Assistance Program](https://www.njeda.gov/esop-assistance-program/)**: Covers 90% of feasibility costs up to $35,000 for businesses with 20+ employees
- **[NJ/NY Center for Employee Ownership](https://smlr.rutgers.edu/nj-ny-center-employee-ownership)**: Free educational programs on conversion at Rutgers
- **[Becoming Employee Owned - NJ](https://becomingemployeeowned.org/st/nj/)**: State-specific conversion information
- **NYC-based resources**: [ICA Group](https://www.icagroup.org/) and [Democracy at Work Institute](https://institute.coop/) provide technical assistance accessible to NJ businesses

### 5. Do I need a registered agent for my worker cooperative LLC?

Yes. [New Jersey law requires](https://www.nj.gov/njfilm/assets/pdf/registering-to-do-business-in-NJ.pdf) all LLCs to maintain a registered agent who is either:
- An individual at least 18 years old who is a New Jersey resident, OR
- A business entity authorized to transact business in New Jersey

The registered agent must have a physical street address in New Jersey (not just a P.O. Box) and be available during regular business hours to accept legal documents. A worker-member can serve as registered agent, or you can hire a professional service.

### 6. How do worker cooperatives handle taxes in New Jersey?

Worker cooperatives structured as LLCs are typically taxed as partnerships (pass-through taxation):

- The cooperative files informational returns
- Income "passes through" to members
- Members report income on personal tax returns
- [Patronage dividends](https://institute.coop/sites/default/files/SampleWorkerCooperativeBylaws.pdf) based on hours worked are deductible by the cooperative

**Requirements for cooperative tax treatment:**
- Proper internal capital account system
- Written notice of allocation to members
- Member consent to patronage distributions in membership agreement

New Jersey has both a Corporation Business Tax and a Gross Income Tax. Consult a tax professional familiar with cooperatives to ensure proper structure and compliance.

### 7. Can I access NYC's Worker Cooperative Business Development Initiative if I'm in New Jersey?

The [Worker Cooperative Business Development Initiative](https://nyc-business.nyc.gov/nycbusiness/article/worker-cooperatives) is a New York City program, so direct funding is limited to NYC-based cooperatives. However, New Jersey cooperatives can benefit from:

- Educational resources and training materials
- Connections to [ICA Group](https://www.icagroup.org/) and [Democracy at Work Institute](https://institute.coop/) who work regionally
- [CUNY Law](https://www.law.cuny.edu/) clinics that may assist regional cooperatives
- Template documents and best practices developed through the initiative
- Networking opportunities at regional events

The [NJ/NY Center for Employee Ownership](https://smlr.rutgers.edu/nj-ny-center-employee-ownership) at Rutgers serves both states and bridges these resources.

### 8. What local permits do I need for my worker cooperative in New Jersey?

[New Jersey has no general state business license](https://business.nj.gov/licensing-and-certification-guide), but local requirements vary significantly by municipality. Common requirements include:

- **Zoning compliance**: Verify your location is zoned for your business type
- **Certificate of Occupancy**: Required in many municipalities
- **Health permits**: Required for food service, healthcare, cosmetic services
- **Professional licenses**: Required for regulated professions
- **Signage permits**: Often regulated locally
- **Building permits**: Required for any construction

To find your specific requirements:
1. Visit [Business.NJ.gov Licensing Guide](https://business.nj.gov/licensing-and-certification-guide)
2. Contact your municipal clerk's office
3. Check with your local health department if applicable
