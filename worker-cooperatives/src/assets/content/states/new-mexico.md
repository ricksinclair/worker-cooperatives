# How to Start a Worker Cooperative in New Mexico

A comprehensive guide to forming and operating a worker-owned cooperative business in the Land of Enchantment.

---

## Quick Summary: What Makes New Mexico Unique

New Mexico offers a distinctive environment for worker cooperatives, shaped by centuries of community-oriented business practices and a strong cooperative tradition rooted in the state's cultural heritage.

### The Acequia Tradition

New Mexico is home to nearly 700 functioning [acequias](https://www.taosacequias.org/acequias)â€”community-operated irrigation systems that have been managed cooperatively for over three centuries. These gravity-fed water systems represent the oldest form of European resource management still operating in the United States. Each acequia is governed democratically with a one-property, one-vote structure, managed by an elected mayordomo (ditch boss) and commissioners. This tradition embodies principles directly relevant to worker cooperatives:

- **Democratic governance**: Community members elect leadership and make collective decisions
- **Shared responsibility**: Water allocation rights are tied to maintenance duties
- **Equity and fairness**: Resources are distributed based on need rather than wealth
- **Intergenerational stewardship**: Systems are maintained for long-term community benefit

### Community-Oriented Business Culture

New Mexico's cultural landscape reflects strong traditions of mutual aid and collective enterprise across its Native American, Hispanic, and Anglo communities. The state has a rich history of community land grants (mercedes), mutual aid societies (mutualistas), and tribal economic cooperation. This cultural foundation creates a receptive environment for worker-owned businesses.

### Key Advantages for Worker Cooperatives in New Mexico

- **Established cooperative statute**: [Chapter 53, Article 4 NMSA 1978](https://law.justia.com/codes/new-mexico/chapter-53/article-4/) provides a legal framework for cooperative associations
- **No annual report requirement**: LLCs in New Mexico do not file annual reports, reducing administrative burden
- **Low filing fees**: Formation costs are among the lowest in the nation ($50-$52)
- **Strong CDFI presence**: Multiple community development lenders serve New Mexico
- **Tax simplicity**: Gross Receipts Tax replaces traditional sales tax, with straightforward compliance

## Best-Fit Legal Structures for Worker Cooperatives

New Mexico offers several paths to forming a worker cooperative, each with distinct advantages. The choice depends on your cooperative's size, goals, and governance preferences.

### Option 1: Cooperative Association (Chapter 53, Article 4)

New Mexico's [Cooperative Association Act](https://law.justia.com/codes/new-mexico/chapter-53/article-4/) provides a dedicated legal structure for cooperatives.

**Key Features:**

- Requires five or more natural persons or two or more associations to incorporate
- Board of directors must have at least five members
- Explicitly deemed nonprofit in character (though this refers to organizational purpose, not tax status)
- One member, one vote governance is standard
- Filing fee: $50 with the [New Mexico Secretary of State](https://www.sos.nm.gov/business-services/)

**Required Elements in Articles of Incorporation** (per [Section 53-4-5 NMSA](https://law.justia.com/codes/new-mexico/chapter-53/article-4/section-53-4-5/)):

- Name of the association (must include "cooperative" or "co-op")
- Duration (may be perpetual)
- Purpose(s) of the association
- Location of principal office
- Number and qualifications of directors
- Terms of membership and voting rights
- Registered agent information

**Advantages:**
- Purpose-built for cooperative enterprises
- Clear statutory guidance on governance
- Recognized cooperative identity

**Considerations:**
- Requires minimum of five directors (may be challenging for small startups)
- Less flexibility than LLC structure
- Fewer template documents available compared to LLCs

### Option 2: Limited Liability Company (LLC) with Cooperative Operating Agreement

Many worker cooperatives choose to form as an LLC and structure their [Operating Agreement](https://www.northwestregisteredagent.com/llc/new-mexico/operating-agreement) to reflect cooperative principles.

**Key Features:**

- File Articles of Organization with the [NM Secretary of State](https://enterprise.sos.nm.gov/forms/business)
- Filing fee: $52
- No minimum number of members
- Maximum flexibility in structuring governance
- Operating Agreement can establish one-member-one-vote, patronage allocations, and democratic control

**Required Information for Articles of Organization:**

- Company name (must include "LLC" or "Limited Liability Company")
- Effective date (optional delay up to 60 days)
- Duration (perpetual unless specified)
- Registered agent name and physical New Mexico address
- Principal business address
- Email address

**Advantages:**
- Flexibility to start small and grow
- Operating Agreement can be customized extensively
- Well-understood structure by banks, landlords, and vendors
- Abundant template resources available

**Considerations:**
- Must clearly document cooperative principles in Operating Agreement
- "Cooperative" not required in name (though permitted)
- May need to explain structure to those unfamiliar with cooperative LLCs

### Option 3: Nonprofit Corporation with Cooperative Bylaws

For cooperatives prioritizing mission over profit distribution, organizing under [Chapter 53, Article 8 NMSA](https://law.justia.com/codes/new-mexico/chapter-53/article-8/) (Nonprofit Corporations) may be appropriate.

**Key Features:**

- Can apply for 501(c)(3) or 501(c)(5) federal tax exemption
- Limited ability to distribute surplus to members
- May be suitable for cooperatives with significant grant funding

**Considerations:**
- Significant restrictions on profit distribution
- More complex compliance requirements
- May not align with traditional worker cooperative model

### Comparison Table

| Feature | Cooperative Association | LLC | Nonprofit |
|---------|------------------------|-----|-----------|
| Minimum members/directors | 5 directors | 1 member | 3 directors |
| Filing fee | $50 | $52 | $50 |
| Governance flexibility | Moderate | High | Moderate |
| Profit distribution | Yes | Yes | Limited |
| "Cooperative" in name | Required | Optional | Optional |
| Annual report | Yes ($30) | No | Yes |
| Template availability | Limited | Abundant | Moderate |

### Recommendation for Most Worker Cooperatives

For most worker cooperatives in New Mexico, the **LLC structure with a cooperative Operating Agreement** offers the best combination of flexibility, simplicity, and legal protection. It allows small groups to start cooperatively, avoids the five-director minimum, and provides well-established legal precedents. The Operating Agreement should explicitly establish:

- Democratic governance (one member, one vote)
- Worker-member eligibility and admission process
- Patronage-based surplus allocation
- Member capital accounts
- Termination and exit procedures

For larger, established cooperatives or those seeking to emphasize their cooperative identity, the **Cooperative Association** structure provides purpose-built legal recognition.

## Step-by-Step Formation Checklist

### Phase 1: Planning and Preparation

**Step 1: Assemble Your Founding Team**
- Identify initial worker-owners committed to cooperative principles
- Establish shared vision and values
- Discuss capital contributions and time commitments
- Consider consulting with the [Cooperative Catalyst of New Mexico](https://cooperationworks.coop/member/cooperative-catalyst-of-new-mexico/)

**Step 2: Choose Your Business Concept**
- Define products or services
- Research market viability
- Develop preliminary financial projections
- Identify startup capital needs

**Step 3: Select Your Legal Structure**
- Review options above (LLC recommended for most startups)
- Consult with an attorney familiar with cooperative law
- Review resources at [Co-opLaw.org - New Mexico](https://www.co-oplaw.org/knowledge-base/new-mexico/)

### Phase 2: Name and Entity Formation

**Step 4: Conduct Name Search and Reservation**

Search for name availability through the [NM Secretary of State Business Search](https://enterprise.sos.nm.gov/forms/business):

- Name must be distinguishable from existing entities
- For LLCs: must include "LLC" or "Limited Liability Company"
- For Cooperative Associations: must include "cooperative" or "co-op"

Optional: Reserve name for 120 days by filing Application for Name Reservation ($20 fee).

**Step 5: Designate a Registered Agent**

Every New Mexico business entity must maintain a registered agent with a physical New Mexico address (no P.O. boxes). The agent must be available during normal business hours to accept legal documents.

Options:
- A member of the cooperative with a New Mexico address
- A commercial registered agent service
- An attorney

**Step 6: File Formation Documents**

**For LLC:**
File Articles of Organization online at the [NM Secretary of State portal](https://enterprise.sos.nm.gov/forms/business):
- Fee: $52
- Processing: Typically 3-5 business days
- All filings must be submitted electronically

**For Cooperative Association:**
File Articles of Incorporation with the Secretary of State:
- Fee: $50
- Must meet requirements of Section 53-4-5 NMSA
- Contact Business Services at 505-827-3600 for specific forms

### Phase 3: Tax Registration

**Step 7: Obtain Federal Employer Identification Number (EIN)**

Apply through the [IRS EIN Online Application](https://www.irs.gov/businesses/small-businesses-self-employed/apply-for-an-employer-identification-number-ein-online):
- Free to obtain
- Immediate issuance for online applications
- Required for all business entities with employees or multiple members

**Step 8: Register with NM Taxation and Revenue Department**

Register for a [New Mexico Business Tax Identification Number (NMBTIN)](https://www.tax.newmexico.gov/businesses/who-must-register-a-business/) through the [Taxpayer Access Point (TAP)](https://tap.state.nm.us/):

This registration covers:
- **Gross Receipts Tax (GRT)**: New Mexico's equivalent of sales tax, ranging from 5.125% to 8.6875% depending on location
- **Wage Withholding Tax**: If you have employees (including worker-owners receiving wages)
- **Compensating Tax**: On goods purchased for use in New Mexico

Registration steps:
1. Visit [TAP portal](https://tap.state.nm.us/)
2. Click "Apply for a New Mexico Business Tax ID"
3. Complete application with business information
4. Receive NMBTIN upon approval

Review [FYI-102: Information for New Businesses](https://www.tax.newmexico.gov/forms-publications/) for complete guidance.

**Step 9: Understand Gross Receipts Tax Obligations**

Unlike traditional sales tax, [New Mexico Gross Receipts Tax](https://www.tax.newmexico.gov/businesses/gross-receipts-overview/) applies to the seller, not the buyer:
- Tax is imposed on gross receipts from selling property or services
- Rates vary by location (state + county + municipal rates)
- Many services are taxable that would be exempt in other states
- Filed monthly, quarterly, or semi-annually based on tax liability

### Phase 4: Local Licensing and Permits

**Step 10: Obtain Local Business License**

New Mexico does not have a statewide business license, but most cities and counties require local registration.

**Albuquerque:**
- Obtain Business License from [City of Albuquerque Planning Department](https://www.cabq.gov/planning/business-registration-information)
- Additional permits may be required based on business type

**Santa Fe:**
- Register with [City of Santa Fe](https://www.santafecountynm.gov/growth-management/building-development/commercial-business-license) for businesses in city limits
- Unincorporated areas: Santa Fe County Growth Management Department

**Other locations:**
- Contact your city clerk or county planning office
- Use the [NM Business Portal Licenses and Permits guide](https://biz.nm.gov/business-navigator/licenses-and-permits/)

**Step 11: Obtain Industry-Specific Licenses**

Check with the [New Mexico Regulation and Licensing Department](https://www.rld.nm.gov/) for professional licenses including:
- Contractors
- Healthcare professionals
- Food service operations
- Alcohol sales
- Professional services

### Phase 5: Internal Organization

**Step 12: Draft and Adopt Bylaws or Operating Agreement**

**For LLCs**, create an Operating Agreement that includes:
- Purpose and cooperative principles
- Membership eligibility and admission process
- Capital contributions and member accounts
- Governance structure (one member, one vote)
- Meeting requirements and decision-making procedures
- Patronage allocation formula
- Surplus distribution policies
- Member termination and buyout procedures
- Dissolution provisions

**For Cooperative Associations**, adopt Bylaws per [Section 53-4-8 and 53-4-9 NMSA](https://law.justia.com/codes/new-mexico/chapter-53/article-4/):
- Time, place, and manner of calling meetings
- Number of members constituting a quorum
- Rights and conditions of membership
- Mode, manner, and effect of transfers
- Terms of office for directors
- Duties and compensation of directors and officers
- Rules for amendment

**Step 13: Hold Organizational Meeting**

- Adopt Bylaws or Operating Agreement
- Elect initial directors or managers
- Appoint officers
- Authorize opening bank accounts
- Ratify any pre-formation actions
- Document all decisions in meeting minutes

**Step 14: Open Business Bank Account**

Bring to the bank:
- EIN confirmation letter
- Articles of Organization/Incorporation
- Operating Agreement or Bylaws
- Resolution authorizing account opening
- Identification for authorized signers

## Governance and Documents You Should Prepare

### Essential Formation Documents

1. **Articles of Organization (LLC) or Articles of Incorporation (Cooperative Association)**
   - Filed with Secretary of State
   - Public document establishing legal existence

2. **Operating Agreement (LLC) or Bylaws (Cooperative Association)**
   - Internal governance document
   - Should clearly establish cooperative principles
   - Sample templates available at [Co-opLaw.org Sample Documents](https://www.co-oplaw.org/knowledge-base/sample-documents/)

3. **Membership Agreement**
   - Individual agreement signed by each worker-owner
   - Outlines rights, responsibilities, capital contributions
   - Establishes member's commitment to cooperative values

4. **Capital Account Policy**
   - Explains how member equity is tracked
   - Details patronage allocation methods
   - Describes circumstances for distribution or redemption

### Recommended Governance Documents

5. **Member Handbook**
   - Cooperative history and values
   - Governance structure explained
   - Member rights and responsibilities
   - Decision-making processes
   - Conflict resolution procedures

6. **Personnel Policies**
   - Hiring and probationary period for prospective members
   - Compensation structure
   - Benefits and leave policies
   - Performance evaluation processes
   - Disciplinary procedures

7. **Financial Policies**
   - Fiscal year designation
   - Budget development process
   - Reserve requirements
   - Distribution policies
   - Investment guidelines

8. **Meeting Procedures**
   - Annual meeting requirements
   - Board meeting schedules
   - Notice requirements
   - Quorum definitions
   - Voting procedures

### Governance Best Practices for New Mexico Cooperatives

**Board Structure:**
- Cooperative Associations require minimum 5 directors
- LLCs can be member-managed or manager-managed
- Consider staggered terms for continuity

**Meeting Requirements:**
- Annual membership meetings are standard
- Provide adequate notice (bylaws should specify)
- Keep detailed minutes
- Document all major decisions

**Decision-Making Models:**
- Consensus for major policy decisions
- Majority vote for routine matters
- Supermajority for amendments to governing documents

**Member Education:**
- Regular training on cooperative principles
- Financial literacy programs
- Leadership development

## Worker-Ownership Conversions

Converting an existing business to worker ownership is an increasingly important succession strategy, particularly as baby boomer business owners retire. New Mexico cooperatives can benefit from this pathway.

### Why Consider Conversion?

- **Business succession**: Provides exit strategy for retiring owners
- **Job preservation**: Keeps businesses and jobs in local communities
- **Wealth building**: Creates ownership opportunities for workers
- **Business continuity**: Maintains institutional knowledge and customer relationships
- **Community benefit**: Prevents closure or acquisition by out-of-state interests

### Conversion Process Overview

**Step 1: Initial Assessment**
- Evaluate business financial health
- Assess worker interest in ownership
- Review existing legal structure
- Engage experienced conversion consultants

**Step 2: Valuation**
- Obtain professional business valuation
- Consider multiple valuation methods
- Negotiate fair price with seller

**Step 3: Transaction Structure**

Common structures include:
- **Direct sale to cooperative**: Workers form cooperative entity that purchases business assets
- **Stock purchase**: Cooperative purchases ownership shares from seller
- **Installment sale**: Seller finances purchase over time
- **Hybrid structures**: Combine elements based on circumstances

**Step 4: Financing the Transition**

Conversion financing may include:
- Seller financing (often 40-60% of purchase price)
- CDFI loans from organizations like The Loan Fund
- SBA-guaranteed loans
- Member capital contributions
- Retained earnings over time

**Step 5: Legal Conversion**

For a [cooperative conversion](https://www.co-oplaw.org/knowledge-base/the-mechanics-of-cooperative-conversion/):
- Form new cooperative entity (LLC or Cooperative Association)
- Transfer assets and operations
- Or amend existing articles and bylaws to reflect cooperative structure
- Execute purchase agreement
- Transition ownership and governance

### Worker Cooperative vs. ESOP

Two main employee ownership models exist. Consider which fits best:

| Factor | Worker Cooperative | ESOP |
|--------|-------------------|------|
| Best for | Businesses under 100 employees | Larger businesses |
| Setup cost | $10,000-50,000 | $50,000-100,000+ |
| Ongoing admin | Lower | Higher (annual valuations, trustee) |
| Governance | One member, one vote | Stock-based voting |
| Tax benefits | State-specific | Significant federal benefits |
| Culture | Democratic participation | Variable employee involvement |

For most small businesses in New Mexico, worker cooperative conversion is more feasible and cost-effective than an ESOP.

### Conversion Resources

**[Democracy at Work Institute - Conversion Resources](https://institute.coop/tools/for-worker-coops/conversions):**
- Case studies and guides
- Conversion curriculum
- Technical assistance referrals

**[Project Equity](https://www.project-equity.org/):**
- National nonprofit focused on conversions
- Technical assistance and consulting
- Financing facilitation

**Succession Planning:**
- Start conversations 3-5 years before planned exit
- Involve workers early in planning process
- Consider phased transitions
- Document all systems and relationships

## Common Pitfalls in New Mexico

### Legal and Formation Pitfalls

**1. Choosing the Wrong Legal Structure**
- Starting with Cooperative Association when only 2-3 founders (requires 5 directors)
- Not including cooperative provisions in LLC Operating Agreement
- Assuming "cooperative" in name alone creates legal cooperative

**Prevention:** Consult with an attorney familiar with cooperative law; use LLC if starting small.

**2. Inadequate Operating Agreement or Bylaws**
- Using generic templates without cooperative provisions
- Failing to address member exit and buyout procedures
- Unclear decision-making authority

**Prevention:** Use cooperative-specific templates from [Co-opLaw.org](https://www.co-oplaw.org/knowledge-base/sample-documents/); customize thoroughly.

**3. Registered Agent Lapses**
- Using a member who moves out of state
- Failing to update address after office move
- Missing important legal notices

**Prevention:** Consider commercial registered agent service; maintain current contact information.

### Tax and Compliance Pitfalls

**4. Misunderstanding Gross Receipts Tax**
- Treating it like sales tax (it applies to seller)
- Not registering in all locations where business occurs
- Missing filing deadlines (25th of month following period)

**Prevention:** Review [FYI-105 Gross Receipts Tax guide](https://www.tax.newmexico.gov/forms-publications/); consult with NM-experienced accountant.

**5. Overlooking Location-Based Tax Rates**
- Collecting wrong rate (rates vary from 5.125% to 8.6875%)
- Not updating for rate changes
- Misidentifying location of service delivery

**Prevention:** Use [NM Taxation and Revenue rate lookup tools](https://www.tax.newmexico.gov/); verify rates quarterly.

**6. Neglecting Local Business Licenses**
- Assuming state registration is sufficient
- Not checking when moving or expanding to new locations
- Missing annual renewal deadlines

**Prevention:** Check with local city/county before opening; set renewal reminders.

### Governance Pitfalls

**7. Insufficient Member Education**
- New members not understanding cooperative principles
- Confusion about rights and responsibilities
- Decisions made without full participation

**Prevention:** Develop comprehensive onboarding; require cooperative education for membership.

**8. Unclear Membership Pathway**
- No defined candidacy period
- Admitting members too quickly without vetting
- No clear criteria for membership denial

**Prevention:** Establish 3-12 month candidacy period; document evaluation criteria.

**9. Poor Meeting Practices**
- Inadequate notice for important decisions
- No minutes or incomplete records
- Informal decisions not properly ratified

**Prevention:** Follow bylaws strictly; designate secretary responsible for records.

### Financial Pitfalls

**10. Undercapitalization**
- Insufficient startup capital
- No capital reserve requirements
- Relying too heavily on debt

**Prevention:** Develop realistic financial projections; require meaningful member capital contributions.

**11. Patronage Distribution Confusion**
- Not understanding tax implications
- Distributing too much, leaving inadequate reserves
- Failing to properly allocate and track patronage

**Prevention:** Work with accountant experienced in cooperative taxation; maintain detailed records.

**12. No Exit Planning**
- Unclear member buyout procedures
- Insufficient reserves for departing members
- No redemption schedule or timeline

**Prevention:** Establish clear exit procedures in Operating Agreement; build redemption reserves.

### Cultural Pitfalls

**13. Founder Dominance**
- Original members resist sharing control
- New members treated as second-class
- Informal power structures undermine democracy

**Prevention:** Build leadership development into culture; term limits for key positions.

**14. Avoiding Difficult Conversations**
- Performance issues not addressed
- Compensation disparities cause resentment
- Conflicts allowed to fester

**Prevention:** Establish clear performance expectations; regular check-ins; conflict resolution procedures.

## Frequently Asked Questions (FAQs)

### Q1: Does New Mexico have a specific worker cooperative statute?

**A:** New Mexico does not have a statute specifically for worker cooperatives, unlike states such as California or Colorado. However, worker cooperatives can form under the general [Cooperative Association Act (Chapter 53, Article 4 NMSA)](https://law.justia.com/codes/new-mexico/chapter-53/article-4/) or, more commonly, as an LLC with a cooperative Operating Agreement. The LLC approach offers more flexibility and is recommended for most worker cooperative startups in New Mexico.

### Q2: What is the difference between Gross Receipts Tax and sales tax?

**A:** [New Mexico Gross Receipts Tax (GRT)](https://www.tax.newmexico.gov/businesses/gross-receipts-overview/) is imposed on the business rather than the customer, though businesses typically pass the cost through to buyers. Unlike traditional sales tax, GRT applies to most services as well as goods. The combined state, county, and municipal rate varies by location (5.125% to 8.6875%). Businesses must register with the NM Taxation and Revenue Department and file returns monthly, quarterly, or semi-annually based on liability.

### Q3: How can we reflect the acequia tradition in our cooperative?

**A:** New Mexico's [acequia systems](https://www.taosacequias.org/acequias) offer valuable governance models: one-property-one-vote principles translate to one-member-one-vote; the practice of tying water rights to maintenance duties parallels tying ownership benefits to work contributions; collective decision-making with elected leadership (mayordomo and commissioners) mirrors cooperative board structures; and long-term stewardship thinking helps cooperatives prioritize sustainability over short-term gains. Consider incorporating these principles into your bylaws and member education.

### Q4: Do we need to file an annual report in New Mexico?

**A:** It depends on your structure. **LLCs do not file annual reports** in New Mexico, which is a significant advantage. **Cooperative Associations** formed under Chapter 53, Article 4 must file an annual report ($30 fee). **Nonprofit Corporations** also have annual reporting requirements. All businesses must maintain current registered agent information with the Secretary of State, regardless of annual report requirements.

### Q5: What are the minimum requirements to form a worker cooperative in New Mexico?

**A:** For an **LLC**: Only one member is required to form, with a $52 filing fee. For a **Cooperative Association** under Chapter 53, Article 4: You need at least five persons or two associations, minimum five directors, and a $50 filing fee. Given these requirements, most small worker cooperative startups choose the LLC structure and add members over time.

### Q6: How do we handle patronage distributions and taxes?

**A:** Worker cooperative patronage distributions have specific tax treatment. Generally:
- Patronage dividends based on labor (hours worked) may be treated as wages subject to employment taxes
- Distributions based on capital are treated as investment returns
- The cooperative may deduct qualified patronage distributions
- Members receive Form 1099-PATR for non-wage patronage
- Consult with an accountant experienced in cooperative taxation, as this area is complex

The [Democracy at Work Institute](https://institute.coop/) offers resources on cooperative accounting and taxation.

### Q7: Where can we find funding for a worker cooperative startup in New Mexico?

**A:** Key funding sources include:
- **[The Loan Fund](https://www.loanfund.org/)**: New Mexico's primary CDFI, loans $5,000-$1 million
- **[Clearinghouse CDFI](https://www.clearinghousecdfi.com/newmexico/)**: Commercial real estate and small business
- **[RCAC](https://financenewmexico.org/funding/loans-funding/rcac/)**: Rural business loans
- **[Shared Capital Cooperative](https://sharedcapital.coop/)**: National cooperative lender
- **Member contributions**: Initial capital from founding worker-owners
- **SBA loans**: Through local lenders with SBA guarantee

The [NMSBDC](https://www.nmsbdc.org/) can help you develop financial projections and identify appropriate funding sources.

### Q8: Can an existing New Mexico business convert to a worker cooperative?

**A:** Yes. Conversion involves:
1. Forming a new cooperative entity (typically LLC with cooperative Operating Agreement)
2. Obtaining a business valuation
3. Negotiating purchase terms with the selling owner
4. Securing financing (often seller financing plus CDFI loan)
5. Transferring assets and operations to the cooperative
6. Transitioning governance to worker-owners

Alternatively, an existing LLC or corporation operating cooperatively can amend its governing documents to formally adopt cooperative principles. Resources like [Project Equity](https://www.project-equity.org/) and the [Democracy at Work Institute](https://institute.coop/topics/conversion) specialize in conversion assistance. Start planning 3-5 years before the intended transition.

   - Meet with attorney familiar with cooperative law
   - Connect with accountant experienced in cooperative taxation
   - Discuss financing options with CDFI lenders

6. **Choose Your Legal Structure**
   - LLC recommended for most startups
   - Cooperative Association for larger, established groups
   - Begin drafting Operating Agreement or Bylaws

### Formation Timeline (60-90 Days)

7. **Complete Formation Filing**
   - Conduct name search
   - File Articles of Organization/Incorporation
   - Obtain EIN from IRS
   - Register with NM Taxation and Revenue Department

8. **Finalize Internal Documents**
   - Adopt Operating Agreement or Bylaws
   - Create membership agreements
   - Develop member handbook

9. **Obtain Necessary Licenses**
   - Register for local business license
   - Apply for industry-specific permits
   - Set up tax filing systems

10. **Build Your Cooperative Culture**

    - Develop governance practices
    - Create systems for democratic participation
    - Connect with other New Mexico cooperatives
