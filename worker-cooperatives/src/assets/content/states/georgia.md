# How to Open a Worker Cooperative in Georgia

A practical guide to forming and operating a worker-owned cooperative business in the Peach State.

---

## 1. Quick Summary

**What makes Georgia unique for worker cooperatives:**

Georgia does not currently have a dedicated worker cooperative statute. Unlike states such as California, Colorado, or Wisconsin that have enacted versions of the Uniform Limited Cooperative Association Act, Georgia's cooperative statutes primarily address electric membership corporations, telephone cooperatives, and agricultural marketing cooperatives.

However, legislative efforts are underway. The **Georgia Limited Worker Cooperative Association Act (LWCA)** has been proposed to provide worker cooperatives with formal legal recognition and a clearer path to organize and operate. As of early 2025, this legislation had not yet been enacted but remains under active advocacy by organizations like the Georgia Cooperative Development Center.

**Common entity choices for worker cooperatives in Georgia:**

- **Limited Liability Company (LLC)** - The most common and flexible option; can be structured cooperatively through a customized operating agreement
- **Corporation (for-profit or nonprofit)** - Less commonly used but viable with cooperative bylaws
- **Agricultural Cooperative Marketing Association** - Available only for agricultural producers under O.C.G.A. Title 2, Chapter 10, Article 3

Georgia is home to over 300 cooperative business organizations providing more than 10,000 jobs, operating across finance, utilities, agriculture, insurance, wholesale supply, and retail sectors. The state has a growing ecosystem of support organizations focused on cooperative development.

## 2. Best-Fit Legal Structures for Worker Cooperatives in Georgia

### Cooperative Corporation Statute

Georgia does **not** have a general cooperative corporation statute suitable for worker cooperatives. The existing cooperative statutes are limited to:

- **Electric Membership Corporations** - O.C.G.A. Title 46, Chapter 3, Article 4 (Georgia Electric Membership Corporation Act)
- **Agricultural Cooperative Marketing Associations** - O.C.G.A. Title 2, Chapter 10, Article 3 (§ 2-10-80 through § 2-10-111)
- **Telephone cooperatives and financial cooperatives** - Governed by separate specialized statutes

These statutes are designed for consumer-owned or producer-owned cooperatives, not worker-owned businesses.

**Proposed Legislation - Georgia Limited Worker Cooperative Association Act:**

The proposed LWCA would:
- Combine the flexibility of LLCs with cooperative governance and financial features
- Allow outside/community investor participation while ensuring workers retain control
- Provide flexibility for membership classes, voting rights, and management structures
- Create a clearer path for worker cooperatives to organize and obtain investor support

For updates on this legislation, contact the [Georgia Cooperative Development Center](https://www.georgiacoopdc.org/).

### LLC Used as a Cooperative (Recommended Approach)

The **Limited Liability Company (LLC)** is the most common and practical structure for worker cooperatives in Georgia. Key advantages include:

**Structural Benefits:**
- Member-managed LLCs allow all members to participate in decision-making (Georgia default)
- Operating agreements can be customized to reflect cooperative principles
- Pass-through taxation avoids double taxation
- Limited liability protection for members
- No minimum or maximum number of members required

**Cooperative Customizations via Operating Agreement:**
- One-member-one-vote governance (instead of default proportional voting)
- Patronage-based profit allocation (based on hours worked or labor contribution)
- Member capital accounts and internal capital accounts
- Democratic governance procedures
- Membership buy-in and buy-out provisions
- Restrictions on transfer of membership interests

**Georgia LLC Default Rules (O.C.G.A. Title 14, Chapter 11):**
- LLCs are automatically member-managed unless otherwise specified
- Each member has one vote by default for general decisions
- Major decisions (dissolution, mergers, adding members) typically require unanimous approval
- Operating agreements are not required to be filed with the state

**Important Considerations:**
- Georgia law does not require an LLC operating agreement, but one is essential for cooperatives
- Consider making the member-managed election in the Articles of Organization for clarity
- The operating agreement should address Subchapter T cooperative tax treatment if applicable

### Worker Cooperative-Friendly Provisions Worth Noting

While Georgia lacks a dedicated worker cooperative statute, the following provisions are relevant:

1. **Flexible LLC Law** - Georgia's LLC statute (O.C.G.A. § 14-11-100 et seq.) provides significant flexibility to structure governance and profit distribution cooperatively

2. **Name Restrictions** - Under O.C.G.A. § 2-10-111, the word "cooperative" in a business name may only be used by organizations complying with agricultural cooperative statutes. Worker cooperatives organized as LLCs typically use terms like "Worker-Owned," "Collective," or "Worker Cooperative" in their names

3. **Nonprofit Option** - Georgia nonprofit corporations (O.C.G.A. Title 14, Chapter 3) can be structured with members and democratic governance, though this may limit certain business activities

## 3. Step-by-Step Formation Checklist

### Step 1: Name Search and Reservation

**Search for Name Availability:**
- Use the [Georgia Business Search](https://ecorp.sos.ga.gov/businesssearch) to check if your desired name is available
- Names must be distinguishable from existing registered entities
- Note: This search is for research purposes only and does not constitute an official availability determination

**Reserve Your Name (Optional but Recommended):**
- File online through the [Georgia Corporations Division](https://ecorp.sos.ga.gov/)
- **Fee:** $35 ($25 filing fee + $10 service charge)
- **Processing time:** 7 business days (expedited options available)
- **Reservation period:** 30 days (non-renewable)
- Can also file by mail using the [Name Reservation Form](https://sos.ga.gov/sites/default/files/forms/Form%20-%20Name%20Reservation.pdf)

**Name Requirements for LLCs:**
- Must contain "Limited Liability Company," "LLC," or "L.L.C."
- Must be distinguishable from other registered entities
- Cannot imply affiliation with government agencies

### Step 2: Prepare Formation Documents

**For an LLC (most common structure):**

Prepare your **Articles of Organization** including:
- LLC name
- Principal office address
- Registered agent name and address
- Management structure (member-managed recommended for cooperatives)
- Organizer information

Prepare your **Operating Agreement** (internal document, not filed with state):
- Member classes and rights
- One-member-one-vote provisions
- Patronage-based allocation formula
- Capital contribution requirements
- Admission and withdrawal procedures
- Governance and meeting procedures
- Dissolution provisions

### Step 3: File Formation Documents

**Online Filing (Recommended):**
- File through the [Georgia Corporations Division Online Portal](https://ecorp.sos.ga.gov/)
- **Filing fee:** $100 online
- **Processing time:** Typically 7-10 business days
- Credit card payment required for online filing

**Mail Filing:**
- **Filing fee:** $110 by mail
- Mail to: Georgia Corporations Division, 2 MLK Jr. Dr., Suite 313, Floyd West Tower, Atlanta, GA 30334
- Payment by check, certified bank check, or money order

**Expedited Processing Options:**
- 2 business days: Additional $100
- Same business day (before noon): Additional $250
- 1 hour: Additional $1,000

### Step 4: Appoint and Maintain a Registered Agent

**Requirements:**
- Must be a Georgia resident individual OR a corporation/LLC authorized to do business in Georgia
- Must have a physical street address in Georgia (P.O. boxes not permitted)
- Must be available during normal business hours to receive service of process
- The LLC cannot serve as its own registered agent, but members or employees may serve

**Consequences of Non-Compliance:**
- Administrative dissolution by the Secretary of State
- Loss of good standing
- Inability to defend lawsuits if service cannot be completed

### Step 5: Obtain an EIN (Federal Employer Identification Number)

**Apply through the IRS:**
- **Online (fastest):** [IRS EIN Online Application](https://www.irs.gov/businesses/small-businesses-self-employed/apply-for-an-employer-identification-number-ein-online)
  - Available Monday-Friday, 7 a.m. to 10 p.m. Eastern
  - EIN issued immediately upon approval
  - Must complete in one session (15-minute timeout)
- **By Fax:** Submit Form SS-4 to 855-641-6935 (4 business days)
- **By Mail:** Mail Form SS-4 to IRS, Attn: EIN Operation, Cincinnati, OH 45999 (4 weeks)

**Cost:** Free (beware of third-party websites that charge fees)

**When Required:**
- Before hiring employees
- Before opening a business bank account
- Before filing certain tax returns

### Step 6: Register with Georgia Department of Revenue

**Online Registration:**
- Register through the [Georgia Tax Center (GTC)](https://gtc.dor.ga.gov/)
- Create an account and select "Register a New Georgia Business"
- Tax account number typically issued within 15 minutes by email

**Before Registering:**
- Obtain your federal EIN first
- Register your entity with the Secretary of State

**Types of Tax Accounts:**
- **Withholding Tax** - Required if you have employees (O.C.G.A. § 48-7-100)
- **Sales and Use Tax** - If selling taxable goods or services
- **Corporate Income Tax** - For corporations (LLCs typically pass through)

**Unemployment Insurance:**
- Register separately with the [Georgia Department of Labor](https://dol.georgia.gov/)
- Required if you have employees

**Contact:** Georgia Department of Revenue at (877) 423-6711

### Step 7: Obtain Local Business Licenses

Georgia requires business licenses at the local (city and/or county) level. Requirements vary by jurisdiction.

**General Process:**
1. Determine your business location (city and county)
2. Contact local government for specific requirements
3. Obtain a Business Occupational Tax Certificate

**City of Atlanta Example:**
- Apply through [ATLBIZ Portal](https://www.atlantaga.gov/i-want-to/apply-for-a-business-license)
- All businesses within Atlanta city limits require a Business Occupational Tax Certificate
- E-Verify registration required for employers with 11+ employees
- Licenses expire December 31 annually

**State Resource:**
- [First Stop Business Information Center](https://sos.ga.gov/page/first-stop-business-information-center) - Georgia Secretary of State's resource for state and local licensing requirements
- [Georgia.org Business License Guide](https://georgia.org/small-business/get-business-license-georgia)

## 4. Governance and Documents You Should Prepare

### Essential Documents Checklist

| Document | Purpose | Filed with State? |
|----------|---------|-------------------|
| Articles of Organization | Creates the LLC | Yes |
| Operating Agreement | Governs internal operations | No |
| Membership Agreement | Individual member terms | No |
| Member Handbook | Policies and procedures | No |
| Meeting Minutes Template | Document decisions | No |
| Patronage Allocation Policy | Define profit distribution | No |

### Operating Agreement Tips for Worker Cooperatives

Your operating agreement is the most critical document for establishing cooperative governance. Consider including:

**Membership Classes:**
- **Worker-Members:** Employees who meet membership criteria and have full voting rights
- **Probationary Members:** New employees working toward full membership (limited or no voting)
- **Non-Worker Members (if applicable):** Community investors or supporters (typically limited voting rights to maintain worker control)

**Voting Provisions:**
- One-member-one-vote for all major decisions
- Quorum requirements (commonly 50% or more of members)
- Supermajority requirements for significant decisions (amendments, dissolution, large expenditures)
- Proxy voting rules
- Board election procedures

**Patronage and Allocations:**
- Define "patronage" clearly (typically hours worked, though other formulas are possible)
- Establish allocation formula (percentage of net income allocated based on patronage)
- Distinguish between:
  - **Qualified allocations** - At least 20% paid in cash; remainder in equity credits
  - **Nonqualified allocations** - Retained by cooperative; taxed when distributed
- Internal capital account structure
- Revolving fund or equity redemption schedule

**Capital Contributions:**
- Initial buy-in amount
- Payment plans for new members
- Treatment of member capital upon departure
- Restrictions on transfer of membership interests

**Membership Admission and Termination:**
- Eligibility requirements (employment duration, hours worked)
- Application and approval process
- Probationary period terms
- Grounds for termination or expulsion
- Appeal procedures
- Return of capital upon departure

**Governance Structure:**
- Board of Directors composition and election
- Officer roles and responsibilities
- Committee structures
- Member meeting frequency and procedures
- Decision-making processes (consensus, majority vote, etc.)

### Subchapter T Tax Considerations

If structured properly, worker cooperatives can benefit from cooperative tax treatment under IRC Subchapter T:
- Patronage dividends distributed to members are deductible by the cooperative
- Members report patronage dividends as income
- Requires operating on a "cooperative basis" with patronage-based allocations

Consult a tax professional or accountant familiar with cooperative taxation to ensure compliance.

## 6. Worker-Ownership Conversions

Converting an existing business to worker ownership is increasingly common as business owners seek succession solutions that preserve jobs and company culture.

### How Conversions Typically Work in Georgia

**General Process:**

1. **Assessment Phase**
   - Business valuation by independent appraiser
   - Feasibility analysis
   - Financial review and due diligence
   - Employee interest assessment

2. **Structure Selection**
   - Worker cooperative (LLC or corporation with cooperative operating agreement)
   - ESOP (Employee Stock Ownership Plan)
   - Employee Ownership Trust (EOT)

3. **Transaction Planning**
   - Financing structure (seller financing, bank loans, cooperative lenders)
   - Legal entity formation or restructuring
   - Tax planning for seller and employees

4. **Implementation**
   - Legal documentation
   - Employee education and governance training
   - Transition of ownership and management
   - Ongoing support and technical assistance

### ESOP vs. Worker Cooperative Conversion

| Factor | ESOP | Worker Cooperative |
|--------|------|-------------------|
| **Best for** | Larger companies (20+ employees), profitable, low debt | Any size, especially smaller businesses |
| **Governance** | Board-elected; trustees vote shares | One-member-one-vote democratic control |
| **Ownership** | Shares allocated based on compensation | Equal or patronage-based ownership |
| **Tax Benefits** | Significant (C-corp selling shareholders, S-corp flow-through) | Subchapter T patronage deductions |
| **Complexity** | Higher (ERISA regulated, requires trustee, annual valuations) | Lower (more flexible structure) |
| **Cost to Establish** | Higher ($50,000-$150,000+) | Lower (varies by complexity) |
| **Worker Control** | Indirect (through trustees) | Direct democratic participation |

**Employee Ownership Trust (EOT):**
- Newer model gaining traction
- Company owned by perpetual trust for employee benefit
- Profits above reinvestment needs go to employees
- May offer middle ground between ESOP complexity and cooperative flexibility

### Georgia-Specific Conversion Resources

- **Georgia Center for Employee Ownership (GACEO):** Education and service provider referrals for all forms of employee ownership
- **Georgia Cooperative Development Center:** Technical assistance for cooperative conversions
- **Shared Capital Cooperative:** Financing for cooperative acquisitions and conversions

### Tax Considerations for Sellers

Business owners selling to employees may benefit from:
- **ESOP:** Potential tax deferral under IRC Section 1042 (C-corps only)
- **Cooperative:** Installment sale treatment; potential community development incentives
- Consult with tax professionals familiar with employee ownership transactions

## 7. Common Pitfalls in Georgia

### Legal Structure Issues

1. **Using "Cooperative" in the Business Name Improperly**
   - O.C.G.A. § 2-10-111 restricts use of "cooperative" to agricultural cooperatives
   - Worker cooperatives should use alternative terms: "Worker-Owned," "Collective," "Worker Cooperative, LLC"
   - Consult an attorney before finalizing your business name

2. **Inadequate Operating Agreement**
   - Georgia does not require an operating agreement, but it is essential for cooperatives
   - Default LLC rules do not reflect cooperative principles
   - Failure to address patronage allocations can create tax problems

3. **Failing to Clearly Establish Worker Control**
   - If seeking cooperative tax treatment or mission alignment, ensure bylaws/operating agreement clearly establish democratic worker control
   - Document that workers have majority voting power

### Compliance and Administrative Issues

4. **Missing Annual Registration Deadline**
   - Due April 1 each year
   - $25 late fee
   - Administrative dissolution if not filed within 60 days of deadline
   - Set calendar reminders; consider multi-year filing option

5. **Registered Agent Lapses**
   - Must maintain a Georgia registered agent at all times
   - Using a P.O. box is prohibited
   - Agent must be available during business hours

6. **Forgetting Local Business Licenses**
   - Georgia requires city/county business licenses in addition to state registration
   - Requirements vary by jurisdiction
   - Licenses typically expire December 31 and must be renewed annually

### Financial and Tax Issues

7. **Misunderstanding Patronage Tax Treatment**
   - Subchapter T has specific requirements for cooperative tax treatment
   - Qualified patronage dividends require 20% minimum cash distribution
   - Members must be notified of allocations in writing
   - Work with an accountant familiar with cooperative taxation

8. **Inadequate Capitalization**
   - Worker cooperatives often struggle with initial capital
   - Plan for member buy-ins, retained earnings, and external financing
   - Consider Shared Capital Cooperative or Seed Commons network for loans

9. **Confusing Wages with Patronage**
   - Member-workers should receive market-rate wages
   - Patronage dividends are distributions of surplus, not compensation
   - Improper classification can create payroll tax issues

### Governance and Culture Issues

10. **Insufficient Member Education**
    - Democratic governance requires educated, engaged members
    - Invest in training on cooperative principles, financial literacy, and governance
    - New member orientation is critical

11. **Unclear Membership Criteria and Process**
    - Define clear eligibility requirements
    - Establish probationary periods
    - Document admission and termination procedures

12. **Lack of Professional Guidance**
    - Georgia's lack of a worker cooperative statute means careful legal structuring is essential
    - Engage attorneys and accountants with cooperative experience
    - Contact GCDC or USFWC for referrals

## 8. FAQs for Georgia

### Q1: Does Georgia have a worker cooperative statute?

**A:** No, Georgia does not currently have a statute specifically authorizing worker cooperatives. However, the Georgia Limited Worker Cooperative Association Act (LWCA) has been proposed and is under active advocacy. In the meantime, worker cooperatives typically organize as LLCs with cooperative operating agreements, or less commonly as corporations with cooperative bylaws. Contact the [Georgia Cooperative Development Center](https://www.georgiacoopdc.org/) for updates on the LWCA legislation.

### Q2: What is the best legal structure for a worker cooperative in Georgia?

**A:** The Limited Liability Company (LLC) is the most common and flexible structure for worker cooperatives in Georgia. It provides:
- Pass-through taxation
- Limited liability protection
- Flexibility to customize governance through the operating agreement
- Member-managed structure by default

Your operating agreement should be customized to reflect cooperative principles (one-member-one-vote, patronage-based allocations, democratic governance). Consult with an attorney experienced in cooperative business structures.

### Q3: How much does it cost to form a worker cooperative in Georgia?

**A:** Estimated minimum costs for forming an LLC-structured worker cooperative:

| Item | Cost |
|------|------|
| Name reservation (optional) | $35 |
| Articles of Organization (online) | $100 |
| Federal EIN | Free |
| Georgia Tax Registration | Free |
| Local Business License | Varies ($50-$300+) |
| Annual Registration (LLC) | $50/year |
| **Minimum Total** | **~$200-$500** |

Additional costs may include:
- Attorney fees for operating agreement drafting ($1,000-$5,000+)
- Accountant consultation
- Initial member education and training

### Q4: Can I use the word "cooperative" in my Georgia business name?

**A:** Caution is advised. O.C.G.A. § 2-10-111 restricts the use of "cooperative" in business names to organizations complying with agricultural cooperative statutes. While this statute technically applies to agricultural cooperatives, worker cooperatives often use alternative terminology to avoid potential issues:
- "[Business Name] Worker-Owned, LLC"
- "[Business Name] Collective, LLC"
- "[Business Name] Worker Cooperative, LLC"

Consult with an attorney regarding your specific circumstances.

### Q5: What annual filings are required for a Georgia worker cooperative LLC?

**A:** Key annual requirements include:

- **Georgia Annual Registration:** Due April 1; $50 fee; filed with Secretary of State
- **Local Business License Renewal:** Typically due by December 31; fees vary
- **Federal Tax Returns:** Form 1065 (partnership return) or cooperative return; due March 15 (or 15th day of 3rd month after fiscal year end)
- **Georgia Tax Returns:** As applicable for withholding, sales tax, etc.
- **1099-PATR Forms:** If distributing patronage dividends to members; due January 31

### Q6: How do worker cooperatives in Georgia handle taxes?

**A:** Worker cooperative taxation depends on structure and elections:

**As an LLC (default partnership taxation):**
- File Form 1065 (partnership return)
- Members receive Schedule K-1
- Income passes through to members

**Subchapter T Cooperative Treatment:**
- Available to cooperatives operating on a cooperative basis
- Patronage dividends are deductible by the cooperative
- Requires proper documentation and member notification
- Qualified allocations must be at least 20% cash

**Member-Workers:**
- Receive W-2 wages for labor (subject to payroll taxes)
- Report patronage dividends as income
- May have capital account balances

Consult a tax professional familiar with cooperative taxation to determine the best approach for your situation.

### Q7: Where can I find technical assistance for starting a worker cooperative in Georgia?

**A:** Key resources include:

- **Georgia Cooperative Development Center (GCDC):** [georgiacoopdc.org](https://www.georgiacoopdc.org/) - Feasibility studies, business planning, training, Georgia Cooperative Academy
- **Georgia Center for Employee Ownership:** [gaceo.org](https://www.gaceo.org/) - Employee ownership education and resources
- **UGA Small Business Development Center:** [georgiasbdc.org](https://georgiasbdc.org/) - Free business consulting at 18 locations statewide
- **U.S. Federation of Worker Cooperatives:** [usworker.coop](https://www.usworker.coop/) - National membership organization, Co-op Clinic, resources
- **Federation of Southern Cooperatives:** [federation.coop](https://www.federation.coop/) - Headquartered in East Point, Georgia

### Q8: Can an existing Georgia business convert to a worker cooperative?

**A:** Yes. Business conversions to worker ownership are increasingly common for succession planning. The process typically involves:

1. Business valuation and feasibility assessment
2. Selection of ownership structure (cooperative, ESOP, or EOT)
3. Legal restructuring and documentation
4. Financing arrangement (seller financing, bank loans, cooperative lenders like Shared Capital)
5. Employee education and governance implementation

The Georgia Center for Employee Ownership and Georgia Cooperative Development Center both provide assistance with conversions. For financing, consider Shared Capital Cooperative's Worker Ownership Loan Fund.
