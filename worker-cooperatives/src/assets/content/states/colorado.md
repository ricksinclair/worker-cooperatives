# How to Start a Worker Cooperative in Colorado

Colorado has earned a reputation as one of the most cooperative-friendly states in the nation, sometimes called "The Delaware of Cooperative Law" due to its robust statutory framework and supportive ecosystem. The state offers multiple legal pathways for forming worker cooperatives, including the innovative Uniform Limited Cooperative Association Act (ULCAA), generous tax credits for employee ownership conversions, and a thriving network of development organizations.

---

## Quick Summary

### What Makes Colorado Unique for Worker Cooperatives

- **Model Cooperative Legislation**: Colorado adopted the Uniform Limited Cooperative Association Act (ULCAA) in 2011, creating one of the most flexible and well-designed cooperative statutes in the country
- **Multiple Entity Options**: Three cooperative statutes (Articles 55, 56, and 58) plus the ability to structure an LLC as a cooperative
- **Public Benefit Corporation Overlay**: Colorado allows cooperatives to elect public benefit corporation status for additional mission protection
- **Generous Tax Credits**: Up to $40,000 in state tax credits for worker cooperative conversions through the Employee Ownership Tax Credit program
- **State Government Support**: The Colorado Employee Ownership Office (within OEDIT) provides dedicated resources, peer networks, and technical assistance
- **Low Filing Fees**: Colorado business filings are among the least expensive in the country
- **Strong Development Ecosystem**: Multiple organizations (RMEOC, CCW, Rocky Mountain Farmers Union) provide cooperative development support

### Common Entity Choices for Worker Cooperatives in Colorado

| Entity Type | Best For | Key Features |
|-------------|----------|--------------|
| Limited Cooperative Association (LCA) - Article 58 | Worker coops seeking outside investment; hybrid structures | Patron and investor member classes; flexible allocation rules |
| Cooperative Corporation - Article 56 | Traditional worker coops focused on patron members only | One-member-one-vote; patronage-based distributions |
| LLC (cooperative operating agreement) | Small worker coops wanting simplicity and flexibility | Pass-through taxation; customizable governance |
| Cooperative Corporation - Article 55 | Older cooperatives; less commonly used today | Historical statute; most have converted to Article 56 |

## Best-Fit Legal Structures for Worker Cooperatives

### 1. Limited Cooperative Association (LCA) - Article 58

The Colorado Uniform Limited Cooperative Association Act ([C.R.S. Title 7, Article 58](https://law.justia.com/codes/colorado/title-7/associations/article-58/)) represents a modern, hybrid approach that combines traditional cooperative principles with modern financing mechanisms.

**Key Features:**
- **Two Member Classes**: Patron members (workers) and investor members, each with distinct rights
- **Flexible Profit Allocation**: Can allocate returns to both patrons (based on patronage) and investors (based on capital contribution)
- **Democratic Safeguards**: Patron members must retain majority voting control (at least 51%)
- **Limited Liability**: Members have limited liability similar to LLCs and corporations
- **Purpose Flexibility**: May be organized for any lawful purpose, whether or not for profit

**When to Choose an LCA:**
- You want to attract outside investment while maintaining worker control
- You need flexibility in how profits are distributed
- You anticipate having both worker-members and investor-members
- You want a modern statute with clear guidance

**Formation Requirements:**
- File Articles of Organization with the Secretary of State
- Adopt bylaws within a reasonable time after formation
- Initial directors (if named) hold organizational meeting to adopt bylaws
- Initial directors need not be members

### 2. Cooperative Corporation - Article 56 (Colorado Cooperative Act)

The [Colorado Cooperative Act (C.R.S. Title 7, Article 56)](https://law.justia.com/codes/colorado/2022/title-7/article-56/) is the "standard" cooperative corporation statute and the most commonly used for traditional worker cooperatives.

**Key Features:**
- **One Member, One Vote**: Democratic control regardless of capital contribution
- **Patronage-Based Earnings**: Distribution of earnings based on members' contributions to or use of the cooperative
- **Limitations on Dividends**: Restrictions on dividends on stock and interest on equity capital
- **Member-Oriented Purpose**: Operated for the mutual benefit of all members
- **Board of Directors**: Traditional corporate governance structure with board and bylaws

**When to Choose an Article 56 Cooperative:**
- You want a purely member-owned structure without outside investors
- You prefer traditional cooperative principles and governance
- Democratic equality among members is paramount
- You want clear statutory guidance for cooperative operations

**Formation Requirements:**
- One or more persons may act as incorporator (must be at least 18 years old)
- File Articles of Incorporation with the Secretary of State
- Bylaws must be adopted within 30 days of the effective date of the Articles

### 3. LLC Structured as a Worker Cooperative

An LLC can be structured to operate as a worker cooperative through a carefully drafted operating agreement that incorporates cooperative principles.

**Key Features:**
- **Pass-Through Taxation**: Profits and losses pass through to members (typically more favorable for small coops)
- **Operational Flexibility**: Operating agreement can be customized to match cooperative principles
- **Internal Capital Accounts**: Can establish patronage-based accounting similar to traditional cooperatives
- **Simpler Formation**: Generally simpler to form than a cooperative corporation
- **No Mandatory Statutory Requirements**: Must rely on operating agreement to establish cooperative governance

**When to Choose an LLC:**
- You have a small group of worker-owners (typically under 10-15)
- You want pass-through taxation
- You want maximum flexibility in structuring governance and distributions
- You are comfortable with a more contract-based (rather than statutory) framework

**Important Considerations:**
- Must carefully draft operating agreement to include cooperative principles
- No statutory "guard rails" specific to cooperatives
- May need to explain cooperative nature to banks, vendors, and others unfamiliar with cooperative LLCs
- Employment law requirements still apply to worker-members

### 4. Public Benefit Corporation Overlay

Colorado is one of the few states that allows cooperatives to elect [Public Benefit Corporation status (C.R.S. Title 7, Article 101, Part 5)](https://leg.colorado.gov/). This can be layered onto any of the three cooperative statutes.

**Benefits:**
- Additional mission-lock protection
- Directors must consider stakeholder interests beyond shareholders
- Enhanced credibility for social mission
- Potential marketing advantages

## Step-by-Step Formation Checklist

### Step 1: Choose Your Legal Structure

- [ ] Review the entity options above
- [ ] Consider consulting with a cooperative attorney (see Resources section)
- [ ] Decide between LCA (Article 58), Cooperative Corporation (Article 56), or LLC

### Step 2: Name Search and Reservation

**Search for Name Availability:**
- Use the [Colorado Secretary of State Name Availability Search](https://www.coloradosos.gov/biz/NameCriteria.do)
- Name must be distinguishable from other registered entity names
- Trade names and trademarks are not included in the search

**Reserve Your Name (Optional but Recommended):**
- File a Statement of Reservation of Name online
- **Fee**: $25
- **Duration**: 120 days (renewable for additional $25)
- Include appropriate entity designator in name (e.g., "Cooperative," "LCA," "LLC")

**Name Requirements by Entity Type:**
- LCA: Must include "limited cooperative association" or abbreviation (L.C.A. or LCA)
- Article 56 Cooperative: Must include "cooperative" or "co-op" or similar designation
- LLC: Must include "Limited Liability Company" or "LLC" or "L.L.C."

### Step 3: Prepare Formation Documents

**For Limited Cooperative Association (LCA):**
- Prepare Articles of Organization including:
  - Name of the LCA
  - Principal office address
  - Registered agent name and address
  - Names and addresses of organizers
  - Statement of purpose
  - Duration (if not perpetual)
  - Initial directors (optional)
- Download sample form: [LCA Articles of Organization (PDF)](https://www.coloradosos.gov/pubs/business/sampleForms/ARTORG_LCA.pdf)

**For Article 56 Cooperative Corporation:**
- Prepare Articles of Incorporation including:
  - Name of the cooperative
  - Principal office address
  - Registered agent name and address
  - Names and addresses of incorporators
  - Purpose of the cooperative
  - Membership structure
  - Stock/membership interests (if applicable)
- See [Articles of Incorporation Instructions](https://www.coloradosos.gov/pubs/business/helpFiles/ARTINC_56_HELP.html)

**For LLC:**
- Prepare Articles of Organization
- Draft Operating Agreement with cooperative provisions (not filed with state but essential)

### Step 4: Appoint a Registered Agent

**Requirements (Effective July 1, 2025):**
- Must have a physical street address in Colorado (P.O. boxes not allowed)
- Must be available during normal business hours to accept legal documents
- Can be an individual or a business entity

**For Individual Registered Agents:**
- Must be at least 18 years old
- Must have Colorado residency verified by:
  - Valid Colorado driver's license or state ID, OR
  - Alternative address verification process (passcode mailed to physical address)

**For Entity Registered Agents:**
- Must be registered with Colorado Secretary of State
- Must be in good standing
- Must maintain physical location in Colorado

**More Information**: [Registered Agent Requirements](https://www.coloradosos.gov/pubs/business/RAchanges.html)

### Step 5: File Formation Documents

**Filing Method:**
- Colorado requires online filing (paper forms not accepted for most filings)
- File through the [Colorado Secretary of State Business Portal](https://www.sos.state.co.us/biz/FileDoc.do)

**Filing Fees:**
- LCA Articles of Organization: $50 (online)
- Article 56 Cooperative Articles of Incorporation: $50 (online)
- LLC Articles of Organization: $50 (online)
- Name Reservation: $25
- Trade Name (DBA): $20

**Processing Time:**
- Online filings are typically processed within 1-3 business days

### Step 6: Obtain an EIN (Employer Identification Number)

- Apply online through the [IRS EIN Assistant](https://www.irs.gov/businesses/small-businesses-self-employed/apply-for-an-employer-identification-number-ein-online)
- **Fee**: Free
- **Processing**: Immediate (online application)
- Required for:
  - Opening a business bank account
  - Hiring employees
  - Filing federal taxes
  - Most state registrations

### Step 7: Register with Colorado Department of Revenue

**Sales Tax License (if selling taxable goods/services):**
- Apply through [MyBizColorado](https://mybiz.colorado.gov/) or [Form CR 0100](https://tax.colorado.gov/how-to-apply-for-a-colorado-sales-tax-license)
- **Fee**: $16 for two-year license + $50 refundable deposit
- Valid for two calendar years (expires end of odd-numbered years)
- Each physical location requires separate license

**Wage Withholding Account (if hiring employees):**
- Register for state income tax withholding
- Apply through MyBizColorado

**Unemployment Insurance:**
- Register with the [Colorado Department of Labor and Employment](https://cdle.colorado.gov/)

**More Information**: [Colorado Department of Revenue](https://tax.colorado.gov/)

### Step 8: Obtain Local Business Licenses

Colorado does not have a general state business license. Licensing is handled at the city and county level.

**Common Requirements:**
- General business license (varies by municipality)
- Home occupation permit (if home-based)
- Zoning permits
- Industry-specific permits (food service, childcare, etc.)
- Signage permits

**How to Find Local Requirements:**
- Contact your city hall or county clerk
- Use [MyBizColorado](https://mybiz.colorado.gov/) for guidance
- Major cities:
  - Denver: [Business Licensing Center](https://www.denvergov.org/Government/Agencies-Departments-Offices/Agencies-Departments-Offices-Directory/Business-Licensing)
  - Colorado Springs: [Business Licensing](https://coloradosprings.gov/businesslicensing)

### Step 9: Draft Governance Documents

- Bylaws (for cooperative corporations and LCAs) or Operating Agreement (for LLCs)
- Member agreements/worker-owner agreements
- Personnel policies
- See Governance section below for detailed guidance

### Step 10: Annual Compliance

**Periodic Report:**
- Due annually on the anniversary of formation
- **Fee**: $10 (online)
- File through the [Secretary of State Business Portal](https://www.sos.state.co.us/pubs/business/businessHome.html)
- Failure to file may result in administrative dissolution

## Governance and Documents You Should Prepare

### Bylaws (Cooperative Corporations and LCAs)

Bylaws provide the framework for your cooperative's legal, governance, and operational structure. Key provisions to address:

**Membership:**
- Eligibility requirements (who can become a member)
- Admission process and probationary periods
- Member capital contributions (buy-in)
- Member rights and responsibilities
- Termination and withdrawal procedures

**Governance:**
- Board of directors composition and election
- Officer positions and duties
- Meeting requirements (annual, regular, special)
- Quorum requirements
- Voting procedures (one member, one vote)
- Committee structure

**Financial:**
- Fiscal year
- Patronage calculation method (hours worked, wages earned, etc.)
- Patronage dividend allocation and distribution
- Retained earnings and reserves
- Internal capital accounts
- Interest on member capital (if any)

**Operations:**
- Amendment procedures
- Dissolution provisions
- Indemnification of directors and officers

### Operating Agreement (LLCs)

For LLCs structured as cooperatives, the operating agreement must explicitly incorporate cooperative principles:

**Essential Cooperative Provisions:**
- Democratic governance (one member, one vote)
- Membership eligibility and classes
- Patronage-based allocation of profits
- Internal capital account system
- Distributions based on patronage (not capital)
- Membership interest transfer restrictions

**Sample Language Resources:**
- [ICA Model Bylaws for Worker Cooperatives](https://institute.coop/sites/default/files/ica-model-bylaws.pdf) (adaptable for LLC operating agreements)
- Consult with a cooperative attorney for Colorado-specific drafting

### Member/Worker-Owner Agreement

A separate agreement between the cooperative and each member typically covers:
- Capital contribution requirements
- Work expectations and standards
- Confidentiality obligations
- Non-compete provisions (if applicable)
- Intellectual property
- Dispute resolution procedures

### Personnel Policies

Even in a worker cooperative, employment law applies. Develop policies covering:
- Compensation structure
- Working hours and schedules
- Time off and leave policies
- Performance evaluation
- Grievance procedures
- Health and safety

## Worker-Ownership Conversions

Colorado is a leading state for business conversions to employee ownership, with strong tax incentives and technical support.

### Conversion Process Overview

**Typical Timeline**: 6-12 months

**Key Steps:**
1. **Feasibility Assessment**: Evaluate if conversion is right for your business
2. **Choose Ownership Structure**: Worker cooperative, ESOP, EOT, or other
3. **Valuation**: Determine fair market value of the business
4. **Deal Structure**: Negotiate terms between selling owner(s) and employees
5. **Legal Documentation**: Draft conversion agreements, bylaws, etc.
6. **Financing**: Secure funding for the buyout
7. **Transition**: Implement new governance and ownership structure
8. **Training**: Educate worker-owners on their new roles

### Worker Cooperative vs. ESOP: Colorado Comparison

| Factor | Worker Cooperative | ESOP |
|--------|-------------------|------|
| **CO Tax Credit** | Up to $40,000 | Up to $150,000 |
| **Governance** | Democratic (one person, one vote) | Not required; varies |
| **Best Size** | Small to medium businesses | Medium to large businesses |
| **Setup Cost** | Lower ($10,000-$75,000 typical) | Higher ($50,000-$150,000+) |
| **Federal Tax Benefits** | Subchapter T deductions | S-corp ESOP can be tax-free |
| **Complexity** | Simpler | More complex (ERISA-regulated) |
| **Worker Control** | Direct ownership and voting | Indirect through trust |

## Common Pitfalls in Colorado

### 1. Choosing the Wrong Entity Structure

**Problem**: Selecting an LCA when you don't need investor members, or choosing an LLC without proper cooperative provisions.

**Solution**:
- Carefully evaluate your needs for outside investment
- If purely worker-owned with no outside investors, Article 56 may be simpler
- Consult with a cooperative attorney before filing

### 2. Inadequate Operating Documents

**Problem**: Using generic bylaws or operating agreements that don't address cooperative-specific issues like patronage allocation, membership termination, or dissolution.

**Solution**:
- Work with an attorney experienced in cooperative law
- Address all key governance and financial issues in writing
- Include clear procedures for common scenarios (member departure, disputes, etc.)

### 3. Confusing Ownership with Management

**Problem**: Believing that worker ownership means management by committee or consensus on every decision.

**Solution**:
- Clearly define decision-making levels and authority
- Distinguish between governance (board/membership decisions) and operations (day-to-day management)
- Establish clear roles, responsibilities, and accountability

### 4. Ignoring Employment Law

**Problem**: Assuming that because workers are owners, employment laws don't apply.

**Solution**:
- Worker-owners are typically still employees for legal purposes
- Comply with minimum wage, overtime, workers' compensation, and other employment laws
- Establish proper payroll and withholding procedures

### 5. Underestimating Startup Capital and Time

**Problem**: Launching without adequate capital or realistic timelines.

**Solution**:
- Develop a detailed business plan with realistic financial projections
- Secure adequate startup capital before launching
- Allow 6-12 months for proper formation and organizing

### 6. Failing to Maintain Registered Agent Compliance

**Problem**: Not meeting new registered agent requirements effective July 1, 2025.

**Solution**:
- Ensure registered agent has verified Colorado physical address
- Use a professional registered agent service if needed
- Update filings if registered agent information changes

### 7. Missing Annual Filings

**Problem**: Failing to file the annual Periodic Report, leading to administrative dissolution.

**Solution**:
- Set calendar reminders for your anniversary date
- File the $10 Periodic Report online annually
- Keep contact information current with Secretary of State

### 8. Not Applying for Tax Credit Before Conversion

**Problem**: Completing a conversion before applying for the Employee Ownership Tax Credit.

**Solution**:
- Apply for the tax credit BEFORE completing your conversion
- Budget 6-12 months for the conversion process
- Contact CEOO early in your planning process

## FAQs for Colorado

### 1. What is the difference between an LCA and an Article 56 Cooperative?

The main difference is that a Limited Cooperative Association (LCA) under Article 58 can have two types of members: patron members (workers) and investor members. This allows the cooperative to raise outside capital while maintaining worker control. An Article 56 Cooperative Corporation is designed for patron-members only and follows more traditional cooperative principles where all earnings are distributed based on patronage. If you don't anticipate needing outside investors, an Article 56 cooperative is typically simpler.

### 2. Can I form a worker cooperative as an LLC in Colorado?

Yes. While Colorado has excellent cooperative-specific statutes, you can structure an LLC to operate as a worker cooperative through your operating agreement. The LLC operating agreement would include provisions for democratic governance (one member, one vote), patronage-based profit allocation, and internal capital accounts. This approach offers flexibility and pass-through taxation but lacks the statutory "guard rails" of the cooperative statutes. It's commonly used for smaller cooperatives.

### 3. How much does it cost to form a worker cooperative in Colorado?

State filing fees are minimal: $50 for Articles of Organization/Incorporation and $10 annually for the Periodic Report. However, total formation costs typically range from $5,000 to $25,000+ when including legal fees for drafting bylaws/operating agreements, accounting setup, and other professional services. The Colorado Employee Ownership Tax Credit can cover up to 50% of conversion costs (up to $40,000 for worker cooperatives). Some cooperative development organizations offer sliding-scale or subsidized assistance.

### 4. What is the Employee Ownership Tax Credit and how do I qualify?

The Colorado Employee Ownership Tax Credit provides up to 50% of qualified conversion costs for businesses transitioning to employee ownership. For worker cooperatives, the maximum credit is $40,000. To qualify, your business must be headquartered in Colorado, have at least 3 full-time employees (or 3 cooperative members), and offer at least 20% equity to employees (excluding founders). Critically, you must apply BEFORE completing your conversion. Applications are accepted on a rolling basis through December 31, 2026. Apply through the [Colorado Employee Ownership Office](https://oedit.colorado.gov/employee-ownership-tax-credit).

### 5. Do worker-owners in a cooperative need to be paid minimum wage?

Generally, yes. There is a legal presumption that anyone working for a corporation or cooperative is an employee, even if they are also owners. As a result, worker-members must typically be paid at least minimum wage and may be entitled to overtime. Workers' compensation insurance, payroll tax withholding, and other employment law requirements also apply. Some very limited exceptions may exist for certain bona fide partner arrangements, but cooperatives should consult an employment attorney and typically treat worker-members as employees.

### 6. Can out-of-state businesses incorporate as a cooperative in Colorado?

Yes. Due to Colorado's excellent cooperative statutes and low filing fees, some cooperative practitioners have suggested Colorado could become "The Delaware of Cooperative Law." An out-of-state cooperative could form in Colorado and then register as a foreign entity in its home state. However, this adds complexity and cost, and you'd need to comply with both states' requirements. Consult with a cooperative attorney to determine if this approach makes sense for your situation.

### 7. What ongoing compliance requirements exist for Colorado cooperatives?

The main ongoing requirement is filing an annual Periodic Report with the Colorado Secretary of State ($10 fee, due on your anniversary date). You must also maintain a registered agent with a valid Colorado physical address, keep your registered agent information current, and comply with any applicable state and local tax filings (sales tax, income tax withholding, etc.). Cooperatives should also hold required member and board meetings as specified in their bylaws and maintain proper records.

### 8. Where can I find help starting a worker cooperative in Colorado?

Colorado has excellent resources:
- **Rocky Mountain Employee Ownership Center (RMEOC)**: [rmeoc.org](https://www.rmeoc.org/) - Comprehensive conversion and development support
- **Center for Community Wealth (CCW)**: [communitywealthbuilding.org](https://www.communitywealthbuilding.org/) - Urban cooperative development, academies
- **Colorado Employee Ownership Office**: [oedit.colorado.gov/colorado-employee-ownership-office](https://oedit.colorado.gov/colorado-employee-ownership-office) - Tax credits, peer networks
- **Colorado SBDC**: [sbdc.colorado.gov](https://sbdc.colorado.gov/) - Free business advising
