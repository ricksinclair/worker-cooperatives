# How to Open a Worker Cooperative in Arkansas

A comprehensive guide to forming and operating a worker-owned cooperative business in the State of Arkansas.

---

## 1. Quick Summary

**What makes Arkansas unique for worker cooperatives:**

Arkansas has a **general cooperative association statute** (Arkansas Code Title 4, Chapter 30) that permits the formation of cooperatives for agricultural, mercantile, mining, manufacturing, or mechanical business purposes. While not specifically designed for worker cooperatives, this statute can accommodate worker-owned businesses operating "on the cooperative plan."

**Key distinguishing features:**
- General cooperative statute permits formation for "manufacturing" or "mechanical" businesses, which can encompass many worker cooperative activities
- One of the most affordable states for LLC formation ($45 online filing fee)
- Strong SBDC network with offices at multiple universities across the state
- Access to Southern regional cooperative support through organizations like the Federation of Southern Cooperatives and Southern Reparations Loan Fund network
- Flexible LLC statute (Arkansas Code Title 4, Chapter 38) allows cooperative principles to be established through operating agreements

**Common entity choices for worker coops in Arkansas:**
1. **Limited Liability Company (LLC)** with cooperative operating agreement - Most common choice offering flexibility and pass-through taxation
2. **Cooperative Association** (Arkansas Code Title 4, Chapter 30) - Traditional cooperative corporation form
3. **Standard Business Corporation** (C-Corp or S-Corp) with cooperative bylaws - Less common but available

## 2. Best-Fit Legal Structures for Worker Coops in Arkansas

### Option A: LLC with Cooperative Operating Agreement (Most Common)

**Statutory Authority:** [Arkansas Code Title 4, Chapter 38 - Arkansas Limited Liability Company Act](https://law.justia.com/codes/arkansas/title-4/subtitle-3/chapter-38/)

Arkansas's LLC statute provides significant flexibility for structuring a worker cooperative through the operating agreement. Under Arkansas Code Section 4-38-105, the operating agreement can govern most aspects of the LLC's internal affairs, including voting rights and profit distribution.

**Key Operating Agreement Provisions for Cooperative LLCs:**

1. **One-Member-One-Vote:** Arkansas LLC law permits flexible voting structures. The operating agreement can explicitly establish democratic voting where each member has one vote regardless of capital contribution.

2. **Patronage-Based Distributions:** The operating agreement can specify that profits are distributed based on labor contribution (patronage) rather than capital investment.

3. **Membership Classes:** Can define worker-member class with specific rights, obligations, and eligibility requirements.

4. **Buyback Provisions:** Include terms for redeeming member interests upon departure.

**Advantages:**
- Pass-through taxation (avoids double taxation)
- Highly flexible governance structure
- Less formal requirements than corporations
- Lower formation costs ($45 online)
- Operating agreement can be customized to achieve cooperative principles
- No Arkansas requirement to have operating agreement on file with state

**Considerations:**
- Cannot use "cooperative" or "co-op" in entity name (reserved for entities formed under cooperative statute)
- Must carefully draft operating agreement to achieve cooperative principles
- No dedicated statutory guidance for cooperative-specific issues

**Filing Information:**
- **Online Filing Fee:** $45
- **Paper Filing Fee:** $50
- **Annual Franchise Tax:** $150 (due May 1)

---

### Option B: Cooperative Association (Arkansas Code Title 4, Chapter 30)

**Statutory Authority:** [Arkansas Code Title 4, Subtitle 3, Chapter 30 - Cooperative Associations](https://law.justia.com/codes/arkansas/title-4/subtitle-3/chapter-30/)

Arkansas's general cooperative statute allows any number of persons, corporations, or entities to associate themselves together as a cooperative corporation for conducting business "on the cooperative plan."

**Key Provisions:**

**Formation (Section 4-30-107):**
Cooperatives may be formed for the purpose of conducting any agricultural, dairy, mercantile, banking, mining, manufacturing, or mechanical business on the cooperative plan.

**Corporate Name (Section 4-30-106):**
The name may begin with "The" and shall end with "Association," "Company," "Corporation," "Exchange," "Society," "Union," or "Incorporated" (or "Inc.").

**Bylaws (Section 4-30-109):**
Any association formed under this chapter may pass bylaws to govern itself in carrying out the provisions of the chapter, provided they are not inconsistent with the statute.

**Stock Ownership and Voting (Section 4-30-112):**
This section addresses percentage limitations on stock ownership and voting by members, supporting democratic cooperative principles.

**Organization of Other Entities (Section 4-30-116):**
An association may organize, form, operate, own, control, have an interest in, own stock of, or be a member of any other corporation or association engaged in authorized activities. This permits federation of cooperative business enterprises in Arkansas.

**Advantages:**
- Specifically designed for cooperative business structure
- May use cooperative-related terminology in entity name
- Statutory framework for cooperative operations
- Recognized cooperative legal form

**Considerations:**
- More formal corporate requirements
- Less flexibility than LLC structure
- Statute primarily oriented toward agricultural cooperatives
- May require more customization for worker cooperative purposes

**Filing Information:**
- Contact Arkansas Secretary of State for current cooperative corporation filing fees
- Forms available at [sos.arkansas.gov](https://www.sos.arkansas.gov/business-commercial-services-bcs/forms-fees/cooperative)

---

### Option C: Standard Corporation with Cooperative Bylaws

A traditional business corporation (C-Corp or S-Corp) can adopt bylaws that incorporate cooperative principles, though this approach requires more customization.

**Statutory Authority:** [Arkansas Code Title 4, Chapter 27 - Arkansas Business Corporation Act](https://law.justia.com/codes/arkansas/title-4/subtitle-3/chapter-27/)

**When this might be appropriate:**
- Planning to seek outside investment in the future
- Desire for well-established corporate law precedents
- Specific industry requirements favor corporate structure
- Planning for Section 1042 rollover eligibility (for C-Corps)

**Filing Information:**
- **Online Filing Fee:** $45
- **Paper Filing Fee:** $50
- **Annual Franchise Tax:** 0.3% of outstanding capital stock or $150, whichever is greater

---

### Worker Coop-Friendly Provisions Worth Noting

1. **Section 1042 Rollover Eligibility:** Selling business owners may defer capital gains when selling 30% or more of stock to a qualified worker cooperative (C-Corp structure required).

2. **Flexible LLC Statute:** Arkansas Code Section 4-38-105 provides substantial freedom to structure voting rights and distributions through the operating agreement.

3. **Manufacturing/Mechanical Business Authorization:** The cooperative statute's inclusion of "manufacturing" and "mechanical" business purposes provides a pathway for non-agricultural worker cooperatives.

4. **Federation Permitted:** Section 4-30-116 explicitly permits cooperatives to organize and participate in other cooperative associations.

## 4. Governance and Documents You Should Prepare

### Operating Agreement (for LLCs) or Bylaws (for Corporations/Cooperatives)

These governing documents are critical for establishing your cooperative's democratic structure. While not filed with the state, they govern internal operations.

**Essential Provisions for Worker Cooperatives:**

#### Membership Classes and Rights
- **Worker-Member Class:** Define eligibility (e.g., minimum hours worked, probationary period)
- **Membership Share:** Amount and terms of initial capital contribution
- **Membership Fee:** Consideration paid for membership share (can be paid over time)
- **Candidate/Probationary Period:** Trial period before full membership

#### Voting Structure
- **One-Member-One-Vote:** Explicitly state that voting power is not proportional to capital contribution
- **Quorum Requirements:** Minimum participation for valid meetings
- **Supermajority Requirements:** Specify decisions requiring more than simple majority (e.g., amendments, dissolution, major asset sales)
- **Proxy Voting:** Whether permitted and under what circumstances
- **Consensus or Modified Consensus:** Alternative decision-making processes if desired

#### Patronage and Profit Distribution
- **Patronage Definition:** How member labor contribution is measured (hours worked, compensation earned, or other metric)
- **Allocation Formula:** How net earnings are divided between:
  - Patronage distributions to members
  - Collective reserve account
  - Retained earnings for operations
- **Written Notice of Allocation:** Procedure for documenting patronage credits
- **Distribution Timing:** When and how distributions are paid (cash, equity credits, or combination)
- **Minimum Retention:** Percentage of net income retained for business operations

#### Internal Capital Accounts
- **Individual Member Accounts:** Track each member's equity stake
- **Collective Reserve Account:** Undivided surplus belonging to cooperative as a whole
- **Interest on Capital:** Whether accounts earn interest
- **Redemption Terms:** How accounts are paid out upon member departure or termination

#### Member Departure and Buyback
- **Voluntary Withdrawal:** Notice requirements and equity redemption timeline
- **Involuntary Termination:** Grounds and process for removing members
- **Death or Disability:** Treatment of membership shares
- **Redemption Schedule:** Timeline for paying out departing member's capital account (often 3-5 years to protect cooperative cash flow)
- **Non-Compete/Non-Solicitation:** Provisions if appropriate for your industry

#### Board of Directors (if applicable)
- **Composition:** Number of directors, term lengths
- **Election Process:** How directors are elected by members
- **Officer Roles:** President, Secretary, Treasurer duties
- **Committees:** Standing committees and their authorities
- **Term Limits:** Whether to limit consecutive terms

#### Meetings
- **Annual Member Meeting:** Required timing and notice
- **Special Meetings:** How called, notice requirements
- **Board Meetings:** Frequency and procedures
- **Record Keeping:** Minutes and document retention
- **Remote Participation:** Rules for virtual meeting attendance

### Additional Documents to Prepare

1. **Member Handbook:** Policies, procedures, expectations
2. **Membership Agreement:** Contract signed by each member
3. **Patronage Policy:** Detailed explanation of how patronage is calculated
4. **Conflict of Interest Policy:** Important for democratic organizations
5. **Buy-Sell Agreement:** Terms for member share transfers
6. **Employment Policies:** Separate from membership (workers can be employees before becoming members)

### Sample Resources

- [Democracy at Work Institute - Resources](https://institute.coop/resources)
- [USFWC Resources Page](https://www.usworker.coop/resources/)
- [Sustainable Economies Law Center](https://www.theselc.org/)

**Recommendation:** Consult with an attorney experienced in cooperative law to customize these documents for your specific situation.

## 6. Worker-Ownership Conversions

### Converting an Existing Business to Worker Ownership

Business succession through worker ownership is an increasingly popular option, particularly for owners without family successors or those committed to preserving jobs and business legacy.

### Two Primary Paths: Worker Cooperative vs. ESOP

| Factor | Worker Cooperative | ESOP |
|--------|-------------------|------|
| **Best Fit** | Smaller businesses (<100 employees, $500K-$10M revenue) | Larger businesses (typically 20+ employees, $1M+ payroll) |
| **Setup Cost** | Lower ($10,000-$50,000 typical) | Higher ($50,000-$150,000+) |
| **Ongoing Cost** | Lower (no annual valuations required) | Higher (annual appraisals, trustee fees, administration) |
| **Governance** | One-member-one-vote | Voting typically proportional to shares |
| **Tax Treatment** | Pass-through (if structured as LLC or S-Corp) | C-Corp can be 100% tax-exempt; S-Corp has limits |
| **Section 1042 Rollover** | Available for C-Corps | Available for C-Corps |
| **Regulation** | Minimal federal regulation | Heavy ERISA/DOL regulation |
| **Democratic Control** | Built into structure | Optional, requires specific plan design |

### Section 1042 Rollover for Both Models

Selling owners of C-corporations can defer capital gains taxes when selling 30% or more of company stock to either:
- An ESOP, or
- A qualified worker cooperative

The seller must reinvest proceeds in qualified replacement property (domestic securities) within the required timeframe.

### Typical Conversion Process for Worker Cooperative in Arkansas

1. **Feasibility Assessment**
   - Business valuation by qualified appraiser
   - Financial analysis and due diligence
   - Worker interest and capacity assessment
   - Legal structure evaluation

2. **Transition Planning**
   - Timeline development (typically 1-3 years)
   - Financing structure design
   - Leadership succession plan
   - Communication strategy for workers

3. **Legal Structure**
   - Determine entity type (typically LLC conversion or new LLC)
   - Negotiate purchase agreement with selling owner(s)
   - Draft cooperative operating agreement/bylaws
   - Address any outstanding liabilities

4. **Financing the Buyout**
   - **Seller financing:** Most common (seller carries note over time)
   - **Bank loans:** SBA 7(a) loans can finance cooperative conversions
   - **CDFI loans:** Shared Capital Cooperative, Seed Commons network
   - **Worker capital contributions:** Initial member buy-in
   - **Combination approaches:** Often most practical

5. **Implementation**
   - Governance training for new worker-owners
   - Operational transition and management succession
   - Ongoing technical assistance and support
   - Cultural development as cooperative

### Arkansas-Specific Considerations

- **LLC Conversion:** An existing LLC can amend its operating agreement to incorporate cooperative principles without changing entity type
- **Corporation to LLC:** Arkansas permits conversion between entity types, allowing a corporation to become an LLC (consult attorney)
- **Cooperative Association:** Traditional corporations can convert to cooperative association status under Chapter 30 if meeting the statutory requirements
- **Asset Sale Alternative:** Rather than converting the existing entity, workers may form a new cooperative that purchases the business assets

### Resources for Conversions

- [Project Equity](https://project-equity.org/) - National organization focused on business conversions
- [Ohio Employee Ownership Center](https://www.oeockent.org/cooperatives/selling-your-business-to-a-worker-owned-cooperative) - Resources for selling to employees
- [Democracy at Work Institute - Conversions](https://institute.coop/topics/esops)
- [Becoming Employee Owned Coalition](https://becomingemployeeowned.org/)

**Recommendation:** Engage professionals experienced in cooperative conversions early in the process. The Arkansas SBDC can provide referrals to appropriate advisors.

## 7. Common Pitfalls in Arkansas

### 1. Assuming Arkansas Has a Worker Cooperative-Specific Statute

Arkansas's cooperative statute (Chapter 30) is a general cooperative law primarily oriented toward agricultural and traditional cooperatives. It does not have provisions specifically designed for worker cooperatives like some other states.

**Solution:** For most worker cooperatives, forming an LLC with a carefully drafted cooperative operating agreement provides more flexibility and clearer guidance. Consult with an attorney familiar with cooperative structures.

---

### 2. Inadequate Operating Agreement

Using generic LLC operating agreement templates without customization for cooperative principles can create legal and operational problems.

**Solution:** Work with an attorney familiar with cooperative law to draft or review governing documents. Key areas often overlooked:
- One-member-one-vote provisions
- Patronage calculation methodology
- Member buyout terms and timeline
- Dispute resolution procedures
- Capital account provisions

---

### 3. Missing the Annual Franchise Tax Deadline

Arkansas requires an annual franchise tax payment of $150 for LLCs, due May 1. Failure to pay results in penalties, interest, and potential administrative dissolution.

**Solution:**
- Mark May 1 on your calendar annually
- Set up reminders at least 30 days before deadline
- File through [Arkansas Secretary of State Franchise Tax Portal](https://www.ark.org/sos/franchise/index.php)

---

### 4. Inadequate Capitalization

Worker cooperatives often start undercapitalized because workers have limited personal wealth to invest.

**Solution:**
- Develop realistic capital requirements before launch
- Consider phased membership buy-in over time
- Build collective reserves from retained earnings
- Explore external financing options early (Shared Capital Cooperative, Seed Commons network)
- Consider seller financing for business conversions

---

### 5. Conflating Ownership with Day-to-Day Management

New worker cooperatives often assume all decisions must be made collectively, leading to inefficiency and frustration.

**Solution:** Clearly define in your operating agreement:
- Which decisions require member vote (strategic/structural)
- Which decisions are delegated to managers or committees (operational)
- Decision-making levels and thresholds
- Management authority and accountability

---

### 6. Neglecting Local Business License Requirements

Arkansas has no statewide general business license, so entrepreneurs sometimes assume no license is needed. Cities and counties have their own requirements.

**Solution:**
- Contact your city clerk and county government before opening
- Research industry-specific licensing requirements
- Budget for license fees in startup costs
- Renew licenses on time

---

### 7. Not Maintaining Registered Agent

Failure to maintain a registered agent at a valid Arkansas address can result in missed legal notices and potential loss of good standing.

**Solution:**
- Ensure continuous registered agent coverage
- Update Secretary of State if agent changes (no fee required)
- Consider professional registered agent service for reliability, especially if no member has a stable Arkansas business address

---

### 8. Treating "Cooperative" as the Value Proposition

Being a cooperative is a business structure, not a product or service. Customers typically buy based on value, quality, and price.

**Solution:** Develop a strong business model independent of cooperative structure. Let your governance model inform your values, but compete on business fundamentals.

## 8. FAQs for Arkansas

### Q1: Does Arkansas have a specific law for worker cooperatives?

**Not specifically.** Arkansas has a general cooperative association statute (Arkansas Code Title 4, Chapter 30) that permits cooperatives for agricultural, mercantile, manufacturing, or mechanical businesses. While worker cooperatives can potentially use this statute, it was not designed with worker ownership specifically in mind. Most worker cooperatives in Arkansas form as LLCs with cooperative operating agreements, which provides greater flexibility.

---

### Q2: Can I form a worker cooperative as an LLC in Arkansas?

**Yes.** This is the most common approach for worker cooperatives in Arkansas. Arkansas's LLC statute (Chapter 38) provides flexibility to establish cooperative governance through the operating agreement, including one-member-one-vote provisions and patronage-based profit distribution. The LLC structure offers pass-through taxation and lower formality requirements.

---

### Q4: What is the annual franchise tax for Arkansas cooperatives?

For LLCs (including those operating as worker cooperatives), the annual franchise tax is a flat **$150**, due by May 1 each year. For corporations with stock, the tax is 0.3% of outstanding capital stock or $150, whichever is greater. Corporations without authorized stock pay $300. Failure to pay results in penalties and potential revocation of authority to conduct business.

### Q5: What resources exist for cooperative development in Arkansas?

- **Arkansas SBDC Network:** Free business advising through university-based centers statewide

### Q6: Can I use "cooperative" or "co-op" in my LLC name?

**Generally, no.** In Arkansas, the terms suggesting cooperative status are typically reserved for entities formed under the cooperative association statute (Chapter 30). An LLC operating with cooperative principles would typically not include "cooperative" in its legal name but could use such terms in marketing materials with appropriate disclaimers. Consult with an attorney for specific guidance.

---

### Q7: How do I register for state taxes in Arkansas?

Register through the [Arkansas Taxpayer Access Point (ATAP)](https://atap.arkansas.gov/) portal. You will need your federal EIN. If selling taxable goods or services, you must obtain a sales tax permit ($50 fee). If hiring employees, register for withholding tax. Allow up to 2 weeks for processing. Contact Arkansas DFA at (501) 682-7290 with questions.

### Q8: Can I convert my existing Arkansas business to a worker cooperative?

**Yes.** Common approaches include:
- **LLC:** Amend the operating agreement to incorporate cooperative principles (one-member-one-vote, patronage distribution, etc.)
- **Corporation:** Convert to LLC then adopt cooperative operating agreement, or amend bylaws for cooperative governance
- **Asset Purchase:** Workers form new cooperative LLC that purchases the business assets

The process typically involves business valuation, financing the ownership transition, and restructuring governance documents. Seller financing is common. The Section 1042 capital gains rollover may be available for C-corporation sales.
