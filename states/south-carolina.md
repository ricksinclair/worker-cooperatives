# How to Start a Worker Cooperative in South Carolina

## Quick Summary

South Carolina offers a business-friendly environment for worker cooperatives, combining low operating costs, minimal regulatory burdens, and a growing support ecosystem. The Palmetto State features:

- **No state corporate income tax on S-corporations** - LLCs and cooperatives structured as pass-through entities avoid double taxation
- **Low cost of living and doing business** - Among the lowest in the Southeast, reducing startup overhead
- **Strong agricultural cooperative tradition** - Deep roots in cooperative development, particularly through the [Federation of Southern Cooperatives](https://www.federation.coop/)
- **Dedicated cooperative support** - The [SC Center for Cooperative and Enterprise Development](https://www.clemson.edu/extension/agribusiness/sccced/index.html) at Clemson provides specialized technical assistance
- **No statewide business license requirement** - Licensing handled at the local level, offering flexibility
- **Fast online filing** - Secretary of State processes online business formations within 24 hours

South Carolina recognizes cooperative associations under [Title 33, Chapter 45](https://www.scstatehouse.gov/code/t33c045.php) of the South Carolina Code, though most worker cooperatives form as LLCs or corporations with cooperative operating structures. The state's position in the Southeast provides access to regional cooperative networks and financing resources specifically designed for underserved communities.

## Best-Fit Legal Structures for Worker Cooperatives in South Carolina

South Carolina offers several legal pathways for forming a worker cooperative. Each structure has distinct advantages depending on your cooperative's size, industry, and governance preferences.

### Option 1: Limited Liability Company (LLC) with Cooperative Operating Agreement

**The most popular choice for worker cooperatives in South Carolina.**

An LLC provides maximum flexibility to structure democratic governance and worker ownership through a custom operating agreement. South Carolina's [LLC statutes (Title 33, Chapter 44)](https://www.scstatehouse.gov/code/title33.php) allow members to define:

- One-member-one-vote governance
- Patronage-based profit distribution
- Member capital accounts
- Democratic decision-making processes

**Advantages:**
- Flexible management structure (member-managed or manager-managed)
- Pass-through taxation avoids double taxation
- Limited liability protection for all members
- No minimum capital requirements
- Operating agreement can be customized for cooperative principles

**Considerations:**
- Requires a well-drafted operating agreement to ensure cooperative character
- Name cannot contain words like "cooperative" or "credit union" without special permits per [SC LLC naming rules](https://www.wolterskluwer.com/en/solutions/bizfilings/state-guides/south-carolina-llc-requirements)
- Members must consent to ownership transfers

**Filing Requirements:**
- File Articles of Organization with the [SC Secretary of State](https://businessfilings.sc.gov/)
- Filing fee: $110
- Must designate a registered agent in South Carolina

### Option 2: Cooperative Association under SC Code Title 33, Chapter 45

South Carolina has a [cooperative association statute](https://www.scstatehouse.gov/code/t33c045.php) that provides a framework for forming cooperatives. Key provisions include:

**Key Statutory Features:**
- **Section 33-45-30**: Authorizes formation of cooperative associations
- **Section 33-45-40**: Requires filing petition with Secretary of State
- **Section 33-45-90**: Limits voting power and stock ownership of single stockholders (democratic control principle)
- **Section 33-45-110**: Allows democratic management and removal of directors/officers
- **Section 33-45-180**: Provides for apportionment of earnings (patronage dividends)
- **Section 33-45-190**: Requires annual report to Commissioner of Agriculture

**Advantages:**
- Explicitly designed for cooperative ownership
- Built-in one-member-one-vote provisions
- Patronage dividend distribution is statutorily recognized
- Clear legal identity as a cooperative

**Considerations:**
- Less commonly used for worker cooperatives (statute was designed primarily for agricultural cooperatives)
- More rigid structure than LLCs
- Annual reporting requirement to Commissioner of Agriculture
- May require more specialized legal guidance

### Option 3: Corporation with Cooperative Bylaws

A standard South Carolina corporation can be structured with bylaws that implement cooperative principles. This approach uses the [Business Corporation Act (Title 33)](https://www.scstatehouse.gov/code/title33.php).

**Advantages:**
- Well-understood corporate form
- Clear governance hierarchy
- Can issue different classes of stock for members vs. investors
- Easier to attract outside investment if needed

**Considerations:**
- More complex formation requirements
- Potential for double taxation (unless S-corp election is made)
- Must file Initial Annual Report (CL-1) within 60 days
- Corporation filing fee: $135 (includes $110 + $25 CL-1 fee)

### Option 4: Nonprofit Corporation with Worker-Member Structure

For mission-driven cooperatives where profit distribution is secondary to social impact, a nonprofit structure under [Title 33, Chapter 31](https://www.scstatehouse.gov/code/title33.php) may be appropriate.

**Advantages:**
- Potential for 501(c)(3) or 501(c)(6) tax exemption
- Access to grant funding
- Clear mission focus

**Considerations:**
- Cannot distribute profits to members
- More regulatory oversight
- Compensation must be "reasonable"
- Best suited for cooperatives with strong charitable or educational missions

### Recommended Structure for Most Worker Cooperatives

**The LLC with a cooperative operating agreement** is typically the best fit for South Carolina worker cooperatives because it offers:
- Maximum flexibility in governance design
- Pass-through taxation
- Strong liability protection
- Ability to implement all seven cooperative principles
- Lower ongoing compliance requirements

Resources for drafting cooperative operating agreements:
- [Democracy at Work Institute Templates](https://institute.coop/bylaw-and-operating-agreement-templates)
- [Sustainable Economies Law Center Templates](https://www.theselc.org/templates)

## Step-by-Step Formation Checklist

### Phase 1: Planning and Preparation (Weeks 1-4)

#### Step 1: Assemble Your Founding Team
- [ ] Identify 3-5 committed founding worker-members
- [ ] Discuss shared vision, values, and business concept
- [ ] Determine each member's role and contribution (capital, labor, skills)
- [ ] Agree on decision-making processes for the formation period

#### Step 2: Conduct Feasibility Analysis
- [ ] Research your industry and market in South Carolina
- [ ] Identify potential customers and competition
- [ ] Estimate startup costs and ongoing expenses
- [ ] Project revenue and break-even timeline
- [ ] Contact [SC SBDC](https://www.scsbdc.com/) for free business consulting

#### Step 3: Choose Your Business Name
- [ ] Brainstorm names that reflect your cooperative identity
- [ ] Check name availability using the [SC Secretary of State Business Entity Search](https://businessfilings.sc.gov/)
- [ ] Ensure name ends with required LLC designator (LLC, L.L.C., Limited Liability Company)
- [ ] Note: Cannot use "cooperative," "bank," "trust," or "credit union" in LLC names without special authorization
- [ ] Consider reserving the name ($25 fee, valid 120 days)

#### Step 4: Select Your Legal Structure
- [ ] Review the structure options outlined above
- [ ] Consult with an attorney familiar with cooperative law
- [ ] Decide between LLC, cooperative corporation, or standard corporation
- [ ] Document your decision and reasoning

### Phase 2: Legal Formation (Weeks 4-6)

#### Step 5: Designate a Registered Agent
- [ ] Choose a registered agent with a physical address in South Carolina
- [ ] Agent must be available during business hours to receive legal documents
- [ ] Options: founding member, attorney, or commercial registered agent service
- [ ] Document agent's name and address for filing

#### Step 6: File Formation Documents with the SC Secretary of State

**For LLC Formation:**
- [ ] Complete Articles of Organization per [SC Code 33-44-202 and 33-44-203](https://www.scstatehouse.gov/code/title33.php)
- [ ] Submit online via [Business Entities Online](https://businessfilings.sc.gov/) (recommended - processed within 24 hours)
- [ ] Or mail to: SC Secretary of State, 1205 Pendleton Street, Suite 525, Columbia, SC 29201
- [ ] Pay filing fee: **$110**

**Required Information for Articles of Organization:**
- LLC name (with proper designator)
- Registered agent name and SC address
- Principal office address
- Management structure (member-managed recommended for cooperatives)
- Organizer name, address, and signature
- Duration (perpetual or specific term)

**For Cooperative Corporation (Title 33, Chapter 45):**
- [ ] File petition with Secretary of State per Section 33-45-40
- [ ] Include articles of incorporation with cooperative provisions
- [ ] Pay applicable filing fees

#### Step 7: Obtain Federal Employer Identification Number (EIN)
- [ ] Apply online at [IRS EIN Application](https://www.irs.gov/businesses/small-businesses-self-employed/apply-for-an-employer-identification-number-ein-online)
- [ ] Immediate issuance for online applications
- [ ] Required for: opening bank accounts, hiring employees, filing taxes
- [ ] Free of charge

#### Step 8: Register with the SC Department of Revenue
- [ ] Complete the [Business Tax Application (SCDOR-111)](https://dor.sc.gov/tax/registration)
- [ ] Register for applicable taxes:
  - [ ] **Employer Withholding Tax** - Required if you have employees
  - [ ] **Sales Tax (Retail License)** - Required if selling taxable goods/services (no cost)
  - [ ] **Corporate Income Tax** - If structured as C-corporation
- [ ] Contact: [SC Department of Revenue](https://dor.sc.gov/), 803-896-1350

**Important for Corporations:**
- File Initial Annual Report (CL-1) and pay $25 license fee within 60 days of formation
- Can pay to Secretary of State or SCDOR

### Phase 3: Local Compliance (Weeks 6-8)

#### Step 9: Obtain Local Business License
- [ ] Note: South Carolina has **no statewide business license** requirement
- [ ] Determine your business location's jurisdiction
- [ ] Use [Municipal Association of South Carolina](https://scbos.sc.gov/business-support/business-resources/state-resources) to find local requirements
- [ ] Contact your city and/or county for specific licensing requirements
- [ ] Apply for local business license (fees vary by jurisdiction and revenue)

**Major Cities - License Information:**
- [Charleston](https://www.charleston-sc.gov/134/Business-License-Information)
- [Columbia](https://businesslicensing.columbiasc.gov/new-business-license/)
- Greenville, Spartanburg, and other cities have their own requirements

#### Step 10: Check Industry-Specific Permits and Licenses
- [ ] Use [SC Business One Stop](https://scbos.sc.gov/business-compliance/licenses) to identify required licenses
- [ ] Common industry requirements:
  - Food service: DHEC permits, food handler certifications
  - Construction: contractor licensing
  - Childcare: DSS licensing
  - Professional services: board certifications
- [ ] Apply for all necessary permits before commencing operations

### Phase 4: Governance Setup (Weeks 6-10)

#### Step 11: Draft Operating Agreement or Bylaws
- [ ] Work with an attorney experienced in cooperative law
- [ ] Include essential cooperative provisions:
  - [ ] Membership eligibility and requirements
  - [ ] One-member-one-vote governance
  - [ ] Patronage-based profit allocation
  - [ ] Member capital accounts
  - [ ] Decision-making processes (consensus, supermajority, etc.)
  - [ ] Board of directors structure and elections
  - [ ] Officer roles and responsibilities
  - [ ] Meeting requirements
  - [ ] Member discipline and termination procedures
  - [ ] Dissolution and distribution of assets

**Template Resources:**
- [Democracy at Work Institute Bylaws and Operating Agreement Templates](https://institute.coop/bylaw-and-operating-agreement-templates)
- [SELC Worker Cooperative Templates](https://www.theselc.org/templates)
- [ICA Model Bylaws for Worker Cooperatives](https://institute.coop/sites/default/files/ica-model-bylaws.pdf)

#### Step 12: Hold Organizational Meeting
- [ ] All founding members attend (in person or virtually)
- [ ] Adopt operating agreement or bylaws
- [ ] Elect initial board of directors (if applicable)
- [ ] Appoint officers
- [ ] Authorize opening of bank accounts
- [ ] Set initial capital contribution requirements
- [ ] Document all decisions in meeting minutes

#### Step 13: Issue Membership Interests
- [ ] Determine initial capital contribution requirements
- [ ] Create membership certificates or records
- [ ] Establish capital account tracking system
- [ ] Collect initial member capital contributions
- [ ] Document in membership ledger

### Phase 5: Operations Setup (Weeks 8-12)

#### Step 14: Set Up Financial Infrastructure
- [ ] Open business bank account (bring EIN, Articles, Operating Agreement)
- [ ] Establish bookkeeping system with member capital account tracking
- [ ] Set up payroll system for worker-members
- [ ] Create chart of accounts appropriate for cooperatives
- [ ] Consider cooperative-specific accounting software

#### Step 15: Establish Employment Policies
- [ ] Draft worker-member handbook
- [ ] Establish compensation structure
- [ ] Create pathways from employee to member status
- [ ] Set up workers' compensation insurance (required in SC for 4+ employees)
- [ ] Develop performance evaluation processes

#### Step 16: Finalize Operational Systems
- [ ] Secure business location and necessary equipment
- [ ] Set up communication and project management tools
- [ ] Create customer/client management systems
- [ ] Develop marketing and outreach materials
- [ ] Establish supplier relationships

## Governance and Documents You Should Prepare

### Essential Governing Documents

#### 1. Articles of Organization (LLC) or Articles of Incorporation
The foundational legal document filed with the Secretary of State. Should include:
- Entity name and type
- Registered agent information
- Principal office address
- Purpose (can be general)
- Duration
- Management structure
- Organizer information

#### 2. Operating Agreement (LLC) or Bylaws (Corporation)
The internal governance document that defines how your cooperative operates. Critical sections include:

**Membership:**
- Eligibility requirements (employment hours, probationary period)
- Application and approval process
- Member capital contribution requirements
- Rights and responsibilities of members
- Voluntary withdrawal procedures
- Involuntary termination grounds and process

**Governance:**
- Meeting requirements (annual, regular, special)
- Quorum requirements
- Voting procedures (one member, one vote)
- Board of directors (if any): size, terms, election process
- Officer positions and duties
- Committees and their authority

**Financial:**
- Fiscal year
- Capital accounts and internal capital
- Patronage allocation formula
- Distribution of net income
- Handling of losses
- Member loans and preferred shares (if any)

**Amendments and Dissolution:**
- Amendment procedures
- Dissolution triggers
- Distribution of assets upon dissolution

#### 3. Membership Agreement
Individual agreement signed by each member covering:
- Commitment to cooperative principles
- Capital contribution obligations
- Work responsibilities
- Confidentiality provisions
- Non-compete clauses (if applicable)
- Acknowledgment of operating agreement

#### 4. Member Handbook
Operational policies and procedures including:
- Work schedules and expectations
- Compensation and benefits structure
- Leave policies
- Grievance procedures
- Health and safety policies
- Professional development opportunities
- Pathway from employee to member

### Financial Documents

#### 5. Patronage Allocation Plan
Document explaining how surplus is allocated to member accounts, including:
- Definition of patronage (hours worked, revenue generated, etc.)
- Allocation formula
- Retained vs. distributed percentages
- Tax treatment (Subchapter T considerations)

#### 6. Capital Account Policy
Rules governing member capital accounts:
- Initial contribution requirements
- Retained patronage allocations
- Interest on member accounts
- Redemption schedule upon departure
- Order of priority for payouts

#### 7. Annual Budget Template
Framework for democratic financial planning:
- Revenue projections
- Operating expenses
- Member compensation
- Capital expenditures
- Patronage distribution plans
- Reserve requirements

### Meeting and Decision-Making Documents

#### 8. Meeting Minutes Template
Standardized format for documenting decisions:
- Date, time, location
- Members present/absent
- Agenda items
- Motions and votes
- Action items assigned

#### 9. Consent Resolution Template
For decisions made outside of meetings:
- Written action without meeting
- Signature blocks for all voting members
- Effective date

#### 10. Member Vote Record
Tracking system for important decisions:
- Issue description
- Voting results by member
- Date and threshold required

## Worker-Ownership Conversions

Converting an existing South Carolina business to worker ownership is an increasingly popular succession strategy. Here's how to approach a conversion in South Carolina.

### Why Consider Conversion?

**For Selling Owners:**
- Preserve the business legacy
- Reward loyal employees
- Achieve fair market value for the business
- Potential tax advantages (depending on structure)
- Community-minded exit

**For Workers:**
- Job security and stability
- Wealth-building opportunity
- Democratic workplace
- Shared success and accountability

### Conversion Readiness Assessment

**Business Health Indicators:**
- Minimal existing debt
- Strong financial performance over past 3-5 years
- Positive future outlook
- Stable customer/client base
- Documented systems and procedures
- Management capacity beyond the owner

**Workforce Readiness:**
- Employees interested in ownership
- Management team capable of leading
- Skills and knowledge to run the business
- Willingness to participate in governance
- Financial capacity for buy-in (or willingness to build it)

### South Carolina Conversion Process

#### Phase 1: Exploration (2-3 months)

**Step 1: Owner Decision**
- Research worker cooperative conversion
- Assess personal readiness to sell
- Consider timeline for transition
- Evaluate emotional attachment to business

**Step 2: Initial Employee Communication**
- Introduce the concept of worker ownership
- Gauge employee interest (without pressure)
- Address initial questions and concerns

**Step 3: Engage Professional Support**
- Hire attorney experienced in cooperative conversions
- Engage business valuator
- Consider cooperative development organization

**Resources:**
- [Democracy at Work Institute Conversion Guide](https://institute.coop/tools/for-worker-coops/conversions)
- [Project Equity](https://project-equity.org/)
- [Southeast Center for Cooperative Development Conversion Program](https://www.co-opsnow.org/our-programs)

#### Phase 2: Feasibility and Planning (3-6 months)

**Step 4: Business Valuation**
Obtain professional valuation considering:
- Asset value
- Earnings history
- Market conditions
- Growth potential
- Intangible assets (customer relationships, reputation)

**Step 5: Feasibility Analysis**
Determine if conversion is financially viable:
- Can the cooperative service acquisition debt?
- What is the realistic timeline for buyout?
- What financing structures are possible?

**Step 6: Form Steering Committee**
Select 3-5 employees to:
- Represent worker interests in negotiations
- Learn about cooperative governance
- Communicate with workforce
- Make recommendations on structure

**Step 7: Structure Design**
Decide on:
- Legal entity type (usually LLC conversion)
- Governance structure
- Membership criteria and buy-in
- Transition timeline
- Owner's ongoing role (if any)

#### Phase 3: Legal and Financial Structuring (3-6 months)

**Step 8: Entity Conversion or Formation**
South Carolina allows [entity conversions](https://www.harborcompliance.com/south-carolina-entity-conversion) between business forms.

**LLC to Cooperative LLC:**
- Amend operating agreement to add cooperative provisions
- No new entity filing required if staying as LLC

**Corporation to Cooperative:**
- May convert to LLC with cooperative operating agreement
- Or amend corporate bylaws with cooperative provisions
- Conversion forms available from [SC Secretary of State](https://businessfilings.sc.gov/BusinessFiling/Home/DownloadForms?pdfCategoryId=11)

**Step 9: Financing the Buyout**
Common financing approaches:
- **Seller financing**: Owner carries note, paid over time from business profits
- **Bank loans**: Traditional or SBA-backed acquisition financing
- **CDFI loans**: Shared Capital Cooperative, CLIMB Fund, others
- **Hybrid approach**: Combination of above

**Step 10: Draft Governing Documents**
Create cooperative operating agreement or bylaws with:
- Membership provisions
- Governance structure
- Profit-sharing (patronage) provisions
- Buy-in requirements
- Capital account policies

#### Phase 4: Transition and Closing (2-4 months)

**Step 11: Employee Education and Decision**
- Conduct Worker Cooperative 101 training
- Share final terms with all employees
- Allow adequate time for decision-making
- Hold vote on conversion (if required)

**Step 12: Close the Transaction**
- Execute purchase agreement
- Transfer ownership interests
- File any required amendments with Secretary of State
- Update registrations with SCDOR
- Notify banks and vendors

**Step 13: Launch Worker Ownership**
- Hold first member meeting
- Elect board of directors
- Establish governance rhythms
- Begin open-book management practices
- Celebrate the transition!

## Common Pitfalls in South Carolina

### Legal and Formation Pitfalls

#### 1. Using "Cooperative" in LLC Name Without Authorization
**The Problem:** South Carolina LLC naming rules [prohibit](https://www.wolterskluwer.com/en/solutions/bizfilings/state-guides/south-carolina-llc-requirements) using words like "cooperative," "bank," "trust," or "credit union" without special permits.

**The Solution:**
- Choose alternative naming (e.g., "Worker-Owned," "Collective," or simply your trade name)
- If you want "cooperative" in your name, consider forming under Title 33, Chapter 45 cooperative statute
- Consult with Secretary of State about permit requirements if needed

#### 2. Inadequate Operating Agreement
**The Problem:** Using generic LLC operating agreements that don't include cooperative provisions.

**The Solution:**
- Use templates specifically designed for worker cooperatives
- Work with attorney familiar with cooperative law
- Include all essential cooperative elements: one-member-one-vote, patronage allocation, member capital accounts

#### 3. Failing to Distinguish Members from Employees
**The Problem:** Treating all workers as members from day one, or never providing a clear path to membership.

**The Solution:**
- Establish clear membership eligibility criteria (hours worked, probationary period)
- Create documented pathway from employee to member
- Distinguish between employee rights and member rights

### Tax and Financial Pitfalls

#### 4. Ignoring Subchapter T Considerations
**The Problem:** Not understanding how cooperative taxation works under IRS Subchapter T.

**The Solution:**
- Work with accountant experienced in cooperative taxation
- Properly document patronage allocations
- Understand the difference between qualified and non-qualified patronage
- Maintain clear capital account records

#### 5. Misclassifying Workers
**The Problem:** Treating worker-members as independent contractors to avoid employment taxes.

**The Solution:**
- Most worker-members are employees for tax purposes
- Consult with employment attorney and accountant
- Properly withhold employment taxes
- Provide W-2s, not 1099s, for regular worker-members

#### 6. Inadequate Capital Structure
**The Problem:** Launching without sufficient capital or a plan for ongoing capitalization.

**The Solution:**
- Conduct thorough startup cost analysis
- Establish realistic member buy-in requirements
- Create retained patronage policy to build internal capital
- Plan for capital needs before they become urgent

### Governance Pitfalls

#### 7. Tyranny of Structurelessness
**The Problem:** Avoiding formal governance structures in favor of informal decision-making, leading to hidden power dynamics.

**The Solution:**
- Establish clear decision-making procedures from the start
- Define which decisions require votes
- Create transparent processes for different decision types
- Document decisions consistently

#### 8. Founder Dominance
**The Problem:** Founding members maintaining outsized control, undermining democratic principles.

**The Solution:**
- Build democratic culture from day one
- Establish term limits for leadership positions
- Create genuine paths for newer members to participate
- Regularly rotate meeting facilitation and leadership roles

#### 9. Meeting Burnout
**The Problem:** Too many meetings or inefficient meetings leading to member disengagement.

**The Solution:**
- Clearly define what requires full group decision
- Use consent-based decision making for routine matters
- Train members in effective meeting facilitation
- Set and respect time limits

### Local Compliance Pitfalls

#### 10. Missing Local Business License Requirements
**The Problem:** Assuming no license is needed because South Carolina has no statewide requirement.

**The Solution:**
- Check with your specific city and county
- Use [SC Business One Stop](https://scbos.sc.gov/business-compliance/licenses/business-license) to identify local requirements
- Remember: you may need licenses in multiple jurisdictions if operating in more than one area

#### 11. Overlooking Industry-Specific Permits
**The Problem:** Launching without required industry permits (food service, construction, childcare, etc.).

**The Solution:**
- Research your industry's specific requirements early
- Contact relevant state agencies (DHEC, LLR, DSS)
- Build permit timelines into your launch plan
- Budget for permit fees and any required modifications

### Growth and Sustainability Pitfalls

#### 12. Growing Too Fast
**The Problem:** Adding members faster than cooperative culture can absorb them.

**The Solution:**
- Establish sustainable growth plan
- Ensure robust onboarding and training for new members
- Maintain mentor relationships for new members
- Preserve time for governance and relationship-building

#### 13. Neglecting Member Education
**The Problem:** Members who don't understand cooperative principles or their rights and responsibilities.

**The Solution:**
- Require cooperative education before or during candidacy period
- Provide ongoing education opportunities
- Connect with [Co-op Academy](https://www.co-opsnow.org/coop-academy) or similar programs
- Send members to cooperative conferences and trainings

## Frequently Asked Questions (FAQs)

### FAQ 1: Does South Carolina have a specific worker cooperative statute?

South Carolina has a general [cooperative association statute (Title 33, Chapter 45)](https://www.scstatehouse.gov/code/t33c045.php) that can be used for worker cooperatives, though it was originally designed primarily for agricultural cooperatives. The statute includes provisions for:
- Democratic control (limited voting per stockholder)
- Patronage-based earnings distribution
- Cooperative governance

However, **most worker cooperatives in South Carolina form as LLCs** with cooperative operating agreements, which offers more flexibility and simpler compliance. The LLC structure allows you to implement all cooperative principles through your operating agreement while benefiting from South Carolina's straightforward LLC formation process.

### FAQ 2: How much does it cost to form a worker cooperative in South Carolina?

**Basic formation costs:**
| Item | Cost |
|------|------|
| LLC Articles of Organization | $110 |
| Name reservation (optional) | $25 |
| EIN from IRS | Free |
| SCDOR tax registration | Free |
| Local business license | Varies ($25-$200+) |
| **Minimum filing costs** | **~$110-$335** |

**Professional services (recommended):**
- Attorney for operating agreement: $1,500-$5,000+
- Accountant setup: $500-$1,500
- Cooperative developer consultation: Often free or sliding scale through SCCCED or Federation of Southern Cooperatives

**Ongoing annual costs:**
- Standard LLCs taxed as partnerships: No annual report filing with Secretary of State
- Local business license renewal: Varies by jurisdiction
- Registered agent (if commercial): $100-$300/year

### FAQ 3: How many people do we need to form a worker cooperative in South Carolina?

**Legally:** A South Carolina LLC requires only one member. A corporation requires one incorporator.

**Practically:** Most successful worker cooperatives recommend:
- **Minimum 3 founding members** to ensure:
  - Distributed decision-making
  - Multiple perspectives
  - Sustainability if one member leaves
  - Ability to establish democratic processes

- **Ideal range: 3-7 founders** to balance:
  - Diverse input
  - Manageable decision-making
  - Shared workload
  - Sufficient capital

There is no legal maximum, but cooperative governance becomes more complex as membership grows.

### FAQ 4: Can we include "cooperative" or "co-op" in our LLC name?

**Caution is advised.** South Carolina LLC naming rules [restrict](https://www.wolterskluwer.com/en/solutions/bizfilings/state-guides/south-carolina-llc-requirements) use of certain words including "cooperative" without special authorization.

**Alternatives:**
- Form under Title 33, Chapter 45 as a cooperative association (then you can use "cooperative")
- Use alternative terms: "Worker-Owned," "Collective," "Workers," your trade name without cooperative in legal name
- Use "cooperative" in your trade name/DBA while keeping it out of your legal LLC name
- Contact SC Secretary of State for guidance on permit requirements

### FAQ 5: Do worker-members pay self-employment tax?

**Generally, no.** Most worker-members of cooperative LLCs are treated as employees for tax purposes and receive W-2 wages. This means:
- Employer and employee each pay 7.65% FICA taxes
- Members do not pay self-employment tax on wages
- The cooperative withholds taxes just like any employer

**However:**
- Patronage dividends may be subject to self-employment tax depending on structure
- Guaranteed payments to member-managers may be treated differently
- Consult with an accountant experienced in cooperative taxation

**Important distinction:** Even though members are "owners," the IRS typically treats worker-cooperative members who provide services as employees for tax purposes.

### FAQ 6: How do we handle it when a worker-member leaves?

Your operating agreement should clearly address member departure, including:

**Voluntary resignation:**
- Notice period required (commonly 30-90 days)
- Process for transitioning responsibilities
- Timeline for capital account redemption
- Return of cooperative property

**Capital account redemption:**
- Typically paid out over time (1-5 years) to protect cooperative cash flow
- May be paid in installments
- Interest may or may not accrue
- Order of priority if funds are limited

**Involuntary termination:**
- Grounds for termination (violation of policies, failure to fulfill obligations)
- Due process required (warning, hearing, vote)
- Supermajority vote typically required
- Same capital account redemption applies

**For the cooperative's protection:**
- Include non-compete and non-solicitation clauses where appropriate
- Address confidentiality obligations
- Establish clear transition procedures

### FAQ 7: What's the difference between forming as an LLC vs. using South Carolina's cooperative statute?

| Feature | LLC with Cooperative Operating Agreement | Title 33 Chapter 45 Cooperative Association |
|---------|------------------------------------------|---------------------------------------------|
| Flexibility | High - customize through operating agreement | Lower - statutory requirements apply |
| Formation complexity | Simpler | More complex |
| Filing fees | $110 | Varies |
| Naming | Cannot use "cooperative" without authorization | Can use "cooperative" |
| Reporting | No annual report for standard LLCs | Annual report to Commissioner of Agriculture |
| Recognition | Well-understood by banks, vendors | Clear cooperative identity |
| Tax treatment | Choose taxation (partnership, S-corp, C-corp) | Specific cooperative tax rules apply |

**Recommendation:** Most worker cooperatives choose the LLC structure for its flexibility, simplicity, and ability to fully implement cooperative principles through the operating agreement.
