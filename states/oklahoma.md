# How to Start a Worker Cooperative in Oklahoma

A comprehensive guide to forming and operating a worker-owned cooperative business in the Sooner State.

---

## Quick Summary: What Makes Oklahoma Unique

Oklahoma offers a distinctive environment for worker cooperatives, shaped by several key factors:

**Deep Agricultural Cooperative Tradition**: Oklahoma has over a century of cooperative enterprise history, particularly in agriculture. Under [Oklahoma Statutes Title 18, Section 421](https://law.justia.com/codes/oklahoma/2016/title-18/section-18-421/), ten or more persons have been able to form cooperative corporations for agricultural, dairy, livestock, mercantile, manufacturing, and other purposes since 1919. This established cooperative infrastructure provides worker cooperatives with a cultural familiarity with cooperative business models and potential mentorship from established co-ops.

**Uniform Limited Cooperative Association Act**: Oklahoma adopted the [Uniform Limited Cooperative Association Act of 2009](https://law.justia.com/codes/oklahoma/title-18/section-18-441-103/) (effective January 1, 2010), codified in Oklahoma Statutes Title 18, Sections 441-101 through 441-1704. This modern cooperative statute provides a flexible legal framework specifically designed for cooperative enterprises of all types, including worker cooperatives.

**Business-Friendly Environment**: Oklahoma offers relatively low formation costs ($100 for LLC filing), no state-level general business license requirement, and the elimination of the corporate franchise tax effective 2024. Combined with affordable operating costs compared to coastal states, Oklahoma presents an accessible environment for worker-owned startups.

**Emerging Worker Cooperative Movement**: The Oklahoma Worker Cooperative Network (OWCN) has been actively promoting worker cooperatives as a tool for economic development, job creation, and community wealth-building throughout the state. Their workshops and advocacy efforts are expanding awareness of worker ownership options.

**Comprehensive Small Business Support**: The [Oklahoma Small Business Development Center (SBDC)](https://www.oksbdc.org/) operates 19 locations across all 77 Oklahoma counties, providing free business advising that worker cooperatives can leverage for development assistance.

**Rural and Tribal Business Support**: Oklahoma has specific programs supporting rural entrepreneurs through the Oklahoma Rural Jobs Act and extensive tribal nation economic development programs that can benefit worker cooperatives forming in underserved communities.

## Best-Fit Legal Structures for Worker Cooperatives in Oklahoma

Oklahoma provides several pathways for structuring a worker cooperative. The optimal choice depends on your cooperative's size, industry, financing needs, and governance preferences.

### Option 1: Limited Cooperative Association (LCA)

The Limited Cooperative Association under the [Uniform Limited Cooperative Association Act of 2009](https://law.justia.com/codes/oklahoma/title-18/section-18-441-105/) is Oklahoma's most purpose-built structure for cooperatives.

**Key Features:**
- **Cooperative-Specific Statute**: Designed explicitly for cooperative governance and operations
- **Flexible Membership**: Can accommodate both patron members (workers) and investor members, with statutory protections ensuring patron member control
- **Perpetual Duration**: Unless otherwise specified in articles of organization, the LCA has perpetual existence under [Section 18-441-105](https://law.justia.com/codes/oklahoma/title-18/section-18-441-105/)
- **Limited Liability**: Members receive liability protection similar to LLCs
- **Naming Requirements**: Must include "Limited Cooperative Association," "Limited Cooperative," "L.C.A.," or "LCA" in the name. "Limited" may be abbreviated as "Ltd." and "Cooperative" may be abbreviated as "Co-op" or "Coop"
- **Broad Powers**: Under [Section 18-438.4](https://law.justia.com/codes/oklahoma/title-18/section-18-438-4/), cooperatives have the power to make any legal contracts necessary for the full exercise of their powers

**Best For**: Worker cooperatives seeking a recognized cooperative structure, those potentially seeking outside investment while maintaining worker control, and cooperatives operating in industries beyond just agriculture.

**Limitations**: Cannot be used for supplying electric energy or natural gas in rural areas (those must use the Rural Electric Cooperative Act).

**Formation**: File Articles of Organization with the [Oklahoma Secretary of State](https://www.sos.ok.gov/corp/filing.aspx).

### Option 2: Limited Liability Company (LLC) with Cooperative Operating Agreement

An LLC structured with cooperative principles in its operating agreement is a popular and flexible alternative.

**Key Features:**
- **Familiar Structure**: LLCs are well-understood by banks, attorneys, and accountants throughout Oklahoma
- **Operating Agreement Flexibility**: The operating agreement governs member relations, allowing you to establish democratic governance, patronage-based distributions, and worker ownership provisions
- **Tax Flexibility**: Can elect pass-through taxation (partnership treatment) or corporate taxation
- **Low Costs**: $100 filing fee for Articles of Organization, $25 annual fee to the Secretary of State
- **Processing Time**: Online filing typically processed in 2-3 business days; mail filing takes 5-7 business days

**Best For**: Smaller worker cooperatives, startups wanting a familiar business structure, and those prioritizing simplicity in formation and ongoing compliance.

**Formation**: File [Articles of Organization](https://www.sos.ok.gov/business/forms.aspx) with the [Oklahoma Secretary of State](https://www.sos.ok.gov/corp/filing.aspx). Filing fee is $100.

### Option 3: Cooperative Corporation

Oklahoma permits cooperative corporations under traditional cooperative statutes in [Title 18, Sections 421-436](https://law.justia.com/codes/oklahoma/2016/title-18/section-18-421/).

**Key Features:**
- **Historic Legal Framework**: Laws dating to 1919, amended in 1923, provide established legal precedent
- **Broad Purposes**: Can be formed for agricultural, dairy, livestock, irrigation, horticultural, mercantile, mining, manufacturing, mechanical, or industrial business upon a cooperative plan
- **Minimum Organizers**: Ten or more persons required to form
- **Bylaws**: Under [Section 18-437.6](https://law.justia.com/codes/oklahoma/title-18/section-18-437-6/), original bylaws are adopted by the board of trustees, thereafter by members

**Best For**: Larger worker cooperatives, those with significant capital needs, or cooperatives preferring traditional corporate governance structures with cooperative principles.

### Tax Considerations: Subchapter T

Worker cooperatives organized as corporations or LLCs/LCAs electing corporate tax treatment can take advantage of [Subchapter T of the Internal Revenue Code](https://www.law.cornell.edu/uscode/text/26/subtitle-A/chapter-1/subchapter-T) (Sections 1381-1388). Key benefits include:

- **Patronage Dividend Deduction**: Cooperatives can deduct patronage dividends distributed to worker-members, avoiding double taxation
- **Single-Level Taxation**: The cooperative deducts patronage-source earnings it distributes to member-patrons from its gross income
- **20% Pass-Through Deduction**: Worker-members may qualify for the 20% qualified business income deduction on patronage dividends
- **Flexible Tax Planning**: Patronage dividends allow flexibility of paying tax at the cooperative level or allocating profit to patrons who then pay the tax
- **Reporting Requirements**: Patronage dividends must be distributed within 8.5 months of tax year end; file Form 1099-PATR for distributions of $10 or more

Consult with a tax professional experienced in cooperative taxation to optimize your structure.

## Step-by-Step Formation Checklist

### Phase 1: Planning and Preparation

**1. Develop Your Cooperative Business Plan**
- Define your business model, products/services, and target market
- Identify founding worker-members and their roles
- Create financial projections including startup costs and operating budget
- Establish preliminary governance structure and decision-making processes

**2. Engage Professional Advisors**
- Consult with an attorney experienced in cooperative or business law
- Engage an accountant familiar with cooperative taxation (Subchapter T)
- Contact the [Oklahoma SBDC](https://www.oksbdc.org/) for free business advising

**3. Choose Your Legal Structure**
- Review the structures outlined above
- Consider tax implications, liability protection, and governance needs
- Consult with legal and tax professionals before deciding

### Phase 2: Name and Registration

**4. Conduct a Business Name Search**
- Search the [Oklahoma Secretary of State Business Name Availability Database](https://www.sos.ok.gov/corp/corpNameAvailability.aspx) to verify name availability
- Names must be "distinguishable" under state law (not deceptively similar to existing registrations)
- For LCAs, ensure your name includes required cooperative designation
- Consider trademark searches to avoid conflicts

**5. Reserve Your Business Name (Optional but Recommended)**
- Submit name reservation through the [Secretary of State forms page](https://www.sos.ok.gov/business/forms.aspx)
- Filing fee: $10
- Reservation valid for 60 days, renewable once for an additional 60 days

**6. Designate a Registered Agent**
- Must have a physical street address in Oklahoma (no P.O. boxes)
- Must be available during normal business hours to accept legal documents
- Can be an individual member, the cooperative itself, or a registered agent service
- Must be at least 18 years old if an individual
- Change of registered agent requires filing and $35 fee

### Phase 3: State Formation Filing

**7. File Formation Documents with the Oklahoma Secretary of State**

For a **Limited Cooperative Association**, file Articles of Organization per [Section 18-441-302](https://law.justia.com/codes/oklahoma/2014/title-18/section-18-441-302) including:
- Cooperative name (with required LCA designation)
- Street and mailing address of initial designated office
- Name and address of registered agent for service of process (per Section 18-441-117)
- Street and mailing address of principal office
- Names and addresses of each organizer
- Duration (if other than perpetual)
- Any other provisions permitted under the LCA Act

For an **LLC**, file [Articles of Organization](https://www.sos.ok.gov/business/forms.aspx) including:
- LLC name
- Registered agent name and address
- Principal place of business
- Duration
- Management structure (member-managed or manager-managed)

**Filing Options:**
- **Online**: Through the [Secretary of State online portal](https://www.sos.ok.gov/corp/filing.aspx) - processed in 2-3 business days
- **Mail**: 421 NW 13th Street, Suite 210, Oklahoma City, OK 73103 - processed in 5-7 business days
- **In person**: Same address, same-day service available for additional $50 fee; documents must be presented before 4:30 PM CT

**Filing Fees:**
- LLC Articles of Organization: $100
- Contact Secretary of State at (405) 521-3912 for current LCA filing fees

### Phase 4: Federal Requirements

**8. Obtain an Employer Identification Number (EIN)**
- Apply online through the [IRS website](https://www.irs.gov/businesses/small-businesses-self-employed/apply-for-an-employer-identification-number-ein-online)
- Free of charge
- Required for all cooperatives, even without employees initially
- Necessary for opening business bank accounts and tax filings

### Phase 5: Oklahoma Tax Registration

**9. Register with the Oklahoma Tax Commission**
- Complete the Business Registration Application through the [OTC New Business Center](https://oklahoma.gov/tax/businesses/new-business-center.html)
- Access the Oklahoma Taxpayer Access Point (OkTAP) at www.tax.ok.gov
- Contact OTC at (405) 521-3160 for questions

**Required Registrations (as applicable):**

**Sales Tax Permit** (if selling tangible goods):
- Oklahoma state sales and use tax rate is 4.5% (local taxes may also apply)
- Fee: $20 plus handling fee
- Register through the [OTC application portal](https://oklahoma.gov/tax/businesses/new-business-center.html)

**Withholding Tax Account** (if you have employees):
- Set up through the Oklahoma Tax Commission
- A permit is not issued, but you receive an account using your FEIN for reporting (Account ID starts with "WTH")
- Under [Oklahoma withholding requirements](https://oklahoma.gov/tax/businesses/withholding.html):
  - If you withhold $500+ per quarter, remit monthly
  - If under $500 per quarter, remit quarterly
  - If required to make federal deposits more frequently than monthly, match that schedule for Oklahoma

**10. Register with the Oklahoma Employment Security Commission (OESC)**
- Required if you have employees
- Sets up your unemployment insurance tax account
- Register through the [OESC EZ Tax Express Portal](https://eztaxexpress.oesc.state.ok.us/) or [OESC Employer Portal](https://employerportal.oesc.ok.gov/)
- Alternatively, mail completed Form OES-1 to: Oklahoma Employment Security Commission, Attn: Employer Compliance, PO Box 52003, Oklahoma City, OK 73152-2003
- Quarterly reports and payments due by last day of the month following quarter end
- Contact: (405) 552-6799

**11. Obtain Workers' Compensation Insurance**
- Required for businesses with employees
- Obtain through a private insurance carrier
- Contact the [Oklahoma Workers' Compensation Commission](https://www.ok.gov/wcc/) for requirements

### Phase 6: Local Licensing and Permits

**12. Check Local Business License Requirements**
- Oklahoma has no state-level general business license
- Requirements vary by city and county among Oklahoma's 77 counties
- Check with your local city hall or county clerk's office

**Common Local Requirements:**
- **Oklahoma City**: [Business Licensing Division](https://www.okc.gov/departments/development-services/business-licensing), (405) 297-2606
- **Tulsa**: License and Collections Department
- **Other cities**: Contact local government offices directly

**Typical Local Permits:**
- Zoning permits/verification
- Sign permits
- Certificate of Occupancy
- Home-based business permits (if operating from residence)
- Industry-specific licenses (food service, contractors, etc.)

### Phase 7: Finalize Internal Documents

**13. Draft and Adopt Bylaws or Operating Agreement**
- See Governance section below for required content
- Under [Section 18-441-304](https://law.justia.com/codes/oklahoma/title-18/section-18-441-304/), bylaws for LCAs establish member and board rights and duties
- Have all founding members review and approve
- Consider professional legal review

**14. Hold Organizational Meeting**
- Per [Section 18-441-303](https://law.justia.com/codes/oklahoma/2014/title-18/section-18-441-303/), initial directors call meeting to adopt initial bylaws
- Elect initial board of directors or managers
- Authorize opening of bank accounts
- Take any other necessary initial actions
- Document all actions in meeting minutes

**15. Open a Business Bank Account**
- Bring EIN confirmation, formation documents, and operating agreement/bylaws
- Consider credit unions or community banks familiar with cooperative structures

## Governance and Documents You Should Prepare

Proper governance documentation is essential for worker cooperative success. Below are the key documents you should prepare.

### 1. Articles of Organization / Articles of Incorporation

This is your formation document filed with the Secretary of State. It establishes your cooperative's legal existence and includes basic information required by statute. Under [Section 18-437.5](https://law.justia.com/codes/oklahoma/title-18/section-18-437-5/), it is not necessary to set forth the purpose for which a cooperative is organized or corporate powers vested in the cooperative.

### 2. Bylaws (for Corporations/LCAs) or Operating Agreement (for LLCs)

This is your primary governance document. Under Oklahoma law, the "organic rules" (articles of organization and bylaws) govern the cooperative's operations.

**Membership Provisions:**
- Eligibility requirements for worker-membership
- Application and admission process
- Membership buy-in amounts and payment terms
- Rights and responsibilities of members
- Probationary or candidacy periods before full membership
- Member departure, resignation, and expulsion procedures
- Return of member capital upon departure

**Governance Structure:**
- General membership meeting procedures and frequency
- Voting rights and procedures (typically one member, one vote)
- "Voting member" defined as a member with right to vote on matters under organic law or organic rules
- Quorum requirements
- Board of directors composition, election, and terms
- Officer positions and responsibilities
- Committee structures (if any)
- Decision-making processes (consensus, majority vote, supermajority for certain decisions)

**Voting Requirements (for LCAs):**
- Amendments to bylaws require at least majority vote of voting power of all members present at a properly called meeting, unless organic rules require greater percentage
- If investor members exist, at least majority of votes cast by patron members required for amendments
- Board may provide description of any bylaws amendment to members within 30 days

**Financial Provisions:**
- Fiscal year
- Allocation of net income (patronage dividends)
- Retained earnings and capital accounts
- Reserve fund requirements
- Audit or financial review requirements

**Operational Provisions:**
- Management structure
- Hiring and termination procedures
- Compensation policies
- Amendment procedures (under Sections 18-441-401 through 18-441-404)

### 3. Member Agreement / Membership Application

A separate document that each worker-member signs, acknowledging:
- Understanding of cooperative principles and member responsibilities
- Agreement to abide by bylaws/operating agreement
- Capital contribution obligations
- Dispute resolution procedures

### 4. Patronage Allocation Policy

If operating under Subchapter T, document how patronage will be calculated and distributed, including:
- Definition of patronage (typically based on hours worked or compensation)
- Timing of allocations
- Form of distributions (cash vs. written notices of allocation)
- Retention and redemption of allocated equity

### 5. Personnel Policies / Employee Handbook

Even in worker cooperatives, clear policies are important:
- Compensation and benefits
- Work schedules and time off
- Performance expectations and reviews
- Grievance procedures
- Health and safety policies

### 6. Meeting Minutes Template

Maintain records of all official meetings, including:
- Board of directors meetings
- General membership meetings
- Committee meetings
- Annual meetings

### Template Resources

The [Democracy at Work Institute](https://institute.coop/) provides template bylaws and operating agreements specifically designed for worker cooperatives. These can serve as starting points, but should be customized for Oklahoma law and your cooperative's specific needs with the assistance of legal counsel.

## Worker-Ownership Conversions

Converting an existing business to worker ownership is an increasingly popular pathway, particularly as baby boomer business owners retire. Oklahoma's cooperative-friendly legal framework supports such transitions.

### Why Consider Conversion?

- **Business Continuity**: Keep the business operating and preserve jobs
- **Legacy Preservation**: Maintain company culture, values, and community presence
- **Employee Reward**: Provide ownership opportunity to loyal workers who built the business
- **High Success Rate**: According to the [Democracy at Work Institute](https://institute.coop/topics/conversion), 90% of worker cooperatives sustain operations beyond five years, compared to 20% of traditional businesses
- **Job Security**: Employees in employee-owned businesses are four times less likely to face layoffs during economic downturns
- **Wealth Building**: Employees possess 2.5 times greater household wealth compared to employees in conventional firms

### The Scale of Opportunity

Every year, 150,000 to 300,000 businesses owned by baby boomers become candidates for employee takeovers as owners reach retirement age. Over the next 15 years, this could create two to four million new worker-owned businesses nationwide.

### Conversion Process Overview

**Stage 1: Feasibility Assessment (1-3 months)**
- Evaluate if the business is suitable for worker ownership
- Assess employee interest and readiness
- Review financial health and business viability
- Consider: Is there capable internal leadership? Are workers interested in ownership?
- Owner must define decision-making power distribution in the process

**Stage 2: Planning and Structuring (3-6 months)**
- Obtain professional business valuation
- Design ownership and governance structure
- Determine financing approach (seller financing, bank loans, member buy-in)
- Draft legal documents
- Form transition committee from interested employees
- Conduct Worker Co-op 101 workshop for employees

**Stage 3: Implementation (3-6 months)**
- Form the cooperative entity
- Execute purchase agreement
- Transfer ownership
- Establish governance systems

**Stage 4: Transition and Stabilization (6-24 months)**
- Train worker-owners in governance and business management
- Build cooperative culture with emphasis on transparency and participation
- Refine systems and processes
- Ongoing education about cooperative principles and financial literacy

### Financing Conversions

**Seller Financing**: Often the most practical approach, with the departing owner providing a loan to the cooperative, paid back over time from business earnings.

**Bank Financing**: SBA 7(a) loans are now available to worker cooperatives for acquisitions.

**Member Equity**: Worker-members contribute buy-in capital, often through payroll deductions over time.

**Combination Approach**: Most conversions use a blend of financing sources.

### Resources for Conversions

- **[Workers to Owners Collaborative](https://becomingemployeeowned.org/workers-to-owners/)**: National initiative supporting business conversions
- **[Project Equity](https://project-equity.org/)**: Technical assistance for employee ownership transitions
- **[Democracy at Work Institute Conversion Resources](https://institute.coop/tools/for-worker-coops/conversions)**: Conversion consulting, case studies, and resources

### Considerations for Oklahoma

- Oklahoma's LCA statute allows mixed membership classes (patron and investor members), potentially useful for gradual transitions where the selling owner retains some investment interest initially
- The flexibility of LCA governance can accommodate transition timelines
- Consult Oklahoma legal counsel experienced in business sales and cooperative law
- Consider tax implications for both the seller and the new cooperative

## Common Pitfalls in Oklahoma

Awareness of common challenges can help your worker cooperative avoid problems:

### 1. Confusing Ownership with Management

**The Pitfall**: Assuming that because everyone is an owner, everyone must be involved in every decision.

**The Solution**: Establish clear distinctions between governance (ownership decisions) and management (day-to-day operations). Create defined roles, decision-making authority levels, and accountability structures. Not every decision requires a vote of all members.

### 2. Inadequate Capitalization

**The Pitfall**: Underestimating startup costs or ongoing capital needs.

**The Solution**: Develop realistic financial projections, maintain adequate reserves, and establish member capital contribution requirements. Oklahoma cooperatives benefit from lower operating costs than coastal states, but still need proper capitalization.

### 3. Treating Cooperative Structure as the Value Proposition

**The Pitfall**: Believing that being a cooperative is enough to attract customers.

**The Solution**: Your products or services must stand on their own merits. The cooperative structure supports good business but does not replace the need for a sound value proposition and marketing strategy.

### 4. Neglecting Legal and Tax Compliance

**The Pitfall**: Failing to maintain proper registrations, filings, and tax compliance.

**The Solution**:
- File annual reports with the Secretary of State (LLCs: $25 annual fee)
- Maintain withholding tax compliance with Oklahoma Tax Commission
- File unemployment insurance reports with OESC quarterly (even if no contributions due)
- Distribute and report patronage dividends properly if using Subchapter T (within 8.5 months of year end)
- Keep local business licenses current

### 5. Unclear Decision-Making Processes

**The Pitfall**: Spending excessive time in meetings without clear processes for reaching decisions.

**The Solution**: Define in your bylaws/operating agreement which decisions require full membership vote, board approval, or can be made by managers/committees. Establish clear meeting procedures and quorum requirements. Use Oklahoma's LCA amendment provisions (Sections 18-441-401 through 18-441-404) as guidance.

### 6. Failure to Document Policies and Decisions

**The Pitfall**: Operating informally without written records.

**The Solution**: Maintain meeting minutes, adopt written policies, and keep records of all significant decisions. This protects the cooperative legally and preserves institutional knowledge.

### 7. Ignoring Oklahoma's Cooperative History and Resources

**The Pitfall**: Not leveraging the state's established cooperative infrastructure.

**The Solution**: Oklahoma has over a century of cooperative history. Connect with the Oklahoma Worker Cooperative Network, agricultural cooperatives, and national resources like USFWC. These connections provide mentorship, advocacy insights, and practical wisdom about operating cooperatives in the state.

### 8. Inadequate Member Education

**The Pitfall**: Assuming new members understand cooperative governance and their responsibilities.

**The Solution**: Develop a robust new member orientation program. Provide ongoing education about cooperative principles, financial literacy, and governance responsibilities. The Democracy at Work Institute offers training resources.

### 9. Not Planning for Member Transitions

**The Pitfall**: Lacking clear procedures for members leaving or new members joining.

**The Solution**: Establish clear policies in your governing documents for:
- Member admission process and probationary periods
- Capital account management
- Return of equity upon departure
- Succession planning

## Frequently Asked Questions (FAQs) for Oklahoma

### 1. Does Oklahoma have a specific worker cooperative statute?

Oklahoma does not have a statute exclusively for worker cooperatives. However, the **Uniform Limited Cooperative Association Act of 2009** ([Oklahoma Statutes Title 18, Sections 441-101 through 441-1704](https://law.justia.com/codes/oklahoma/title-18/)) provides a flexible, modern cooperative framework that works well for worker cooperatives. The statute accommodates "patron members" who conduct patronage with the association (i.e., workers in a worker cooperative). Alternatively, worker cooperatives can form as LLCs with cooperative operating agreements or as cooperative corporations under Sections 421-436.

### 2. How much does it cost to form a worker cooperative in Oklahoma?

Basic formation costs include:
- **LLC Articles of Organization**: $100
- **Name Reservation** (optional): $10
- **Sales Tax Permit** (if applicable): $20 + handling fee
- **EIN**: Free
- **Annual LLC Fee**: $25
- **Same-day processing** (optional): Additional $50

Additional costs may include legal fees for document drafting, accounting setup, and any local licenses. Note that Oklahoma eliminated the corporate franchise tax effective 2024. Overall, Oklahoma's formation costs are relatively low compared to many other states.

### 3. Do I need a business license in Oklahoma to operate a worker cooperative?

Oklahoma does not require a state-level general business license. However:
- Certain industries require state-level professional or occupational licenses
- Local cities and counties may require business licenses (check with Oklahoma City at (405) 297-2606, Tulsa, or your local municipality)
- You will need to register with the Oklahoma Tax Commission for applicable taxes
- Industry-specific permits (food service, contractors, etc.) may be required

### 4. Can a worker cooperative in Oklahoma have non-worker investors?

Yes, if structured as a **Limited Cooperative Association**. Oklahoma law under [Section 18-441-102](https://law.justia.com/codes/oklahoma/title-18/section-18-441-102/) defines both "patron members" and "investor members." The statute includes protections ensuring patron members maintain control (majority of patron member votes required for certain amendments). For LLCs, the operating agreement can be structured to accommodate outside investment while preserving worker control. Consult with legal counsel to structure this properly.

### 5. How is a worker cooperative taxed in Oklahoma?

At the **state level**, Oklahoma taxes business income according to entity type. The corporate franchise tax was eliminated effective 2024. At the **federal level**, worker cooperatives structured as corporations or electing corporate tax treatment can use [Subchapter T of the Internal Revenue Code](https://www.law.cornell.edu/uscode/text/26/subtitle-A/chapter-1/subchapter-T) (Sections 1381-1388) to deduct patronage dividends distributed to worker-members, potentially avoiding double taxation. Subchapter T allows flexibility in whether tax is paid at the cooperative or patron level. LLCs can elect pass-through taxation, with income reported on individual members' returns. Consult a tax professional experienced in cooperative taxation.

### 6. What resources are available for worker cooperatives in Oklahoma?

Key resources include:
- **[Oklahoma SBDC](https://www.oksbdc.org/)**: Free business advising at 19 locations statewide, (580) 745-2877
- **Oklahoma Worker Cooperative Network (OWCN)**: Local workshops and advocacy
- **[Oklahoma Department of Commerce](https://www.okcommerce.gov/)**: Business incentives including Quality Jobs Program and OIEP
- **[OCAST SSBCI Programs](https://oklahoma.gov/ocast/funding-opportunities/ssbci.html)**: Capital access for startups and small businesses
- **[U.S. Federation of Worker Cooperatives](https://www.usworker.coop/)**: National resources, templates, and peer networks
- **[Democracy at Work Institute](https://institute.coop/)**: Technical assistance and developer training

### 7. How long does it take to form a worker cooperative in Oklahoma?

The formation filing with the Secretary of State can be processed within 2-3 business days online, or same-day for a $50 additional fee. Mail filings take 5-7 business days plus mail time. However, the complete process of planning, developing governance documents, establishing member agreements, and setting up tax accounts typically takes **3-6 months** for a well-prepared group. More complex situations, particularly conversions of existing businesses, may take **12-24 months**.

### 8. Can we convert our existing Oklahoma business to a worker cooperative?

Yes. Business conversions to worker cooperatives are increasingly common and particularly promising since existing businesses already have customers, assets, and employees. The process involves:
1. Feasibility assessment and employee interest evaluation
2. Business valuation
3. Legal and financial structuring
4. Formation of the cooperative entity
5. Transfer of ownership
6. Transition period with training and culture development

Oklahoma's LCA statute supports mixed membership structures that can facilitate gradual transitions. National resources like the [Workers to Owners Collaborative](https://becomingemployeeowned.org/workers-to-owners/) and [Democracy at Work Institute](https://institute.coop/our-work/workers-owners-conversions) provide conversion guidance. Engage experienced legal and financial professionals.

Ready to start your worker cooperative in Oklahoma? Here is your action plan:

### Immediate Actions (This Week)

1. **Schedule a Free Consultation** with your nearest [Oklahoma SBDC office](https://business.oksbdc.org/mapresults.aspx) to discuss your business concept and get connected with advising resources.

2. **Research Your Business Name** using the [Oklahoma Secretary of State Business Name Availability Search](https://www.sos.ok.gov/corp/corpNameAvailability.aspx) to verify availability.

3. **Connect with the National Network** by exploring the [U.S. Federation of Worker Cooperatives](https://www.usworker.coop/) resources and considering membership for access to templates, peer networks, and technical assistance.

### Short-Term Actions (Next 30 Days)

4. **Develop Your Business Plan** with clear financial projections, governance structure, and operational plans. The Oklahoma SBDC can assist with this process at no cost.

5. **Identify Your Founding Members** and begin discussions about governance, capital contributions, and roles within the cooperative.

6. **Consult Professionals**: Identify an attorney familiar with Oklahoma business law (and ideally cooperative structures) and an accountant experienced with cooperative taxation.

7. **Explore Funding Options**: Contact the Oklahoma Department of Commerce at 800-588-5959 for a no-cost incentives analysis.

### Formation Phase (1-3 Months)

8. **Choose Your Legal Structure** based on professional advice and your cooperative's specific needs.

9. **Draft Governing Documents** using templates from DAWI or USFWC as starting points, customized for Oklahoma law with attorney review.

10. **File Formation Documents** with the Oklahoma Secretary of State online or at 421 NW 13th Street, Suite 210, Oklahoma City, OK 73103.

11. **Complete Tax Registrations** with the IRS (EIN), [Oklahoma Tax Commission](https://oklahoma.gov/tax/businesses/new-business-center.html), and [Oklahoma Employment Security Commission](https://employerportal.oesc.ok.gov/).

### Post-Formation (Ongoing)

12. **Establish Operations** including bank accounts, insurance, and bookkeeping systems.

13. **Develop Member Education Programs** to ensure all worker-owners understand their rights and responsibilities.

14. **Connect with the Cooperative Community** through the Oklahoma Worker Cooperative Network, USFWC, and other networks to learn from established cooperatives.
