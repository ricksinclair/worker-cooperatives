# How to Start a Worker Cooperative in Iowa

> **Last Updated:** 2024 | **Applicable Statutes:** Iowa Code Chapters 489, 490, 499, 501, 501A

---

## 1. Quick Summary: What Makes Iowa Unique for Worker Cooperatives

Iowa stands out as a state with a **deep cooperative tradition** and an increasingly supportive ecosystem for worker ownership. The state has multiple cooperative statutes, a dedicated Iowa Center for Employee Ownership, and free legal assistance available through Drake University Law School's clinic.

### Key Highlights for Iowa Worker Cooperatives

- **Strong Agricultural Cooperative Heritage:** Iowa has one of the nation's most robust cooperative sectors, with farmer cooperatives, electric cooperatives, and credit unions forming the backbone of rural communities
- **Multiple Legal Structure Options:** Worker cooperatives can organize under Chapter 499 (traditional cooperatives), Chapter 501 (closed cooperatives), Chapter 501A (flexible cooperative associations), or Chapter 489 (LLCs structured as cooperatives)
- **LLC Preferred for Worker Cooperatives:** Due to its flexibility, Iowa's Uniform Limited Liability Company Act (Chapter 489) is widely considered the preferred legal framework for employee-owned cooperatives (EOCs)
- **Model Documents Available:** Drake University Law School has developed Model Organizational and Operating Documents specifically for Employee-Owned Cooperative Iowa LLCs
- **Dedicated State Support:** The Iowa Center for Employee Ownership (IA-CEO) provides education, resources, and guidance specifically for worker cooperatives
- **Low Formation Costs:** Iowa has some of the lowest business formation fees in the nation ($50 for LLC formation)

### Common Entity Choices

| Structure | Best For | Key Consideration |
|-----------|----------|-------------------|
| LLC (Chapter 489) | Most worker coops | Maximum flexibility; preferred for EOCs |
| Cooperative Corporation (Chapter 499) | Traditional cooperatives | Cannot use without dealing with members on cooperative basis |
| Closed Cooperative (Chapter 501) | Smaller, membership-based coops | Membership terminates on death |
| Cooperative Association (Chapter 501A) | Coops seeking outside investment | Allows patron and non-patron members |

## 2. Best-Fit Legal Structures for Worker Cooperatives in Iowa

### Option A: LLC Structured as a Worker Cooperative (Most Common)

**Governing Law:** [Iowa Code Chapter 489 - Uniform Limited Liability Company Act](https://www.legis.iowa.gov/docs/code/489.pdf)

The LLC is the **preferred legal framework** for Iowa employee-owned cooperatives (EOCs) due to its flexibility. The key is structuring the LLC as a membership organization for the mutual benefit of its employee members and incorporating core cooperative principles into the operating agreement.

**Advantages:**
- Maximum flexibility in governance and capital structure
- Pass-through taxation (single level of taxation)
- Limited liability protection for members
- Ability to customize member rights, voting, and profit distribution
- Less regulatory burden than traditional cooperative statutes

**Key Requirements for a Worker Cooperative LLC:**
- Governance based on "one person, one vote" principle
- Profits distributed based on patronage (labor contributed)
- Democratic decision-making structure
- Operating agreement incorporating cooperative principles

**Filing Requirements:**
- File Certificate of Organization with Iowa Secretary of State
- Filing fee: $50 (online or mail)
- Biennial report: $30 online / $45 paper (odd-numbered years)

### Option B: Traditional Cooperative Corporation (Chapter 499)

**Governing Law:** [Iowa Code Chapter 499 - Cooperative Associations](https://www.legis.iowa.gov/docs/ico/chapter/499.pdf)

This chapter applies to cooperative associations formed after July 4, 1935, and is commonly used by agricultural, petroleum, utility, and consumer food cooperatives.

**Key Provisions:**
- Each voting member has one vote and no more
- Proxy voting is prohibited
- Net earnings distributed to members in proportion to their dealings with the cooperative
- No member may own more than one membership or share of common stock
- Minimum of five directors required
- Stock not freely transferable unless articles expressly permit

**Restrictions on Use:**
- Must deal with or function for members at least to the extent required by statute
- Cannot use the term "cooperative" in name unless organized under this chapter
- Members must meet eligibility requirements

**Filing Requirements:**
- File Articles of Incorporation with Iowa Secretary of State
- Biennial report due January 1 - April 1 of even-numbered years

### Option C: Closed Cooperative (Chapter 501)

**Governing Law:** [Iowa Code Chapter 501 - Closed Cooperatives](https://www.legis.iowa.gov/docs/ico/chapter/501.pdf)

A simpler cooperative structure suitable for smaller, closely-held cooperatives.

**Key Provisions:**
- Name must contain "cooperative," "coop," or "co-op"
- Three or more individuals may organize
- Membership terminates upon death of member
- Members cannot sell or transfer voting interests

**Filing Requirements:**
- File Articles with Secretary of State
- Filing fee: $20 for original or amended articles
- $5 for other required documents

### Option D: Cooperative Associations Act (Chapter 501A)

**Governing Law:** [Iowa Code Chapter 501A - Cooperative Associations Act](https://www.legis.iowa.gov/docs/ico/chapter/501A.pdf)

Enacted in 2005, this modern statute allows cooperatives to organize on a partnership model similar to an LLC, with the ability to have both patron and non-patron members.

**Key Provisions:**
- Allows patron and non-patron membership interests
- Enables access to outside equity capital from non-patron investors
- At least 50% voting power on general matters must be allocated to patron members
- Net income allocated to patron members distributed on basis of patronage
- Perpetual duration unless otherwise specified

**Best For:** Worker cooperatives seeking outside investment while maintaining patron member control

**Potential Considerations:**
- May involve securities law compliance for equity interests
- More complex structure than basic LLC

### Worker Cooperative-Friendly Provisions Worth Noting

1. **One Person, One Vote:** Iowa cooperative statutes uniformly support democratic governance
2. **Patronage-Based Distribution:** All cooperative statutes require or permit distribution based on patronage (for worker coops, this means hours worked or other labor measure)
3. **Restricted Transferability:** Member interests typically cannot be freely transferred, preserving the cooperative character
4. **Tax Treatment:** Worker cooperatives can qualify for pass-through taxation, avoiding double taxation on allocated patronage

## 3. Step-by-Step Formation Checklist

### Step 1: Name Search and Reservation

**Search for Name Availability:**
- Use the [Iowa Secretary of State Business Entity Search](https://sos.iowa.gov/search/business/search.aspx)
- Enter your proposed name to check for conflicts
- Database includes over 600,000 business entities (active and inactive)

**Name Requirements:**
- LLC names must include "Limited Liability Company," "L.L.C.," or "LLC"
- Cooperative names (under Chapter 499, 501, 501A) must contain "cooperative," "coop," or "co-op"
- Name must be distinguishable from existing registered names

**Reserve Your Name (Optional but Recommended):**
- File "Application for Reservation of Name"
- Reservation period: 120 days
- Fee: $10
- Available online or by mail

### Step 2: Formation Filing

**For LLC (Preferred Method):**

1. **Online Filing (Recommended):**
   - Go to [Iowa Fast Track Filing](https://filings.sos.iowa.gov/Account/Login)
   - Create an account
   - Select "Form a Business"
   - Complete Certificate of Organization
   - Pay $50 filing fee via credit card or eCheck
   - Processing: As fast as 5 minutes to 2 business days

2. **Mail Filing:**
   - Download Certificate of Organization form from [Iowa SOS Forms Page](https://sos.iowa.gov/businesses/business-entity-forms-and-fees)
   - Mail completed form with $50 fee to:
     ```
     Iowa Secretary of State
     Business Services Division
     321 E. 12th Street
     Des Moines, IA 50319
     ```
   - Processing: Several weeks

**Certificate of Organization Must Include:**
- LLC name
- Registered agent name and address
- Principal office address
- Whether member-managed or manager-managed
- Name and address of organizer(s)
- Duration (perpetual or specific term)

**For Cooperative Corporation (Chapter 499):**
- File Articles of Incorporation with Secretary of State
- Include statement of cooperative purpose and operations
- Minimum five directors

### Step 3: Appoint a Registered Agent

**Requirements:**
- Must have a physical Iowa address (not a P.O. Box)
- Must be available during regular business hours
- Can be an individual Iowa resident or authorized business entity

**Special Rule for Cooperative Associations:**
- A cooperative association can only choose a domestic corporation or not-for-profit corporation as a registered agent (not an LLC)

**Options:**
1. **Be Your Own Agent:** If you live in Iowa and your business office serves as the registered office
2. **Use a Member:** Designate a member who meets the requirements
3. **Commercial Registered Agent Service:** Typically $49-$150/year

**Consequences of Non-Compliance:**
- Failure to maintain a registered agent can result in loss of good standing
- May prevent business loans, multi-state expansion, or authority to conduct business

### Step 4: Obtain Federal EIN

**What:** Employer Identification Number from the IRS (required for tax filing, bank accounts, hiring)

**How to Apply:**
- **Online (Free, Immediate):** [IRS EIN Online Application](https://www.irs.gov/businesses/small-businesses-self-employed/apply-for-an-employer-identification-number-ein-online)
- **By Mail/Fax:** Use Form SS-4

**Required Information:**
- Legal name of cooperative
- Principal business address
- Responsible party information
- Type of entity
- Reason for applying

### Step 5: Register with Iowa Department of Revenue

**When Required:**
- If you have employees (withholding tax)
- If you sell taxable goods or services (sales tax)
- If you collect hotel/motel taxes or other special taxes

**How to Register:**
- **Online:** [GovConnectIowa](https://govconnect.iowa.gov/) - Click "Register a New Business"
- **Paper:** Form 78-005a, Iowa Business Tax Permit Registration

**Tax Types Available:**
- Sales and use tax
- Withholding tax
- Hotel/motel tax
- Motor vehicle rental tax
- Household hazardous material permits

**Processing Time:**
- Online: As fast as one business day
- Mail: Up to 6 weeks

**Note:** Sales tax permits are free. Your EIN becomes your state withholding tax number.

**Resource:** [Iowa Department of Revenue Starting a Business Guide](https://revenue.iowa.gov/taxes/file-my-taxes/business-taxes/starting-business)

### Step 6: Local Licensing and Permits

Iowa does not have a general state business license, but local requirements vary.

**Common Local Requirements:**
- Zoning permits
- Building permits
- Signage permits
- Certificate of Occupancy
- Home-based business permits (in some municipalities)
- Health department inspections (food businesses)

**How to Find Local Requirements:**
1. Contact your city clerk's office
2. Contact your county administration office
3. Use [IASourceLink Business License Information Center](https://license.iasourcelink.com/)
4. Visit [Iowa.gov Licenses & Permits Portal](https://www.iowa.gov/licenses-permits)

**Note:** Des Moines, Cedar Rapids, and Iowa City have more comprehensive local licensing requirements than smaller communities.

## 4. Governance and Documents to Prepare

### Operating Agreement (for LLC Structure)

The operating agreement is the most critical document for a worker cooperative LLC. Iowa does not require LLCs to have an operating agreement, but it is **essential** for cooperatives to document their governance structure.

**Key Provisions to Include:**

#### Member Classes
- **Worker-Members:** Define eligibility criteria (e.g., minimum hours worked, probationary period)
- **Candidacy/Probationary Period:** Typically 3-12 months before full membership
- **Membership Buy-In:** Amount required to purchase membership interest
- **Exit Provisions:** How members withdraw or are expelled; redemption of interests

#### Voting Rights
- **One Person, One Vote:** Establish democratic voting principle
- **Quorum Requirements:** Minimum percentage of members needed for valid decisions
- **Majority vs. Supermajority:** Specify which decisions require different thresholds
- **Meeting Frequency:** Annual meetings, regular member meetings, special meetings

#### Patronage and Allocations
- **Definition of Patronage:** For worker coops, typically hours worked or a formula based on labor contribution
- **Allocation of Net Income:** How profits are divided between:
  - Collective/indivisible reserves
  - Individual capital accounts
  - Cash distributions
- **Retained Patronage (Capital Credits):** Portion of patronage retained for cooperative capitalization
- **Rotation Period:** When and how retained patronage is paid out to members

#### Capital Structure
- **Initial Capital Contribution:** Member buy-in amount
- **Internal Capital Accounts:** Individual accounts tracking each member's equity
- **Preference Share/Non-Voting Interests:** If seeking outside investment
- **Interest on Capital:** Whether and how much to pay (typically limited in cooperatives)

#### Management and Officers
- **Board of Directors:** Size, election procedures, terms, duties
- **Officers:** Roles (President, Secretary, Treasurer, etc.) and responsibilities
- **Committees:** Standing committees and their authority
- **Hired Management:** Relationship between board, officers, and any hired managers

### Bylaws (for Cooperative Corporation Structure)

Under Iowa Code Chapter 501A, bylaws must govern:
- Business affairs and structure
- Qualifications, classification, rights, and obligations of members
- Classifications, allocations, and distributions of membership interests
- Number and election of directors; their qualifications, powers, duties, and compensation
- Qualifications of members and any limitations on their number
- Manner of admission, withdrawal, suspension, and expulsion of members
- Capital structure, including classes and relative rights of member interests

### Additional Documents to Prepare

1. **Member Handbook:** Policies, procedures, expectations
2. **Membership Agreement:** Contract between cooperative and each member
3. **Buy-Sell Agreement:** Terms for buying and selling member interests
4. **Employment Policies:** Even though members are owners, document workplace policies
5. **Conflict of Interest Policy:** Required for good governance
6. **Meeting Minutes Templates:** Document member and board meetings

### Model Documents Available

**Drake University Law School's Entrepreneurial and Transactional Law Clinic** has developed Model Organizational and Operating Documents specifically for Employee-Owned Cooperative Iowa LLCs. Contact the [Iowa Center for Employee Ownership](https://ia-ceo.org/) for access to these resources.

## 6. Worker-Ownership Conversions

### Overview

Business succession is a critical issue in Iowa, with an estimated 79-81% of small business owners planning to exit their business in the next 10 years. Converting to worker ownership is a proven strategy for preserving business legacy while providing a buyer for the selling owner.

### Conversion to Worker Cooperative

**Typical Process:**
1. **Assess Feasibility:** Evaluate whether the business is suitable for conversion (stable cash flow, engaged workforce, viable market position)
2. **Engage Advisors:** Work with attorneys and accountants experienced in cooperative conversions
3. **Business Valuation:** Obtain independent valuation of the business
4. **Structure the Transaction:** Determine payment terms (lump sum vs. seller financing)
5. **Form the Cooperative:** Create the new worker cooperative entity
6. **Asset/Stock Transfer:** Transfer ownership to the cooperative
7. **Develop Governance:** Establish bylaws, operating agreement, and policies
8. **Train Worker-Owners:** Educate employees on ownership responsibilities
9. **Transition Management:** Phase seller out of daily operations over agreed period

**Tax Considerations:**
- Sellers of businesses converting to EOC form can potentially avoid current recognition of gain if the transaction is structured properly
- Consult a tax professional for specific guidance

### ESOP vs. Worker Cooperative Comparison

| Factor | ESOP | Worker Cooperative |
|--------|------|-------------------|
| **Governance** | Voting tied to shares; not necessarily one person, one vote | Democratic: one person, one vote |
| **Regulation** | Heavy (IRS, DOL, ERISA) | Lighter regulatory burden |
| **Setup Cost** | High ($50,000-$150,000+) | Lower (varies) |
| **Ongoing Admin** | Annual valuations, DOL filings required | Less administrative burden |
| **Tax Benefits** | Significant (S-corp ESOP exemption from federal income tax; Section 1042 deferral) | Pass-through taxation; single level of tax |
| **Best For** | Larger businesses (typically 20+ employees) | Smaller to mid-sized businesses; values-driven organizations |
| **Control** | Less direct worker control | Maximum worker control |

**Iowa-Specific ESOP Incentive:**
House File 2284 (2012) provides a 50% reduction in Iowa capital gains taxes for business owners selling to an ESOP when the ESOP owns at least 30% of the company after the sale.

## 7. Common Pitfalls in Iowa

### 1. Choosing the Wrong Legal Structure

**The Problem:** Using a traditional cooperative statute when an LLC would be more flexible, or organizing under Chapter 490 (general corporations) and losing the ability to use "cooperative" in the name.

**The Solution:** Carefully evaluate options with an attorney. For most worker cooperatives, the LLC structure under Chapter 489 offers the best combination of flexibility and cooperative principles.

### 2. Inadequate Operating Agreement

**The Problem:** Using generic LLC operating agreement templates that don't incorporate cooperative principles, leading to governance disputes and legal ambiguity.

**The Solution:** Use the Drake University Law School model documents or work with an attorney experienced in cooperative law to draft a comprehensive operating agreement.

### 3. Securities Law Compliance Overlooked

**The Problem:** Issuing member equity interests without considering whether they constitute "securities" under federal and state law.

**The Solution:** Consult with an attorney about securities law exemptions. Worker cooperative membership interests may qualify for exemptions, but this requires careful structuring.

### 4. Insufficient Capitalization

**The Problem:** Underfunding the cooperative at startup, leading to cash flow problems and inability to achieve goals.

**The Solution:** Develop realistic financial projections. Consider member buy-ins, retained patronage policies, and external financing options. Iowa's cooperative statutes traditionally limit returns on capital, so plan for alternative capitalization strategies.

### 5. Registered Agent Issues

**The Problem:** Using a P.O. Box, using an out-of-state agent, or allowing the registered agent to lapse, resulting in loss of good standing.

**The Solution:** Ensure your registered agent has a physical Iowa address and is available during business hours. Note that cooperative associations under Chapter 501A can only use domestic corporations or nonprofits as registered agents (not LLCs).

### 6. Missing Biennial Reports

**The Problem:** Forgetting to file the biennial report, leading to administrative dissolution.

**The Solution:**
- LLCs file in odd-numbered years (January 1 - April 1)
- Corporations and cooperative associations file in even-numbered years
- Set calendar reminders; use online filing for faster processing

### 7. Inadequate Member Education

**The Problem:** Worker-owners don't understand their rights and responsibilities, leading to disengagement or governance problems.

**The Solution:** Implement ongoing education programs about cooperative principles, financial literacy, and democratic participation. The Iowa Center for Employee Ownership offers resources.

### 8. Ignoring Local Requirements

**The Problem:** Complying with state requirements but overlooking local zoning, licensing, or permit requirements.

**The Solution:** Contact your local city clerk and county administration early in the process. Use [IASourceLink](https://license.iasourcelink.com/) to research local requirements.

## 8. Frequently Asked Questions (FAQs)

### Q1: Do I need to use a specific cooperative statute to form a worker cooperative in Iowa?

**A:** No. While Iowa has several cooperative-specific statutes (Chapters 499, 501, and 501A), most worker cooperatives in Iowa form as LLCs under Chapter 489 and structure their operating agreement to incorporate cooperative principles. This approach offers maximum flexibility. However, if you want to use the word "cooperative" in your business name, you typically must organize under one of the cooperative statutes.

### Q2: What is the cost to form a worker cooperative in Iowa?

**A:** Formation costs are relatively low in Iowa:
- LLC Certificate of Organization: $50
- Name reservation (optional): $10
- EIN: Free
- State tax registration: Free
- Biennial report: $30 (online) or $45 (paper)
- Operating agreement drafting: Varies (free through Drake ETC for qualifying businesses, or $500-$3,000+ with private attorney)

### Q3: Can I get free legal help forming a worker cooperative in Iowa?

**A:** Yes. Drake University Law School's Entrepreneurial and Transactional Law Clinic provides free legal services to qualifying small to medium-sized business owners in rural Iowa communities considering employee ownership. Contact the [Iowa Center for Employee Ownership](https://ia-ceo.org/) for referral.

### Q4: How is a worker cooperative taxed in Iowa?

**A:** Worker cooperatives structured as LLCs typically receive pass-through tax treatment, meaning the cooperative itself is not taxed; instead, income and deductions pass through to members' individual tax returns. Patronage-based allocations to members are generally deductible by the cooperative. Iowa follows federal tax treatment in most cases. Consult a tax professional for your specific situation.

### Q5: What is the minimum number of people needed to form a worker cooperative in Iowa?

**A:**
- For an LLC: Iowa law allows a single-member LLC, but a worker cooperative by definition should have multiple worker-owners
- For Chapter 499 cooperative: Requires at least five directors
- For Chapter 501 closed cooperative: Three or more individuals
- For Chapter 501A cooperative association: No minimum specified, but practical operation requires multiple members

### Q6: How do patronage distributions work in an Iowa worker cooperative?

**A:** Patronage in a worker cooperative is typically based on labor contribution (hours worked, production units, or other labor measures). Net income allocated to members beyond dividends on capital and reserve additions is distributed based on this patronage. The cooperative can distribute in cash, capital credits, allocated patronage equities, revolving fund certificates, or its own securities. The specific formula should be detailed in your operating agreement or bylaws.

### Q7: Can a worker cooperative have non-worker investors in Iowa?

**A:** Yes, particularly under Chapter 501A (Cooperative Associations Act), which allows both patron and non-patron membership interests. This enables worker cooperatives to access outside equity capital while maintaining democratic control. However, at least 50% of voting power on general matters must remain with patron (worker) members. Non-patron interests may also raise securities law considerations.

### Q8: What happens to a member's equity when they leave an Iowa worker cooperative?

**A:** This depends on your operating agreement. Typically, departing members receive the balance of their internal capital account over time (not immediately, to protect the cooperative's cash flow). Common approaches include:
- Redemption at book value over 3-5 years
- Redemption when the member reaches retirement age
- Redemption tied to the cooperative's revolving fund cycle

The operating agreement should clearly specify the redemption process, timeline, and any discounts for early departure.
