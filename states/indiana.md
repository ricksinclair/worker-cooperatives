# How to Start a Worker Cooperative in Indiana

> **Disclaimer**: This guide provides general information and is not legal or tax advice. Consult a qualified attorney and accountant familiar with cooperative law before forming your worker cooperative.

---

## 1. Quick Summary

**What makes Indiana unique for worker cooperatives:**

Indiana does not have a dedicated worker cooperative statute like some states (e.g., California, Colorado, or Massachusetts). However, worker cooperatives can still be formed using general business entity structures, most commonly **Limited Liability Companies (LLCs)** with cooperative operating agreements, or standard **corporations** with cooperative bylaws.

Indiana does have specific cooperative statutes for:
- **Agricultural Cooperatives** ([Indiana Code § 15-12-1](https://law.justia.com/codes/indiana/2011/title15/article12/chapter1/)) - primarily for producers of agricultural products
- **Utility/Communications Cooperatives** ([Indiana Code § 8-1-17](https://law.justia.com/codes/indiana/title-8/article-1/chapter-17/section-8-1-17-11/)) - for local and general cooperative corporations providing utility services

**Common entity choices for Indiana worker cooperatives:**
- **LLC (Limited Liability Company)** - Most flexible; allows cooperative principles through a customized operating agreement
- **Corporation** - Can be structured with cooperative bylaws; useful for larger organizations or those seeking certain tax treatments
- **Nonprofit Corporation** - Under [Indiana Code Title 23, Article 17](https://law.justia.com/codes/indiana/title-23/article-17/) for cooperatives with charitable or community purposes

**Key advantages of forming in Indiana:**
- Fast online filing through [INBiz](https://www.inbiz.in.gov/) (typically 1 business day approval)
- No state franchise tax on LLCs
- Reasonable filing fees ($95-$100 for LLC formation)
- Strong regional cooperative development support through the Indiana Cooperative Development Center

## 2. Best-Fit Legal Structures for Worker Cooperatives in Indiana

### Option A: Cooperative LLC (Most Common Approach)

Since Indiana lacks a specific worker cooperative statute, forming an **LLC with a cooperative operating agreement** is the most common and flexible approach.

**Advantages:**
- Complete flexibility to design governance, voting, and profit-sharing structures
- Pass-through taxation (or elect corporate taxation)
- Limited liability protection for members
- Can be structured to qualify for Subchapter T cooperative tax treatment
- Lower formation and maintenance costs than corporations

**Key operating agreement provisions to include:**
- One member, one vote (regardless of capital contribution)
- Democratic governance and member control
- Patronage-based profit allocation
- Membership classes (if needed: worker-members, investor-members, etc.)
- Procedures for admitting new worker-members
- Buy-back provisions when members leave

**Indiana LLC Operating Agreement Resources:**
- [Indiana LLC Operating Agreement Templates](https://www.llcuniversity.com/indiana-llc/operating-agreement/)
- [Cooperative LLC Operating Agreement Template (Subchapter T)](https://institute.coop/sites/default/files/LLC%20Taxed%20as%20a%20T%20Corp.pdf) - from the Democracy at Work Institute

### Option B: Corporation with Cooperative Bylaws

A standard **Indiana business corporation** under [Indiana Code Title 23, Article 1](https://law.justia.com/codes/indiana/title-23/article-1/) can be structured with cooperative principles through its articles of incorporation and bylaws.

**Advantages:**
- More familiar structure for some investors and lenders
- Can issue different classes of stock
- May be preferred for larger cooperatives or specific tax strategies

**Considerations:**
- More formalities (board meetings, minutes, annual reports)
- Typically higher formation and maintenance costs
- May require more complex documentation to ensure cooperative principles are maintained

### Option C: Nonprofit Corporation

Under [Indiana Code Title 23, Article 17 (Indiana Nonprofit Corporation Act)](https://law.justia.com/codes/indiana/title-23/article-17/), a nonprofit corporation may be appropriate for worker cooperatives that:
- Have a primary charitable, educational, or community purpose
- Do not intend to distribute profits to members (or have limited distribution)
- Seek 501(c)(3) or other tax-exempt status

**Note:** Most worker cooperatives distribute patronage dividends and thus typically do not qualify for 501(c)(3) status, making for-profit LLC or corporation structures more appropriate.

### Option D: Agricultural Cooperative (Limited Use)

[Indiana Code § 15-12-1](https://law.justia.com/codes/indiana/2011/title15/article12/chapter1/) provides a specific framework for agricultural cooperatives. This statute:
- Requires five or more persons engaged in production of agricultural products
- Restricts use of "cooperative" in business names to compliant entities
- Provides antitrust exemptions for marketing contracts
- Is designed for producer cooperatives, not general worker cooperatives

**Best for:** Farm worker cooperatives, agricultural marketing cooperatives, or food production cooperatives where members are producers of agricultural products.

### Worker Cooperative-Friendly Provisions Worth Noting

While Indiana lacks dedicated worker cooperative legislation, several aspects of Indiana law are favorable:

1. **Flexible LLC Act**: [Indiana Code § 23-18](https://www.wolterskluwer.com/en/solutions/bizfilings/state-guides/indiana-llc-requirements) allows significant customization through operating agreements
2. **No Minimum Capital Requirements**: LLCs and corporations can be formed without minimum capital
3. **Single-Member LLC Allowed**: Can start with one member and grow
4. **No State Franchise Tax on LLCs**: Reduces ongoing costs
5. **Fast Processing**: Online filings typically processed within 1 business day

## 3. Step-by-Step Formation Checklist

### Step 1: Choose and Reserve Your Business Name

**Search for name availability:**
1. Go to [INBiz Business Name Search](https://bsd.sos.in.gov/publicbusinesssearch)
2. Search your proposed name to ensure it's distinguishable from existing Indiana business entities
3. Your name must be different from domestic and foreign corporations, LLCs, and limited partnerships

**Reserve your name (optional but recommended):**
- File a Name Reservation Application through [INBiz](https://www.inbiz.in.gov/)
- **Fee:** $10 online
- **Duration:** 120 days
- **Renewal:** Additional 120 days for $10 if needed

**Naming requirements:**
- LLC names must include "Limited Liability Company," "LLC," or "L.L.C."
- Corporation names must include "Corporation," "Incorporated," "Company," "Corp.," "Inc.," or "Co."
- Consider including "Cooperative," "Co-op," or "Worker-Owned" in your name (not legally required for LLCs/corporations)

### Step 2: Appoint a Registered Agent

Indiana law ([Indiana Code § 23-0.5-4](https://www.northwestregisteredagent.com/registered-agent/indiana)) requires all registered businesses to have a registered agent.

**Requirements:**
- Must have a physical street address in Indiana (no P.O. boxes)
- Must be available during normal business hours to receive legal documents
- Can be an individual (18+ years old, Indiana resident) or a business entity authorized in Indiana
- Cannot be the business entity itself
- Must provide written consent to serve

**Options:**
- A member of the cooperative with an Indiana address
- A commercial registered agent service (typically $49-$150/year)

### Step 3: Prepare and File Formation Documents

#### For an LLC:

**File Articles of Organization:**
- **Online via [INBiz](https://inbiz.in.gov/business-filings):** $95 (plus ~$2 processing fee)
- **Paper filing:** $100
- **Processing time:** Typically 1 business day for online filings

**Required information:**
- LLC name
- Principal office address
- Registered agent name and address
- Duration (perpetual or specific term)
- Member-managed or manager-managed designation

#### For a Corporation:

**File Articles of Incorporation:**
- **Online via [INBiz](https://inbiz.in.gov/business-filings):** $75
- **Paper filing:** $90
- **Processing time:** Typically 1 business day for online filings

**Required information:**
- Corporate name
- Number and classes of shares authorized
- Registered agent name and address
- Incorporator information
- Principal office address

### Step 4: Create Your Operating Agreement or Bylaws

**For an LLC - Operating Agreement:**

While not legally required in Indiana, an operating agreement is essential for worker cooperatives. Include:
- **Purpose statement:** Commitment to cooperative principles
- **Membership:** Who qualifies, how to join, probationary periods
- **Capital contributions:** Initial and ongoing requirements
- **Governance:** One member, one vote; board structure; meeting requirements
- **Patronage allocation:** How profits/losses are distributed based on work performed
- **Member accounts:** Internal capital accounts, retained patronage
- **Distributions:** Cash vs. retained patronage; timing of distributions
- **Departure provisions:** What happens when members leave (buyout terms, vesting)
- **Dissolution:** How to wind down cooperatively

**For a Corporation - Bylaws:**
- Similar cooperative provisions adapted to corporate structure
- Define voting rights per share or per member
- Establish board election procedures
- Detail patronage dividend policies

### Step 5: Obtain an Employer Identification Number (EIN)

All worker cooperatives need an EIN from the IRS.

**How to apply:**
- **Online (fastest):** [IRS EIN Application](https://www.irs.gov/businesses/small-businesses-self-employed/apply-for-an-employer-identification-number-ein-online)
- **By mail:** Complete Form SS-4 and mail to the IRS
- **By fax:** Complete Form SS-4 and fax to the appropriate IRS office

**Cost:** Free

**When you need an EIN:**
- Before hiring employees
- To open a business bank account
- For tax filings

### Step 6: Register with the Indiana Department of Revenue

If your cooperative will have employees, sell taxable goods, or engage in other taxable activities, you must register with the [Indiana Department of Revenue](https://www.in.gov/dor/business-tax/register-a-new-business/).

**File the BT-1 (Business Tax Application):**
- **Online via [INBiz](https://inbiz.in.gov/taxes-fees/tax-registration)**
- **Processing time:** 48-72 hours

**Common registrations:**
- **Withholding Tax:** Required if you have employees
- **Sales Tax (Registered Retail Merchant Certificate):** Required if selling taxable goods/services ($25 one-time fee)
- **Unemployment Insurance Tax:** Through the [Indiana Department of Workforce Development](https://www.in.gov/dwd/)

**Information needed:**
- Federal EIN
- NAICS code (industry classification)
- Business structure details
- Responsible officer information (including SSN)
- State of incorporation/organization

**Resources:**
- [BT-1 Application Checklist](https://www.in.gov/dor/business-tax/register-a-new-business/online-business-tax-application-bt-1-checklist/)
- Indiana DOR Contact: (317) 232-2240

### Step 7: Obtain Local Licenses and Permits

Indiana does not have a single statewide business license. Requirements vary by:
- Location (city/county)
- Type of business activity
- Industry regulations

**Where to check:**
- [INBiz Licensing Portal](https://inbiz.in.gov/certification/licensing) - State-level licenses and permits
- Your city or county clerk's office
- Local zoning office
- Health department (for food-related businesses)

**Indianapolis/Marion County:**
- [Department of Business and Neighborhood Services](https://www.indy.gov/agency/department-of-business-and-neighborhood-services)
- Requires specific licenses depending on business type
- Zoning, signage, and health compliance required

**Professional licensing:**
- [Indiana Professional Licensing Agency](https://www.in.gov/pla/) - For regulated professions

**State Information Center:** (800) 457-8283 or (317) 233-0800

### Step 8: File Biennial Report (Ongoing Requirement)

Indiana requires a Business Entity Report every two years.

- **Due:** By the end of your anniversary month (based on formation date)
- **Online fee:** $32
- **Paper fee:** $50
- **File via:** [INBiz](https://www.inbiz.in.gov/)

## 4. Governance & Documents to Prepare

### Essential Documents Checklist

| Document | Purpose | Required? |
|----------|---------|-----------|
| Articles of Organization/Incorporation | Legal formation | Yes |
| Operating Agreement / Bylaws | Internal governance | Highly recommended |
| Membership Agreement | Member rights and obligations | Recommended |
| Member Handbook | Policies, procedures, culture | Recommended |
| Patronage Allocation Policy | How profits are distributed | Recommended |
| Buy-Sell Agreement | Member exit procedures | Recommended |
| Meeting Minutes Template | Record of decisions | Required for corps |

### Operating Agreement/Bylaws Tips for Worker Cooperatives

#### Membership Classes

Many worker cooperatives use multiple membership classes:

- **Worker-Members:** Full voting rights, patronage-based allocation
- **Probationary Members:** Limited rights during trial period (commonly 3-12 months)
- **Investor Members (if any):** Limited voting rights (typically capped at 20-49% of total votes), return on capital limited

**Example structure:**
```
Worker-Members: 1 vote per member, 100% of patronage allocation
Investor Members (if used): Votes capped at 25% of total, fixed dividend only
```

#### Voting Rights

Core cooperative principle: **One member, one vote**

Consider defining:
- Quorum requirements (commonly 50%+ of members)
- Supermajority requirements for major decisions (bylaws amendments, dissolution, merger)
- Board election procedures
- How to handle proxy voting or absentee voting

#### Patronage and Profit Allocation

**Patronage** is the basis for distributing cooperative profits to worker-members, typically based on:
- Hours worked
- Compensation earned
- Equal shares (all members receive same allocation)
- Hybrid formula

**Subchapter T Tax Treatment:**
To qualify for cooperative tax treatment under [IRS Subchapter T](https://www.irs.gov/), the cooperative must:
1. Operate on a cooperative basis
2. Allocate net earnings to patrons based on business done with/for them
3. Provide written notices of allocation
4. Distribute patronage dividends within 8.5 months of fiscal year-end

**Typical distribution:**
- 20-50% in cash (immediately)
- 50-80% in retained patronage (credited to member capital accounts)

#### Capital Accounts and Member Equity

Design your internal capital account system:

- **Initial buy-in:** Amount new members contribute (commonly $500-$5,000, or can be deducted from wages over time)
- **Retained patronage:** Portion of annual profit credited to member accounts
- **Redemption schedule:** When and how members receive their equity upon departure (e.g., over 3-5 years to protect cooperative cash flow)

#### Sample Operating Agreement Framework

Consider including these sections:
1. Formation and Purpose
2. Definitions
3. Membership (qualifications, admission, classes)
4. Capital Contributions and Accounts
5. Governance (voting, meetings, quorum)
6. Board of Directors (if applicable)
7. Officers and Management
8. Allocation of Profits and Losses
9. Distributions (patronage dividends)
10. Transfer and Termination of Membership
11. Dissolution
12. Miscellaneous (amendments, dispute resolution)

## 6. Worker-Ownership Conversions

### Converting an Existing Business to a Worker Cooperative

Business conversions represent a significant opportunity for worker ownership in Indiana, particularly as baby boomer business owners retire (the "Silver Tsunami").

**The Indiana Cooperative Development Center ([ICDC](https://www.icdc.coop/)) offers conversion assistance** for businesses looking to transition to cooperative ownership.

### Conversion Process Overview

1. **Feasibility Assessment**
   - Evaluate business financial health
   - Assess employee interest and readiness
   - Review legal structure and debt obligations

2. **Valuation**
   - Obtain independent business valuation
   - Consider cooperative-friendly valuation methods
   - Negotiate fair price with selling owner(s)

3. **Legal Restructuring**
   - Form new cooperative entity (typically LLC)
   - Draft operating agreement with cooperative governance
   - Address existing contracts, leases, licenses

4. **Financing the Buyout**
   - Seller financing (most common in cooperative conversions)
   - Bank loans (SBA, cooperative lenders)
   - Member equity contributions
   - Community investment

5. **Transition Planning**
   - Train worker-members in governance and management
   - Transfer knowledge from departing owners
   - Implement cooperative systems and culture

### ESOP vs. Worker Cooperative: Key Differences

| Feature | ESOP | Worker Cooperative |
|---------|------|--------------------|
| **Ownership mechanism** | Stock held in trust | Direct membership ownership |
| **Governance** | Board-controlled (not necessarily democratic) | Democratic (one member, one vote) |
| **Voting** | Often based on shares | One person, one vote |
| **Profit distribution** | Stock appreciation + dividends | Patronage dividends based on work |
| **Tax treatment** | ERISA-regulated; significant tax benefits | Subchapter T (if structured properly) |
| **Minimum company size** | Typically 15+ employees | No minimum |
| **Setup costs** | $50,000-$150,000+ | $5,000-$30,000 typically |
| **Ongoing administration** | Annual valuation, ERISA compliance | Simpler compliance |
| **Best for** | Larger companies, sellers wanting maximum proceeds | Smaller businesses, values-driven transitions |

**Indiana ESOP resources:**
- [Katz, Sapper & Miller ESOP Services](https://www.ksmcpa.com/) - Indianapolis-based firm with ESOP expertise
- [The ESOP Association](https://www.esop.org/)
- [National Center for Employee Ownership](https://www.nceo.org/)

### When to Choose a Worker Cooperative Over an ESOP

A worker cooperative may be preferable when:
- The business has fewer than 20 employees
- Democratic governance is a priority
- Setup budget is limited
- The seller prioritizes legacy and worker empowerment over maximum sale price
- Employees want direct involvement in business decisions

## 7. Common Pitfalls in Indiana

### Legal and Formation Pitfalls

1. **Not creating a written operating agreement**
   - Indiana doesn't require one, but operating without one means state default rules apply
   - These defaults don't reflect cooperative principles
   - **Solution:** Always create a detailed operating agreement

2. **Using "Cooperative" in the name incorrectly**
   - Under [Indiana Code § 15-12-1](https://law.justia.com/codes/indiana/2011/title15/article12/chapter1/), using "cooperative" for agricultural product activities requires compliance with the agricultural cooperative statute
   - **Solution:** Consult an attorney if your worker cooperative involves agricultural production

3. **Choosing the wrong entity type**
   - Some groups default to nonprofit status, which may not allow patronage distributions
   - **Solution:** Carefully evaluate LLC vs. corporation vs. nonprofit based on your goals

### Tax Pitfalls

4. **Failing to qualify for Subchapter T treatment**
   - Must operate on a cooperative basis and properly allocate patronage
   - Written notices of allocation required
   - **Solution:** Work with a CPA familiar with cooperative taxation

5. **Misclassifying worker-members**
   - Confusion about whether members are employees or self-employed
   - **Solution:** In most worker cooperatives, members are employees of the cooperative (W-2 workers)

6. **Not registering for all required taxes**
   - Forgetting unemployment insurance, local taxes, or sales tax
   - **Solution:** Complete the BT-1 application thoroughly; consult the SBDC

### Governance Pitfalls

7. **Inadequate capitalization**
   - Starting without sufficient capital to weather slow periods
   - **Solution:** Create realistic financial projections; establish adequate member buy-ins and retained earnings policies

8. **Unclear membership criteria**
   - Not defining when workers become members or what happens during probation
   - **Solution:** Document membership pathway clearly in operating agreement

9. **Ignoring meeting and documentation requirements**
   - Failing to hold required meetings or keep minutes
   - **Solution:** Schedule regular member and board meetings; maintain records

10. **No plan for member departures**
    - Lack of buyout provisions creates disputes
    - **Solution:** Include detailed redemption schedules and procedures in founding documents

### Operational Pitfalls

11. **Underestimating the learning curve for democratic governance**
    - Consensus can be slow; members may lack business experience
    - **Solution:** Invest in governance training; consider graduated decision-making (committees, consent-based models)

12. **Not seeking professional help early**
    - Trying to DIY legal and tax matters
    - **Solution:** Budget for attorney and CPA consultations during formation

## 8. FAQs for Indiana Worker Cooperatives

### Q1: Does Indiana have a specific worker cooperative law?

**A:** No. Indiana does not have a dedicated worker cooperative statute like California (Cal. Corp. Code § 12200) or Colorado (C.R.S. § 7-58-101). However, worker cooperatives commonly form as LLCs or corporations with cooperative operating agreements or bylaws. Indiana's flexible LLC statute ([Indiana Code § 23-18](https://iga.in.gov/laws/2023/ic/titles/23)) allows you to structure governance and profit-sharing according to cooperative principles.

### Q2: How much does it cost to form a worker cooperative in Indiana?

**A:** Approximate startup costs:
- **LLC filing (Articles of Organization):** $95-$100
- **Name reservation (optional):** $10
- **Registered agent (if using a service):** $49-$150/year
- **EIN:** Free
- **Operating agreement drafting:** $0 (DIY) to $2,000-$5,000 (attorney)
- **Sales tax registration:** $25 (if applicable)
- **Biennial report:** $32 (every 2 years)

**Total estimate:** $200-$500 (basic DIY) to $3,000-$7,000 (with professional assistance)

### Q3: Can a worker cooperative get an SBA loan in Indiana?

**A:** Yes. Worker cooperatives are eligible for SBA loans, including:
- **SBA 7(a) loans:** General business loans
- **SBA 504 loans:** For real estate and equipment
- **SBA Microloans:** Smaller loans up to $50,000

Contact your regional [Indiana SBDC](https://www.isbdc.org/) for assistance with SBA loan applications.

### Q4: How are worker cooperative members taxed in Indiana?

**A:** Worker cooperative taxation typically works as follows:
- **As employees:** Members receive wages (W-2) and pay income and payroll taxes
- **Patronage dividends:** Distributed profits based on work performed; taxable to members when received in cash or as qualified written notices of allocation
- **Indiana state income tax:** Flat rate of 3.05% (as of 2025) on adjusted gross income
- **Cooperative-level taxes:** If structured under Subchapter T, the cooperative deducts patronage dividends, reducing or eliminating cooperative-level tax

**Recommendation:** Work with a CPA experienced in cooperative taxation.

### Q5: Do all workers have to be members of the cooperative?

**A:** No. Many worker cooperatives have a mix of:
- **Worker-members:** Full ownership and voting rights
- **Non-member employees:** Workers who haven't yet qualified for membership or choose not to join

Common practices:
- Probationary periods (3-12 months) before membership eligibility
- Membership offered after meeting hours/time requirements
- Some positions (temporary, part-time) may be non-member roles

Your operating agreement should define membership criteria clearly.

### Q6: What ongoing filings are required for an Indiana worker cooperative LLC?

**A:** Key ongoing requirements:
- **Biennial Business Entity Report:** Every 2 years via [INBiz](https://www.inbiz.in.gov/) ($32 online)
- **Federal tax returns:** Form 1120-C (cooperative) or Form 1065 (partnership return for LLCs), plus member K-1s
- **Indiana state tax returns:** IT-20 or IT-65, plus individual returns
- **Payroll tax filings:** Quarterly and annual federal and state filings
- **Sales tax returns:** If registered as a retail merchant (frequency varies by volume)
- **Unemployment insurance:** Quarterly filings with Indiana DWD

### Q7: Can we convert our existing Indiana business to a worker cooperative?

**A:** Yes. Business conversions to worker cooperatives are becoming increasingly popular. The process typically involves:
1. Business valuation
2. Forming a new cooperative entity
3. Financing the buyout (often through seller financing)
4. Transferring assets and operations
5. Training new worker-owners

The [Indiana Cooperative Development Center](https://www.icdc.coop/) provides assistance with conversions. For larger businesses, consider whether an ESOP might be more appropriate.

### Q8: Where can I find a lawyer or accountant who understands cooperatives in Indiana?

**A:** Resources for finding cooperative-knowledgeable professionals:
- **Indiana Cooperative Development Center:** [icdc.coop](https://www.icdc.coop/) - Can provide referrals
- **U.S. Federation of Worker Cooperatives:** [Consultant directory](https://www.usworker.coop/)
- **Indiana State Bar Association:** [Lawyer referral service](https://www.inbar.org/)
- **Katz, Sapper & Miller:** Indianapolis firm with ESOP/employee ownership expertise

When interviewing professionals, ask about their experience with:
- Cooperative or employee-owned businesses
- Subchapter T taxation
- Operating agreements for member-owned businesses
