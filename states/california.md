> **Last Updated:** January 2026
>
> **Disclaimer:** This guide provides general information and is not legal or tax advice. Consult a qualified attorney and accountant familiar with California cooperative law before forming your worker cooperative.

## 1. Quick Summary: What Makes California Unique

California stands out as having **one of the most comprehensive and worker cooperative-friendly legal frameworks in the United States**. With the passage of **Assembly Bill 816 (AB 816)** in 2015, California became the first state to formally integrate worker cooperatives into its Cooperative Corporation Law, creating a clear statutory framework specifically designed for employee-owned businesses.

### Key Advantages of California for Worker Cooperatives

- **Dedicated Worker Cooperative Statute:** California Corporations Code Sections 12200-12656 provide a complete legal framework specifically addressing worker cooperative formation, governance, and operations
- **Employment Presumption Protections:** AB 816 clarifies that election to organize as a worker cooperative does not automatically create a presumption that worker-members are employees for all purposes
- **Securities Exemption:** Worker cooperatives can sell memberships up to $1,000 without securities registration (increased from $300 under prior law)
- **Community Investor Provisions:** Worker cooperatives can accept capital from non-worker investors while maintaining worker control
- **Capital Account Structure:** Explicit statutory authorization for capital account cooperatives
- **Strong Support Ecosystem:** Home to numerous cooperative development organizations, technical assistance providers, and financing sources
- **State Policy Support:** The California Employee Ownership Act (2022) established state resources to promote business transitions to employee ownership

### Common Entity Choices for Worker Cooperatives in California

| Entity Type | Best For | Key Consideration |
|-------------|----------|-------------------|
| **Worker Cooperative Corporation** | Most worker cooperatives | Recommended default choice; statutory protections for democratic structure |
| **LLC (structured as cooperative)** | Flexibility needed; managing member exemptions | Requires careful Operating Agreement drafting to preserve cooperative principles |
| **Hybrid ESOP-Cooperative** | Larger businesses; significant tax benefits | Complex setup; typically requires 15+ employees |

## 2. Best-Fit Legal Structures for Worker Cooperatives in California

### Option A: California Worker Cooperative Corporation (Recommended)

**Governing Law:** [California Corporations Code Sections 12200-12656](https://leginfo.legislature.ca.gov/faces/codes_displayText.xhtml?lawCode=CORP&division=3.&title=1.&part=2.&chapter=1.&article=1.)

The California Worker Cooperative Corporation is the **most comprehensive and protective legal structure** for worker cooperatives in the state. Established through AB 816, this entity type embeds democratic governance directly into the statutory framework.

#### Key Features

**Definition (Section 12201.5):**
A "worker cooperative" or "employment cooperative" is defined as a corporation formed under Part 2 of the Corporations Code that includes a class of worker-members who are natural persons whose patronage consists of labor contributed to or other work performed for the corporation.

**Democratic Control Requirements:**
- One member, one vote principle is embedded in statute
- At least 51% of workers must be worker-members or membership candidates
- Community investor voting is limited to approval rights over major decisions (mergers, sale of major assets, reorganization, or dissolution)

**Capital Account Cooperatives (Section 12317):**
Worker cooperatives may establish themselves as capital account cooperatives, which allows:
- Assignment of retained net earnings and losses to individual member capital accounts
- Creation of an unallocated capital account for collective reserves
- Flexible patronage distribution structures

**Merger Protections (Section 12530.5):**
A worker cooperative cannot merge with any corporation other than another worker cooperative unless it first revokes its worker cooperative election, providing structural protection for democratic control.

**Articles of Incorporation Requirements (Sections 12310-12310.5):**
To elect worker cooperative status, articles must include:
- Statement: "This corporation is a worker cooperative corporation organized under the Cooperative Corporation Law"
- Corporate name, initial agent for service of process, addresses
- Whether voting power and proprietary interests are equal or unequal
- If unequal, the rules for determining voting power and proprietary interests

#### Advantages
- Strongest statutory protection for democratic governance
- Clear legal framework reduces ambiguity
- Securities exemption for memberships up to $1,000
- Merger protections prevent dilution of worker control
- Community investor provisions allow capital raising without losing control
- Recognized and understood by California agencies

#### Disadvantages
- Requires board of directors (may be burdensome for very small groups)
- $800 annual minimum franchise tax
- Less flexibility than LLC for unusual governance arrangements
- Worker-members are generally presumed employees (though AB 816 provides some flexibility)

### Option B: Limited Liability Company (LLC) Structured as a Cooperative

**Governing Law:** [California Revised Uniform Limited Liability Company Act (Cal. Corp. Code Sections 17701.01-17713.13)](https://leginfo.legislature.ca.gov/faces/codes_displaySection.xhtml?lawCode=CORP&sectionNum=17701.01.)

An LLC can be structured to operate as a worker cooperative through its Operating Agreement. This approach offers more flexibility but requires careful drafting to preserve cooperative principles.

#### Key Considerations

**Operating Agreement as Foundation:**
The Operating Agreement must be carefully drafted to include:
- One member, one vote provisions
- Member eligibility requirements (worker-member class)
- Patronage-based allocation of profits and losses
- Democratic decision-making procedures
- Transfer restrictions to maintain worker ownership
- Dissolution provisions protecting cooperative principles

**Employment Status (Critical Distinction):**
Managing members of California LLCs may be exempt from certain employment law requirements (including workers' compensation in some cases) if they have genuine ownership and control. However, this requires careful structuring and legal guidance. Worker-members who do not have significant ownership and control will likely be considered employees regardless of entity type.

**Tax Treatment Options:**
- **Partnership taxation (default):** Pass-through taxation; no entity-level federal tax; members report income on personal returns
- **S-corporation election:** Pass-through with some payroll tax advantages
- **C-corporation election:** Required for Subchapter T cooperative tax treatment

**Subchapter T Considerations:**
If the LLC elects to be taxed as a corporation, it can utilize Subchapter T cooperative tax treatment for patronage dividends. LLCs taxed as partnerships cannot use Subchapter T.

#### Advantages
- Greater flexibility in governance structure
- Managing members may have exemptions from certain employment laws
- No mandatory board of directors
- Easier informal decision-making for small groups
- Same $800 minimum franchise tax as corporations

#### Disadvantages
- Cooperative principles not embedded in statute; rely entirely on Operating Agreement
- Risk that future members could amend Operating Agreement to remove cooperative provisions
- May not be recognized as a cooperative by some lenders or grant programs
- Securities compliance may be more complex
- Less clarity on applicable law compared to cooperative corporation statute

### AB 816: California's Worker Cooperative Act

[Assembly Bill 816](https://leginfo.legislature.ca.gov/faces/billNavClient.xhtml?bill_id=201520160AB816), signed by Governor Brown on August 12, 2015, made California a national leader in worker cooperative law. Key provisions include:

| Provision | Impact |
|-----------|--------|
| **Worker Cooperative Definition** | Created clear statutory definition of worker cooperatives |
| **Employment Presumption** | Election to organize as worker cooperative does not create presumption workers are employees for all purposes |
| **Securities Exemption** | Raised membership exemption from $300 to $1,000 |
| **Community Investors** | Authorized non-worker investors with limited voting rights |
| **Capital Accounts** | Explicit authorization for capital account cooperative structure |
| **Patronage Allocation** | Clarified that net earnings shall be allocated based on labor patronage |
| **Merger Protections** | Worker cooperatives can only merge with other worker cooperatives |

### California Employee Ownership Act (2022)

The [California Employee Ownership Act](https://calosba.ca.gov/for-small-businesses-and-non-profits/employee-ownership-hub/), authored by Senator Becker and signed by Governor Newsom in 2022, established the Employee Ownership Hub within the California Office of the Small Business Advocate (CalOSBA). This law:

- Reduces barriers for businesses transitioning to employee ownership
- Provides state resources and technical assistance for worker cooperative conversions
- Partners with organizations like Project Equity to support small business transitions
- Recognizes worker cooperatives alongside ESOPs and Employee Ownership Trusts (EOTs) as legitimate employee ownership structures

## 3. Step-by-Step Formation Checklist

### Phase 1: Pre-Formation Planning

#### Step 1.1: Founding Group Alignment
- [ ] Identify founding worker-members (typically 2-5 people)
- [ ] Discuss and document shared values, mission, and business concept
- [ ] Determine governance preferences (consensus, majority vote, hybrid)
- [ ] Agree on membership requirements and candidacy period
- [ ] Discuss capital contributions and patronage allocation

#### Step 1.2: Business Planning
- [ ] Develop business plan including cooperative-specific elements
- [ ] Project startup costs and ongoing capital needs
- [ ] Research industry-specific licensing requirements
- [ ] Identify potential customers, suppliers, and financing sources

#### Step 1.3: Choose Legal Structure
- [ ] Review cooperative corporation vs. LLC options
- [ ] Consult with attorney experienced in California cooperative law
- [ ] Decide on entity type based on group's needs

### Phase 2: Entity Formation

#### Step 2.1: Name Search and Reservation

**California Secretary of State Business Search:**
[https://bizfileonline.sos.ca.gov/search](https://bizfileonline.sos.ca.gov/search)

**Process:**
1. Search the California Secretary of State business database to check name availability
2. Names are checked against like entities (corporation names checked against other corporations)
3. The word "cooperative" or abbreviations thereof can only be used if the entity is organized as a cooperative under California law

**Name Reservation (Optional but Recommended):**
- **Filing Portal:** [bizfileOnline.sos.ca.gov](https://bizfileonline.sos.ca.gov/)
- **Fee:** $10
- **Duration:** 60 days
- **Details:** Can list up to three name choices; only first available choice is reserved
- **Renewal:** Can renew, but not for consecutive 60-day periods (at least 1 day gap required)

**Important:** Business search is preliminary only. Do not order stationery or make financial commitments until formation documents are filed and accepted.

- Initial agent for service of process (name and California street address)
- Initial street address of corporation
- Initial mailing address (if different)
- Whether voting power and proprietary interests are equal or unequal

#### Step 2.3: Registered Agent Requirement

California requires every corporation and LLC to maintain a registered agent (called "agent for service of process") in California.

**Requirements:**
- Must be a California resident with a physical street address in California, OR
- A corporation (including the cooperative itself if it maintains a California office) designated to receive service

**Purpose:** Receives legal documents and official correspondence on behalf of the entity

**Change of Agent:** File Statement of Information or specific change form with Secretary of State

## 4. Governance and Documents You Should Prepare

### Essential Governing Documents

#### For Worker Cooperative Corporations

**1. Articles of Incorporation**
- Legal formation document filed with Secretary of State
- Contains worker cooperative election statement
- Establishes fundamental structure (see Section 2 above)

**2. Bylaws (Critical Document)**

The bylaws govern the internal operations of the cooperative and should address:

| Topic | Considerations |
|-------|----------------|
| **Membership** | Classes (worker-member, community investor); eligibility requirements; candidacy period (typically 6-12 months); admission process; member responsibilities |
| **Patronage** | How patronage is measured (hours worked, wages earned, equal); allocation formula; distribution timing; retention policies |
| **Capital Accounts** | Individual capital accounts; unallocated reserves; minimum capital contributions; redemption of accounts upon departure |
| **Voting** | One member, one vote; quorum requirements; supermajority requirements for major decisions; proxy voting; electronic meetings |
| **Board of Directors** | Size; qualifications; terms; election process; officer positions; committee structure |
| **Decision-Making** | Consensus procedures (if used); matters reserved for member vote; delegation to board or managers |
| **Meetings** | Annual meeting requirements; special meetings (can be called by board, president, or 5%+ of members—or for worker cooperatives with 4+ worker-members, the greater of 3 worker-members or 5%); notice requirements |
| **Termination** | Voluntary withdrawal; involuntary termination; appeal procedures; treatment of capital account |
| **Amendment** | Process for amending bylaws; supermajority requirements |

**Sample Bylaws Resources:**
- [Democracy at Work Institute Bylaw Templates](https://institute.coop/bylaw-and-operating-agreement-templates)
- [Sustainable Economies Law Center Templates](https://www.theselc.org/templates)
- [California Center for Cooperative Development Legal Sourcebook](https://cccd.coop/sites/default/files/resources/LegalSourcebookForCaliforniaCooperatives_0.pdf)

---

#### For LLCs Structured as Cooperatives

**1. Articles of Organization**
- Filed with Secretary of State
- Basic formation document

**2. Operating Agreement (Critical Document)**

The Operating Agreement is the foundation of the cooperative structure for an LLC. It must comprehensively address:

**Membership and Classes:**
- Worker-member class with voting rights
- Community investor class (if any) with limited rights
- Eligibility, admission, and candidacy requirements
- Member contributions (labor and capital)

**Democratic Governance:**
- One member, one vote principle (explicitly stated and protected)
- Amendment procedures requiring supermajority to protect cooperative provisions
- Consider "cooperative lock" provisions that prevent removal of cooperative principles

**Patronage and Allocations:**
- How profits and losses are allocated among members
- Patronage basis (hours, wages, equal)
- Qualified vs. non-qualified allocations for tax purposes
- Distribution timing and retention policies

**Management Structure:**
- Member-managed vs. manager-managed
- Role of any board or steering committee
- Officer positions and authorities
- Day-to-day decision-making delegation

**Capital Accounts:**
- Individual internal capital accounts for each member
- Collective reserve accounts
- Redemption upon termination of membership

**Transfer Restrictions:**
- Prohibit or restrict transfer of membership interests
- Right of first refusal for the cooperative
- Ensure interests cannot be sold to non-workers

**Dissolution:**
- Distribution of assets upon dissolution
- Priority of claims
- Treatment of indivisible reserves

**Templates:**
- [Democracy at Work Institute LLC Operating Agreement Template](https://institute.coop/bylaw-and-operating-agreement-templates)
- [Sustainable Economies Law Center LLC Templates](https://www.theselc.org/templates)

### Additional Documents to Prepare

| Document | Purpose |
|----------|---------|
| **Member Handbook** | Explains cooperative principles, policies, and expectations in accessible language |
| **Membership Application** | Formal application process for prospective members |
| **Membership Agreement** | Contract between cooperative and each member |
| **Conflict of Interest Policy** | Addresses potential conflicts for board members and officers |

| **Financial Policies** | Check signing authority, purchasing, expense reimbursement |
| **Personnel Policies** | If members are also employees: working conditions, leave, discipline |

### Tax Treatment Considerations

**Subchapter T (IRC Sections 1381-1388):**
Cooperative corporations and LLCs electing corporate taxation can use Subchapter T for favorable tax treatment of patronage dividends:

- **Patronage Dividends:** Amounts paid to members based on patronage can be deducted by the cooperative
- **Qualified Allocations:** At least 20% paid in cash; members include full amount in current year income
- **Non-Qualified Allocations:** Cooperative pays tax; members include in income when redeemed
- **IRS Form 1099-PATR:** Cooperative reports patronage dividends to members

**20% Pass-Through Deduction:**
Worker-members receiving patronage dividends may qualify for the 20% qualified business income deduction under IRC Section 199A.

**California State Tax Treatment:**
- California generally follows federal cooperative tax treatment
- Cooperatives file California Form 100 (corporations) or 568 (LLCs)
- Consult California-specific guidance from FTB and a qualified CPA

## 6. Worker-Ownership Conversions

Business conversion to worker ownership is an increasingly popular succession strategy in California, particularly as many business owners approach retirement (the "silver tsunami"). California actively promotes conversions through the Employee Ownership Hub and partnerships with organizations like Project Equity.

### How Conversions Typically Work in California

**Basic Process:**

1. **Exploration:** Selling owner(s) explore employee ownership options with advisors
2. **Feasibility Assessment:** Financial analysis, employee interest survey, structural evaluation
3. **Valuation:** Independent business valuation determines fair price
4. **Structure Selection:** Choose between worker cooperative, ESOP, or EOT
5. **Transaction Design:** Develop financing, payment terms, and transition timeline
6. **Legal Documentation:** Create cooperative formation documents and purchase agreement
7. **Employee Education:** Train workers on cooperative ownership and governance
8. **Closing:** Transfer ownership, often with seller financing over time
9. **Transition Period:** Sellers may remain as employees or consultants during transition

### Worker Cooperative vs. ESOP: High-Level Comparison

| Factor | Worker Cooperative | ESOP |
|--------|-------------------|------|
| **Ownership** | Direct; one share per worker-member | Indirect; trust holds shares for employees |
| **Governance** | One member, one vote | Voting typically proportional to account balance |
| **Minimum Size** | Any size (even 2-3 workers) | Typically 15-20+ employees for cost-effectiveness |
| **Setup Cost** | Lower ($5,000-$50,000 typical) | Higher ($50,000-$150,000+ typical) |
| **Annual Costs** | Lower ($2,000-$5,000 typical) | Higher ($15,000-$50,000+ for administration) |
| **Tax Benefits** | Patronage dividend deductions; Section 199A deduction | Seller tax deferral (C-corp); deductible contributions |
| **Complexity** | Simpler structure and compliance | Complex ERISA compliance |
| **Culture** | Explicitly democratic; active participation | Varies; can be passive ownership |
| **Best For** | Small-medium businesses; values-aligned owners; high worker engagement | Larger businesses; owners seeking tax benefits; gradual transition |

**Hybrid Models:**
Some California businesses (like Sun Light & Power in Berkeley) have implemented hybrid ESOP-cooperative structures to combine tax benefits of ESOPs with democratic governance of cooperatives.

### Resources for Conversions

- **Project Equity:** Free initial consultations; comprehensive conversion services
- **CalOSBA Employee Ownership Hub:** State resources and referrals
- **Democracy at Work Institute:** Publications on conversion process
- **Worker Cooperative Academy:** Training program for conversion candidates

### Considerations for Selling Owners

- **Timeline:** Conversions typically take 12-24 months
- **Financing:** Seller financing is common; various structures available
- **Involvement:** Sellers often remain involved during transition
- **Legacy:** Worker ownership preserves jobs and business continuity
- **Tax Planning:** Work with qualified advisors on optimal structure

## 7. Common Pitfalls in California

### Legal and Structural Pitfalls

**1. Employment Law Compliance**
- **Pitfall:** Assuming worker-members are not employees
- **Reality:** Worker-members of cooperative corporations are generally presumed employees for California employment law purposes (minimum wage, overtime, workers' compensation, paid sick leave)
- **Solution:** Comply with employment laws from day one; consult with employment attorney; maintain proper payroll and insurance

**2. LLC Operating Agreement Vulnerabilities**
- **Pitfall:** Failing to protect cooperative provisions in LLC Operating Agreement
- **Reality:** Future members could amend the agreement to remove cooperative principles
- **Solution:** Include supermajority or unanimous consent requirements for changes to cooperative provisions; consider "cooperative lock" provisions; or use cooperative corporation structure instead

**3. Securities Law Issues**
- **Pitfall:** Failing to properly structure membership investments
- **Reality:** Membership interests can be securities requiring registration or exemption
- **Solution:** Keep membership investments under $1,000 to qualify for California exemption; consult securities attorney for larger amounts; properly document all investments

**4. Merger and Conversion Restrictions**
- **Pitfall:** Not understanding that worker cooperative corporations cannot merge with non-cooperatives
- **Reality:** Under Section 12530.5, worker cooperatives can only merge with other worker cooperatives
- **Solution:** Understand this protection before forming; if flexibility needed, consider LLC structure or carefully plan any future structural changes

### Governance and Operations Pitfalls

**5. Confusing Ownership with Management**
- **Pitfall:** Believing every decision must be made by consensus or full member vote
- **Reality:** Cooperatives can and should delegate day-to-day decisions to managers or committees
- **Solution:** Clearly define in bylaws which decisions require member vote vs. board action vs. management discretion; separate governance meetings from operational meetings

**6. Inadequate Member Education**
- **Pitfall:** Admitting members who don't understand cooperative responsibilities
- **Reality:** Uninformed members may not participate effectively or may have unrealistic expectations
- **Solution:** Implement thorough candidacy/probationary periods (6-12 months); provide cooperative education; use member handbook; consider Worker Cooperative Academy

**7. Unclear Patronage and Capital Policies**
- **Pitfall:** Not establishing clear rules for profit allocation, capital contributions, and redemption
- **Reality:** Disputes over money can destroy cooperatives
- **Solution:** Document patronage calculation method, distribution timing, capital account policies, and redemption procedures clearly in bylaws

### Financial Pitfalls

**8. Undercapitalization**
- **Pitfall:** Starting without adequate capital reserves
- **Reality:** Cooperatives need working capital just like any business; patronage distributions should not deplete needed reserves
- **Solution:** Require meaningful member capital contributions; maintain collective reserves; project cash flow carefully; don't distribute all profits

**9. Franchise Tax Surprise**
- **Pitfall:** Forgetting about California's $800 minimum franchise tax
- **Reality:** Both corporations and LLCs owe $800 annually regardless of profit
- **Solution:** Budget for this expense from day one; note additional LLC fees apply if income exceeds $250,000

**10. Subchapter T Misunderstanding**
- **Pitfall:** Assuming LLC taxed as partnership can use Subchapter T
- **Reality:** Only cooperatives taxed as corporations can use Subchapter T cooperative tax treatment
- **Solution:** Work with CPA familiar with cooperative taxation; consider entity election carefully

### Cultural and Relational Pitfalls

**11. Insufficient Conflict Resolution Processes**
- **Pitfall:** No clear procedures for addressing member disputes or underperformance
- **Reality:** Conflict is inevitable; lack of process leads to festering problems
- **Solution:** Establish clear grievance procedures, performance expectations, and termination processes in bylaws and member handbook

**12. Founder Dominance**
- **Pitfall:** Founders maintaining outsized control as cooperative grows
- **Reality:** This contradicts cooperative principles and breeds resentment
- **Solution:** Plan for leadership transition; involve new members genuinely; follow one member, one vote

## 8. Frequently Asked Questions (California-Specific)

### Q1: Should we form a cooperative corporation or an LLC?

**Answer:** For most worker cooperatives in California, the **cooperative corporation is typically recommended**. The Worker Cooperative Corporation statute (created by AB 816) provides structural protections for democratic governance that are embedded in law, including one member one vote requirements, merger protections, and community investor limitations. An LLC offers more flexibility but requires careful drafting of the Operating Agreement to maintain cooperative principles, and those provisions can potentially be changed by future members. Choose an LLC if you need the employment law flexibility of managing member status or have unusual governance needs. Consult with an attorney experienced in California cooperative law to assess your specific situation.

### Q2: Do worker-members have to be treated as employees?

**Answer:** Generally, **yes for cooperative corporations**. Workers for California corporations are presumed employees for employment law purposes. This means compliance with minimum wage, overtime, workers' compensation insurance, and other employment requirements. However, AB 816 provides that election to be a worker cooperative does not create a presumption of employment for all purposes, providing some flexibility. For LLCs, managing members with genuine ownership and control may be exempt from some employment requirements, but this must be carefully structured. All cooperatives should consult with an employment attorney and comply with applicable employment laws.

### Q3: How is California's $800 minimum franchise tax handled?

**Answer:** Both cooperative corporations and LLCs must pay the **$800 minimum franchise tax annually** to the California Franchise Tax Board, regardless of income or profit. For corporations, newly incorporated entities may be exempt from this tax in their first taxable year (check current law). For LLCs, the first $800 is typically due by the 15th day of the 4th month after filing with the Secretary of State. Additionally, LLCs with total income over $250,000 must pay additional fees ranging from $900 to $11,790. Budget for this expense from the start.

### Q4: Can we raise capital from non-worker investors?

**Answer:** **Yes, under AB 816's community investor provisions.** California's Worker Cooperative Corporation law explicitly allows worker cooperatives to have non-worker "community investors" who contribute capital. However, community investor voting power is strictly limited to approval rights over major decisions (mergers, sale of major assets, reorganization, or dissolution). They cannot have voting control over day-to-day operations or board elections. This allows cooperatives to raise capital while maintaining worker control.

### Q5: How do we handle securities compliance when selling memberships?

**Answer:** California provides a **securities exemption for cooperative membership sales up to $1,000** (raised from $300 by AB 816). If membership investments stay at or below this threshold, no securities registration is typically required. For investments above $1,000 or more complex capital structures, you may need to rely on other exemptions or register the securities. Work with a securities attorney, especially if accepting larger investments or investments from non-workers.

### Q6: What resources are available for converting an existing business to a worker cooperative?

**Answer:** California has excellent resources for conversions:
- **Project Equity** offers free consultations and comprehensive conversion services
- **CalOSBA Employee Ownership Hub** provides state resources and referrals
- **Worker Cooperative Academy** (run by SELC, Project Equity, and EBCLC) offers training
- **Local initiatives** in cities like Santa Clara offer free technical assistance

These resources were expanded by the California Employee Ownership Act (2022). Conversion is increasingly common as business owners retire and seek succession plans that preserve jobs and business legacy.

### Q7: Can our worker cooperative merge with a regular corporation later?

**Answer:** **No, not directly.** Under California Corporations Code Section 12530.5, a worker cooperative that has not revoked its worker cooperative election can only merge with another worker cooperative. This is a protection to preserve worker control. If merger with a non-cooperative is desired, the cooperative would first need to revoke its worker cooperative election, which requires following specific procedures and member approval. This limitation should be understood before forming as a worker cooperative corporation.

### Q8: What local support is available in California for starting a worker cooperative?

**Answer:** California has the strongest support ecosystem in the nation:
- **California Center for Cooperative Development (CCCD):** Technical assistance, training, micro-grants
- **Sustainable Economies Law Center (SELC):** Legal resources and templates
- **Network of Bay Area Worker Cooperatives (NoBAWC):** Peer networking in Bay Area
- **Arizmendi Association:** Development model and ongoing support (primarily bakeries)
- **US Federation of Worker Cooperatives:** Headquartered in Oakland; national resources
- **SBDCs:** General small business advising throughout the state

Contact CCCD or SELC as starting points—they can connect you with appropriate resources for your region and industry.
