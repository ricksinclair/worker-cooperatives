# How to Start a Worker Cooperative in Utah

A comprehensive guide to forming and operating a worker-owned cooperative business in the Beehive State.

---

## Quick Summary: What Makes Utah Unique

Utah offers a distinctive environment for worker cooperatives, characterized by several favorable factors:

**Modern Cooperative Legislation**: Utah has adopted the [Uniform Limited Cooperative Association Act (ULCAA)](https://le.utah.gov/xcode/Title16/Chapter16/C16-16_1800010118000101.pdf) under Title 16, Chapter 16 of the Utah Code. This provides a purpose-built legal framework for cooperative enterprises with remarkably low filing fees - just $22 for formation and $7 for annual reports.

**Business-Friendly Climate**: Utah consistently ranks among the top states for business formation and economic growth. The state features streamlined online registration through the [Utah Division of Corporations and Commercial Code](https://corporations.utah.gov/), efficient government services via UtahID, and reasonable filing fees across all entity types.

**Low Tax Burden**: Utah maintains a flat income tax rate of 4.85%, and the state offers various economic development incentives that can benefit cooperative businesses, particularly in rural areas through programs like the [Utah Small Business Credit Initiative](https://business.utah.gov/usbci/).

**Strong Entrepreneurship Culture**: Utah's entrepreneurial spirit is deeply embedded in its culture, with a thriving startup ecosystem centered in the "Silicon Slopes" region. This culture of innovation and self-reliance translates well to cooperative enterprise development.

**Regional Support Networks**: While Utah lacks a state-specific worker cooperative association, organizations like the [Rocky Mountain Employee Ownership Center (RMEOC)](https://www.rmeoc.org/) serve the broader Rocky Mountain region and provide valuable resources for Utah cooperatives.

**Cooperative Heritage**: Utah has a long history of cooperative enterprise, including food cooperatives like [Utah Co-op](http://www.utahcoop.org/) (member-owned since the 1970s) and the emerging [Wasatch Food Co-op](https://www.wasatch.coop/) opening in Salt Lake City.

**Growing CDFI Ecosystem**: Utah has a developing network of Community Development Financial Institutions, including the [Utah Microloan Fund](https://www.utahmicroloanfund.org/), providing accessible capital for underserved entrepreneurs who may not qualify for traditional bank financing.

## Best-Fit Legal Structures for Worker Cooperatives in Utah

Utah provides multiple legal pathways for forming worker cooperatives. Understanding each option helps you choose the best structure for your specific needs.

### Option 1: Limited Cooperative Association (LCA) - Recommended

The **Uniform Limited Cooperative Association** under [Utah Code Title 16, Chapter 16](https://law.justia.com/codes/utah/title-16/chapter-16/) is the most purpose-built structure for worker cooperatives in Utah.

**Key Features:**
- Designed specifically for cooperative enterprises
- Allows flexibility in combining patron (worker) ownership with investor membership
- Provides limited liability protection for members
- Permits democratic governance with one-member-one-vote provisions
- Allows allocation of profits based on patronage (labor contribution)
- Enables both patron and investor classes of membership
- Can legally use "cooperative" in business name

**Governance Requirements:**
- A majority of the board of directors must be elected exclusively by patron members (workers) per [Section 16-16-804](https://law.justia.com/codes/utah/2010/title-16/chapter-16/16-16-804)
- Organic rules can establish additional governance requirements
- Flexible meeting and voting provisions

**Formation Requirements:**
- File Articles of Organization with the [Utah Division of Corporations](https://corporations.utah.gov/business-entities/uniform-limited-cooperative-association/)
- Designate a registered agent in Utah
- Pay $22 filing fee
- Adopt bylaws and operating agreement

**Annual Compliance:**
- Annual report due by end of anniversary month
- Annual report fee: $7 ($10 late fee if missed)

**Best For:** Worker cooperatives seeking a cooperative-specific legal structure with flexibility for potential outside investment while maintaining worker control.

### Option 2: Limited Liability Company (LLC)

A **Cooperative LLC** formed under [Utah Code Title 48, Chapter 3a](https://le.utah.gov/xcode/Title48/Chapter3A/48-3a.html) (the Utah Revised Uniform Limited Liability Company Act) offers maximum flexibility through a carefully drafted operating agreement.

**Key Features:**
- Well-understood legal structure with extensive case law
- Filing fee of $59 for Certificate of Organization
- Maximum flexibility in structuring governance and profit distribution
- Pass-through taxation by default
- Can incorporate cooperative principles through operating agreement
- Utah does not require a written operating agreement (but one is essential for cooperatives)

**Important Considerations:**
- Cannot use the word "cooperative" in the business name (reserved for nonprofit agricultural cooperatives and LCAs under Utah law)
- Requires careful drafting of operating agreement to establish cooperative governance
- May not be immediately recognized as a cooperative by partners and customers
- Default rules under the [Utah LLC Act](https://le.utah.gov/xcode/Title48/Chapter3A/48-3a-S112.html) may not align with cooperative principles - you must override them in your operating agreement

**Key Default Provisions to Address:**
- **Per Capita Voting**: Without an operating agreement, each member's vote counts equally regardless of capital contribution
- **Oral Operating Agreements**: Utah permits oral operating agreements - ensure your written agreement states it is the sole agreement
- **Fiduciary Duties**: The Act imposes specific fiduciary standards that should be understood and addressed

**Formation Process:**
- File Certificate of Organization online through [businessregistration.utah.gov](https://corporations.utah.gov/business-entities/domestic-limited-liability-company/)
- Draft comprehensive operating agreement incorporating cooperative principles
- Pay $59 filing fee
- Annual report fee: $20

**Best For:** Smaller worker cooperatives, startups seeking a familiar business structure, or cooperatives that want maximum operational flexibility.

### Option 3: Corporation with Cooperative Provisions

A standard **Utah Corporation** under [Title 16, Chapter 10a](https://le.utah.gov/xcode/Title16/Chapter10a/C16-10a_1800010118000101.pdf) can be structured cooperatively through bylaws and shareholder agreements.

**Key Features:**
- Traditional corporate structure with cooperative modifications
- Clear separation of roles (shareholders, directors, officers)
- Well-established legal precedents
- Can elect S-corporation status for pass-through taxation

**Considerations:**
- Less flexibility than LCA or LLC for cooperative governance
- Traditional corporate formalities required (annual meetings, minutes, etc.)
- May require creative structuring to achieve cooperative principles
- Filing fee: $70 for domestic corporation

**Best For:** Larger worker cooperatives, those seeking traditional corporate governance with cooperative modifications, or situations where corporate structure is preferred by lenders or partners.

### Option 4: Agricultural Cooperative Association

The **Uniform Agricultural Cooperative Association Act** under [Utah Code Title 3](https://law.justia.com/codes/utah/title-3/) is available for cooperatives engaged in agricultural production, processing, or marketing.

**Key Features:**
- Specifically designed for agricultural enterprises
- Can use "cooperative" in business name
- Special provisions for marketing contracts
- Exemption from certain antitrust provisions
- Can convert to a limited cooperative association under [Section 3-1-46](https://law.justia.com/codes/utah/title-3/)

**Limitations:**
- Limited to agricultural purposes
- Not suitable for general worker cooperatives in other industries

**Best For:** Farm worker cooperatives, food production cooperatives, and agricultural processing cooperatives.

### Comparison Summary

| Feature | LCA | LLC | Corporation | Ag Coop |
|---------|-----|-----|-------------|---------|
| Cooperative-Specific | Yes | No | No | Yes |
| Limited Liability | Yes | Yes | Yes | Yes |
| Use "Cooperative" in Name | Yes | No | No | Yes |

## Step-by-Step Formation Checklist

Follow this comprehensive checklist to form your worker cooperative in Utah.

### Phase 1: Planning and Preparation

**Step 1: Define Your Cooperative**
- [ ] Identify founding worker-members (minimum requirements vary by structure)
- [ ] Define the cooperative's purpose and business activities
- [ ] Determine the products or services you will provide
- [ ] Conduct market research and develop a business plan
- [ ] Establish initial capital requirements

**Step 2: Choose Your Legal Structure**
- [ ] Review the legal structure options above
- [ ] Consult with an attorney familiar with cooperative law
- [ ] Consider tax implications with a CPA
- [ ] Document your decision and rationale

**Step 3: Select a Business Name**
- [ ] Brainstorm potential names that reflect cooperative values
- [ ] Check name availability through [Utah Division of Corporations Business Entity Search](https://corporations.utah.gov/)
- [ ] Verify the name meets Utah naming requirements:
  - Must be distinguishable from other registered businesses
  - LLCs cannot include "cooperative" - use terms like "collective," "workers," or "democratic" instead
  - LCAs can include "cooperative" or "limited cooperative association"
- [ ] Consider reserving your name ($22 fee, holds name for 120 days)
- [ ] Check domain name availability for your business website

### Phase 2: Formation Filing

**Step 4: Prepare Formation Documents**

*For Limited Cooperative Association:*
- [ ] Draft Articles of Organization including:
  - Cooperative name
  - Principal office address
  - Registered agent name and address
  - Purpose statement
  - Member structure (patron/investor classes)
  - Initial board of directors information

*For LLC:*
- [ ] Draft Certificate of Organization
- [ ] Prepare comprehensive Operating Agreement with cooperative provisions

**Step 5: File with Utah Division of Corporations**
- [ ] Create a [UtahID account](https://id.utah.gov/) if you don't have one
- [ ] Log in to [corporations.utah.gov](https://corporations.utah.gov/online-registration-instructions/)
- [ ] Select "Formations" from the left side menu
- [ ] Choose appropriate entity type:
  - "Domestic Limited Cooperative Association" for LCA
  - "Domestic Limited Liability Company" for LLC
- [ ] Complete all required fields
- [ ] Pay filing fee:
  - LCA: $22
  - LLC: $59
- [ ] Submit and await approval (most filings processed instantly; allow 2-4 business days if additional review needed)

**Step 6: Designate Registered Agent**
- [ ] Choose a registered agent (individual or company) with a Utah street address
- [ ] Ensure agent is available during business hours to receive legal documents
- [ ] Include registered agent information in formation documents
- [ ] Consider using a professional registered agent service for privacy and reliability

### Phase 3: Federal and State Tax Registration

**Step 7: Obtain Federal Employer Identification Number (EIN)**
- [ ] Apply online at [IRS.gov](https://www.irs.gov/businesses/small-businesses-self-employed/apply-for-an-employer-identification-number-ein-online)
- [ ] Complete Form SS-4 application
- [ ] Receive EIN immediately upon completion (online applications)
- [ ] Keep EIN confirmation for your records

**Step 8: Register with Utah State Tax Commission**

Note: The Utah OneStop Business Registration (OSBR) system was permanently shut down on September 12, 2024. Tax registration is now handled separately through the Taxpayer Access Point.

- [ ] Access [Taxpayer Access Point (TAP)](https://tap.utah.gov/)
- [ ] Click "Apply for a tax account(s) - TC-69" in the Apply Online section
- [ ] You will need:
  - Federal Employer ID Number (FEIN)
  - Names, addresses, and SSNs of all owners/officers
  - Utah Entity Number (from Division of Commerce)
- [ ] Register for applicable tax accounts:
  - [ ] **Employer Withholding License** (required if paying Utah wages)
  - [ ] **Sales Tax License** (if selling taxable goods or services)
- [ ] Allow up to 2 weeks to receive your Withholding Account ID
- [ ] Contact: [tapsupport@utah.gov](mailto:tapsupport@utah.gov) or 801-297-2200

**Tax Payment Requirements:**
- Utah employer withholding rate: 4.85% flat rate
- Payment frequency based on amount withheld:
  - Monthly: $1,000+ withheld per month
  - Quarterly: Under $1,000 withheld per month
  - Annual: Household employers filing Schedule H

### Phase 4: Unemployment Insurance and Workers' Compensation

**Step 9: Register for Unemployment Insurance**
- [ ] Register with [Utah Department of Workforce Services](https://jobs.utah.gov/ui/employer/public/osbr.aspx)
- [ ] Create employer account at jobs.utah.gov using your UtahID
- [ ] Receive nine-character Employer Registration Number (e.g., C2-345678-9)
- [ ] Registration required if:
  - You pay or will pay $20,000+ in wages in a calendar quarter, OR
  - You have or will have 10+ employees in at least 20 different weeks during the year
- [ ] Contact: 801-526-9235 or 1-800-222-2857

**Step 10: Obtain Workers' Compensation Coverage**
- [ ] Contact the [Utah Labor Commission](https://laborcommission.utah.gov/) for requirements
- [ ] Obtain workers' compensation insurance from:
  - Private insurance carrier
  - State Insurance Fund
  - Self-insurance (if qualified)
- [ ] Maintain proof of coverage as required by law

### Phase 5: Local Licensing and Permits

**Step 11: Obtain Local Business License**

Utah does not require a state general business license, but all cities and counties require local business licenses.

- [ ] Contact your city or county business licensing office
- [ ] Determine applicable fees (vary by location)
- [ ] Submit required documentation
- [ ] Pass any required inspections (zoning, building, parking)

**Common Local Licensing Contacts:**
- [Salt Lake City Business Licensing](https://www.slc.gov/Finance/business-licensing/) - All businesses operating within city limits need a license
- [South Salt Lake Business Licensing](https://sslc.gov/214/Business-Licensing) - License required before conducting business; home occupations included
- [Salt Lake County](https://www.saltlakecounty.gov/business-licenses-permitting/) - Contact MSD for unincorporated areas

**Important Notes:**
- Business licenses are location-specific - change of address requires new application
- Multiple locations require separate licenses
- Home-based businesses may be exempt in some jurisdictions (per Utah State Statute, Salt Lake City does not require home business license unless there is neighborhood impact)

**Step 12: Obtain Industry-Specific Permits**
- [ ] Check with [Utah Division of Occupational and Professional Licensing (DOPL)](https://dopl.utah.gov/) for professional licenses
- [ ] Contact [Utah Department of Health](https://health.utah.gov/) for food service permits
- [ ] Contact Salt Lake Valley Health Department if in Salt Lake County
- [ ] Obtain any other industry-specific permits or certifications
- [ ] Research federal permits if applicable (EPA, OSHA, etc.)

### Phase 6: Operational Setup

**Step 13: Establish Financial Systems**
- [ ] Open a business bank account in the cooperative's name
- [ ] Set up accounting system appropriate for cooperative allocation tracking
- [ ] Establish payroll system compliant with Utah requirements
- [ ] Create system for tracking member patronage/labor contributions
- [ ] Obtain business insurance (general liability, property, etc.)

**Step 14: Hold Organizational Meeting**
- [ ] Conduct first official meeting of founding members
- [ ] Adopt bylaws or operating agreement
- [ ] Elect initial board of directors/managers
- [ ] Appoint officers as required
- [ ] Authorize bank accounts and signatories
- [ ] Approve initial capital calls or membership fees
- [ ] Document all decisions in meeting minutes

## Governance and Documents You Should Prepare

Effective governance documentation is essential for worker cooperative success. Prepare these documents carefully with legal assistance.

### Core Governing Documents

#### 1. Articles of Organization / Certificate of Organization

Your formation document filed with the state. Include:
- Legal name of the cooperative
- Principal place of business
- Registered agent information
- Purpose statement
- Duration (perpetual or specific term)
- Initial member/organizer information
- Basic structure provisions

#### 2. Bylaws or Operating Agreement

The primary internal governance document. Under Utah law, these constitute the "private organic rules" that govern internal affairs. Essential provisions include:

**Membership:**
- Eligibility requirements for worker-membership
- Application and admission process
- Membership classes (if any)
- Probationary periods
- Rights and responsibilities of members
- Transferability of membership interests
- Termination and expulsion procedures

**Governance Structure:**
- Board of directors composition and election
- Officer positions and duties
- Member meeting requirements (annual, special)
- Quorum requirements
- Voting procedures (one member, one vote)
- Committee structures

**Economic Participation:**
- Capital contribution requirements
- Member account structures (capital accounts, patronage accounts)
- Allocation of surplus and losses
- Patronage dividend distribution
- Distribution timing and methods
- Retained earnings policies

**Operations:**
- Decision-making processes
- Work expectations and standards
- Conflict resolution procedures
- Amendment procedures

**Important for LLCs:** Under the [Utah Revised Uniform Limited Liability Company Act](https://le.utah.gov/xcode/Title48/Chapter3A/48-3a-S112.html), operating agreements can be oral, which creates risk. Your written agreement should explicitly state it is the sole and complete operating agreement.

#### 3. Member Handbook / Policies Manual

Document operational policies including:
- Work schedules and expectations
- Compensation structure
- Benefits and leave policies
- Performance evaluation procedures
- Grievance procedures
- Safety policies
- Communication protocols

#### 4. Membership Agreement

Individual agreement signed by each member covering:
- Acknowledgment of cooperative principles
- Capital contribution obligations
- Work commitment expectations
- Confidentiality provisions
- Non-compete clauses (if applicable)
- Buy-out provisions upon departure

### Additional Recommended Documents

#### 5. Board Governance Policies
- Board member responsibilities
- Meeting procedures
- Conflict of interest policy
- Executive limitations
- Financial oversight procedures

#### 6. Financial Policies
- Budget development process
- Expense authorization levels
- Reserve fund policies
- Internal audit procedures
- Patronage calculation methods

#### 7. Employment/Membership Transition Documents
- New member orientation checklist
- Probationary period evaluation forms
- Exit interview forms
- Buy-out calculation worksheets

#### 8. Meeting Templates
- Annual meeting agenda template
- Board meeting minutes template
- Member meeting minutes template
- Proxy/ballot forms

### Record-Keeping Requirements

Utah law requires cooperatives to maintain:
- Articles of Organization and amendments
- Bylaws and operating agreements
- Meeting minutes (members and board)
- Member register with contact information
- Financial statements and records
- Tax filings and correspondence
- Contracts and agreements

Retain records for at least seven years, with permanent retention for formation documents and meeting minutes.

## Worker-Ownership Conversions

Converting an existing business to worker ownership can be an excellent path to creating a worker cooperative, particularly as Utah business owners reach retirement age.

### Why Consider Conversion?

**For Selling Owners:**
- Preserve the business legacy and community presence
- Potential tax benefits through Section 1042 capital gains deferral
- Flexible payment terms through seller financing
- Reward loyal employees who built the business

**For Workers:**
- Acquire an established business with existing customers and cash flow
- Lower risk than startup
- Preserve jobs and workplace culture
- Build wealth through ownership

### Conversion Process Overview

#### Step 1: Assess Readiness

**Business Assessment:**
- Strong financial health over past 3-5 years
- Positive future financial forecast
- Minimal debt relative to assets
- Established customer base and market position
- Documented systems and procedures

**Leadership Assessment:**
- Replacement leadership identified and capable
- Management depth beyond current owner
- Employee interest in ownership
- Skills gap analysis completed

**Employee Assessment:**
- Employee interest and commitment level
- Understanding of cooperative principles
- Willingness to invest capital
- Capacity for governance participation

#### Step 2: Valuation

- Obtain professional business valuation
- Consider multiple valuation methods
- Document all assumptions clearly
- Allow for negotiation flexibility

#### Step 3: Structure the Sale

**Asset Sale:**
- Cooperative purchases business assets
- Seller retains entity liabilities
- Often simpler for tax purposes
- May require re-negotiating contracts

**Entity Sale (Stock/Membership):**
- Cooperative acquires ownership interests
- Entity continues with existing contracts
- May have tax advantages for seller
- Assumes existing liabilities

**Hybrid Approaches:**
- Combination of methods
- Phased transitions
- Seller notes with gradual transfer

#### Step 4: Financing the Conversion

**Common Funding Sources:**
- Seller financing (often 50-70% of purchase price)
- Bank loans (SBA 7(a) loans can be used)
- Worker capital contributions
- Community development loans (CDFIs)
- Philanthropic or impact investors

**Tax Advantages:**
- Section 1042 tax deferral may be available
- Allows seller to defer capital gains if:
  - Cooperative owns at least 30% of company stock after sale
  - Seller reinvests proceeds in qualified replacement property
  - Seller held stock for at least 3 years

**ESOP Considerations:**
For larger businesses (typically 25+ employees), an Employee Stock Ownership Plan may provide greater tax benefits than a cooperative conversion. The [Rocky Mountain Chapter of The ESOP Association](https://www.esopassociation.org/) serves Arizona, Colorado, New Mexico, Utah, and Wyoming.

#### Step 5: Legal Conversion

**Option A: Create New Cooperative Entity**
- Form new LCA or LLC
- Transfer assets from existing business
- May require consent from creditors and customers

**Option B: Convert Existing Entity**
- Utah allows conversion between entity types
- File Articles/Statement of Conversion
- File articles for new entity type
- Maintains existing contracts and relationships

Utah Code [16-16-1601 through 16-16-1613](https://law.justia.com/codes/utah/title-16/chapter-16/) covers conversion and merger provisions for limited cooperative associations.

### Post-Conversion Considerations

- Develop comprehensive member training
- Establish governance structures
- Create financial literacy programs for new owners
- Build management and leadership capacity
- Connect with cooperative networks for ongoing support

## Common Pitfalls in Utah

Avoid these frequent mistakes when forming and operating a worker cooperative in Utah.

### Legal and Formation Pitfalls

**1. Choosing the Wrong Legal Structure**
- Pitfall: Defaulting to LLC without considering LCA benefits
- Solution: Evaluate all options with an attorney familiar with cooperative law; the LCA offers lower fees ($22 vs $59), can use "cooperative" in its name, and is specifically designed for cooperative governance

**2. Using "Cooperative" in LLC Name**
- Pitfall: Utah restricts use of "cooperative" to nonprofit agricultural cooperatives and limited cooperative associations
- Solution: Choose LCA structure if cooperative identity is important, or use alternative terms like "collective," "workers," or "democratic" for LLCs

**3. Inadequate Operating Agreement**
- Pitfall: Using generic templates that don't address cooperative principles, or relying on Utah's default provisions
- Solution: Work with cooperative-experienced attorney to draft customized documents; remember Utah allows oral operating agreements, so explicitly state your written agreement is the complete agreement

**4. Skipping Local Business License**
- Pitfall: Assuming state registration is sufficient
- Solution: Contact your city or county immediately after state formation; Utah requires local licenses everywhere (though some home business exemptions exist)

### Financial Pitfalls

**5. Undercapitalization**
- Pitfall: Starting with insufficient working capital
- Solution: Develop detailed financial projections; ensure adequate member capital contributions; explore CDFI options like Utah Microloan Fund; line up credit before you need it

**6. Improper Patronage Tracking**
- Pitfall: Failing to accurately track member labor contributions
- Solution: Implement robust accounting systems from day one; consult with cooperative-experienced CPA

**7. Misunderstanding Tax Obligations**
- Pitfall: Missing registration deadlines or payment schedules, or confusion due to OneStop shutdown
- Solution: Register directly through TAP (tax.utah.gov) since OneStop closed September 2024; understand withholding payment schedules (monthly, quarterly, or annual based on amounts)

**8. Inadequate Financial Reserves**
- Pitfall: Distributing all surplus without building reserves
- Solution: Establish reserve policies in bylaws; maintain adequate cash reserves for operations and future investment

### Governance Pitfalls

**9. Unclear Decision-Making Processes**
- Pitfall: Ambiguity about who decides what and how
- Solution: Document clear decision-making authority in bylaws; distinguish between member votes, board decisions, and management authority

**10. Neglecting Member Education**
- Pitfall: Assuming workers understand cooperative governance automatically
- Solution: Develop comprehensive orientation program; provide ongoing education on cooperative principles and business literacy

**11. Avoiding Difficult Conversations**
- Pitfall: Letting conflicts fester; avoiding performance issues
- Solution: Establish clear conflict resolution procedures; conduct regular performance discussions; address problems early

**12. Founder Syndrome**
- Pitfall: Founding members maintaining disproportionate control
- Solution: Build in term limits; ensure board rotation; actively develop new member leadership

### Operational Pitfalls

**13. Inadequate Documentation**
- Pitfall: Operating on informal agreements and verbal understandings
- Solution: Document everything: policies, procedures, meeting minutes, member agreements

**14. Ignoring Annual Requirements**
- Pitfall: Missing annual report filings (due by end of anniversary month)
- Solution: Calendar all compliance deadlines; LCA annual reports are only $7 but incur $10 late fee; consider using a registered agent service that provides reminders

**15. Isolation from Cooperative Networks**
- Pitfall: Operating without connection to broader cooperative movement
- Solution: Join USFWC; connect with RMEOC; attend cooperative conferences; network with other cooperatives

### Growth Pitfalls

**16. Growing Too Fast**
- Pitfall: Expanding before governance and systems can support growth
- Solution: Develop scalable systems before expanding; ensure new members receive adequate training and integration

**17. Diluting Cooperative Culture**
- Pitfall: Losing cooperative identity as new members join
- Solution: Strong orientation programs; ongoing cooperative education; embed values in all policies and procedures

## Frequently Asked Questions (FAQs)

### Q1: What is the minimum number of people required to form a worker cooperative in Utah?

**Answer:** The requirements depend on your chosen legal structure. For a Limited Cooperative Association (LCA) under Utah Code Title 16, Chapter 16, at least one person can form the association by filing articles of organization, though practical operations typically require multiple worker-members to function as a true cooperative. For an LLC, only one organizer is required, but a meaningful worker cooperative would typically have at least three to five worker-members to enable democratic governance. Check the specific statutory requirements for your chosen entity type with the [Utah Division of Corporations](https://corporations.utah.gov/).

### Q2: Can I use the word "cooperative" in my Utah business name?

**Answer:** It depends on your entity type. Under Utah law, the term "cooperative" is restricted. **Limited Cooperative Associations (LCAs)** can use "cooperative" or "limited cooperative association" in their names. **Nonprofit agricultural cooperatives** under Title 3 can also use the term. However, **LLCs and corporations** generally cannot use "cooperative" in their business names in Utah. If using the cooperative identity is important to your business, consider forming as an LCA rather than an LLC. Alternative terms like "collective," "worker-owned," or "democratic" may be used with other entity types.

### Q3: How much does it cost to form a worker cooperative in Utah?

**Answer:** Utah offers some of the lowest cooperative formation fees in the nation. Costs vary by structure:

**State Filing Fees:**
- Limited Cooperative Association: $22 (formation) + $7 (annual report)
- LLC: $59 (formation) + $20 (annual report)
- Corporation: $70 (formation) + $20 (annual report)
- Name reservation (optional): $22

**Other Costs:**
- Registered agent service (if used): $50-$300 annually
- Legal assistance: $1,500-$5,000+ for formation documents
- Local business license: Varies by location ($100-$200+)
- EIN: Free from IRS
- State tax registration: Free

Budget at least $2,000-$7,000 for professional formation with proper documentation. DIY formation costs less but carries greater risk of inadequate documentation.

### Q4: What are the tax implications for worker cooperatives in Utah?

**Answer:** Tax treatment depends on your entity structure:

**Pass-Through Entities (LLC, LCA):** Income passes through to members who report it on personal tax returns. Members pay Utah's flat 4.85% income tax on their share of cooperative income.

**Patronage Distributions:** Worker cooperatives can deduct patronage dividends paid to members, reducing the cooperative-level tax burden. Members report patronage dividends as income.

**Subchapter T:** Cooperatives may elect taxation under Subchapter T of the Internal Revenue Code, which provides special treatment for patronage dividends.

**Employment Taxes:** Regardless of structure, cooperatives must withhold and pay employment taxes on wages paid to worker-members.

Consult with a CPA experienced in cooperative taxation for specific guidance on your situation.

### Q5: Do worker-members in a Utah cooperative count as employees?

**Answer:** In most cases, yes. Worker-members of cooperatives are typically treated as employees for tax and labor law purposes, even though they are also owners. This means:

- The cooperative must withhold income taxes and FICA
- Worker-members are covered by Utah employment laws
- Workers' compensation insurance is required
- Unemployment insurance contributions apply (if thresholds are met: $20,000+ in quarterly wages or 10+ employees)
- Minimum wage and overtime laws apply

The worker-member's dual status as owner and employee is a unique feature of worker cooperatives. Some compensation may be structured as patronage dividends rather than wages, but consult with legal and tax professionals to ensure compliance.

### Q6: How do we handle a member who wants to leave the cooperative?

**Answer:** Member departures should be addressed in your bylaws or operating agreement. Common provisions include:

- **Notice period:** Required advance notice (typically 30-90 days)
- **Capital account distribution:** Return of member's capital contributions, often over time to protect cooperative cash flow
- **Patronage account distribution:** Payment of allocated but undistributed patronage dividends
- **Non-compete provisions:** Reasonable restrictions on competitive activity
- **Buy-out formula:** Clear method for calculating any amounts owed
- **Transition planning:** Procedures for knowledge transfer and work reassignment

Utah's LCA statute ([Title 16, Chapter 16, Part 11](https://law.justia.com/codes/utah/title-16/chapter-16/)) addresses dissociation of members from limited cooperative associations.

### Q7: Can a worker cooperative in Utah accept outside investors?

**Answer:** Yes, but with important limitations to preserve worker control. The Limited Cooperative Association (LCA) structure specifically allows for investor members alongside patron (worker) members. Key provisions:

- **Board control:** A majority of the board must be elected by patron members per [Section 16-16-804](https://law.justia.com/codes/utah/2010/title-16/chapter-16/16-16-804)
- **Investor returns:** Investors can receive returns on investment and share of control, but cannot dominate governance
- **Organic rules:** Your governing documents can further limit investor rights

If maintaining pure worker ownership is important, structure your cooperative to either prohibit investor members or strictly limit their rights. An LLC operating agreement can be drafted to accommodate various investment structures while maintaining worker control.

### Q8: What ongoing compliance requirements exist for Utah worker cooperatives?

**Answer:** Utah cooperatives must maintain ongoing compliance with:

**State Filings:**
- Annual Report with [Utah Division of Corporations](https://corporations.utah.gov/) - due by end of anniversary month
  - LCA: $7 fee ($10 late fee)
  - LLC: $20 fee ($10 late fee)
- Renew online via UtahID

**Tax Filings:**
- Withholding tax deposits (monthly/quarterly/annual based on amount)
- Quarterly unemployment insurance reports (via jobs.utah.gov)
- Annual federal and state income tax returns
- Form 1099-PATR for patronage dividends exceeding $10

**Local Requirements:**
- Annual business license renewal
- Any industry-specific permit renewals

**Internal Requirements:**
- Annual member meeting
- Regular board meetings
- Maintain corporate records and minutes
- Member capital account reconciliation

Create a compliance calendar to track all deadlines and consider using a registered agent service that provides compliance reminders.
