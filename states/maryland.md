# How to Open a Worker Cooperative in Maryland

*A practical guide to forming and operating a worker-owned cooperative business in the State of Maryland.*

---

## 1. Quick Summary

**What makes Maryland unique:** Maryland is one of a small number of U.S. states with a dedicated worker cooperative statute. The [Maryland Limited Worker Cooperative Association Act](https://mgaleg.maryland.gov/mgawebsite/Legislation/Details/SB0144) (SB144/HB15), signed into law in April 2025, takes effect **October 1, 2026**. This law creates a purpose-built legal structure for worker cooperatives, establishing them as a specific type of limited liability company with democratic governance provisions built into the statute.

**Baltimore's thriving ecosystem:** Maryland—particularly Baltimore—is home to one of the fastest-growing worker cooperative ecosystems in the country. The [Baltimore Roundtable for Economic Democracy (BRED)](https://baltimoreroundtable.org/) serves as a hub for cooperative development, financing, and education.

**Common entity choices:**
- **Limited Worker Cooperative Association (LWCA):** Available October 1, 2026, under the new statute—the recommended structure going forward
- **LLC structured as a cooperative:** Currently the most popular option, using an operating agreement to establish cooperative governance
- **Cooperative corporation:** Less common for worker co-ops, but available under general Maryland corporate law

## 2. Best-Fit Legal Structures for Worker Coops in Maryland

### Option A: Limited Worker Cooperative Association (LWCA) — Available October 2026

The [Maryland Limited Worker Cooperative Association Act](https://mgaleg.maryland.gov/2025RS/Chapters_noln/CH_199_sb0144e.pdf) creates a tailored legal entity specifically designed for worker cooperatives. Key statutory provisions include:

**Formation Requirements:**
- Minimum of **three worker members** required to begin business operations (unless the sole member is another LWCA)
- Must elect LWCA status and file with the Maryland State Department of Assessments and Taxation (SDAT)

**Governance Features:**
- Democratic self-management by worker members
- Assembly of all voting members for governance decisions
- Board of representatives consisting of at least three individuals
- Cooperative agreement governs internal operations, capital structure, membership rights, profit allocation, and voting procedures

**Member Protections:**
- Members are not personally liable for debts or obligations of the cooperative solely by reason of being a member
- Worker members are considered employees of the association for purposes of state employment law
- Flexible voting power allocation options: one member/one vote, by number of members, or by patronage

**Why choose an LWCA:**
- Purpose-built for worker cooperatives
- Clear statutory framework reduces ambiguity
- Worker-member employee classification is codified
- Simpler compliance compared to retrofitting an LLC

### Option B: LLC Structured as a Cooperative (Available Now)

Until the LWCA statute takes effect, the most common approach is forming a standard Maryland LLC and drafting an operating agreement that incorporates cooperative principles.

**How it works:**
- File [Articles of Organization](https://dat.maryland.gov/sdat%20forms/artorgan.pdf) with SDAT as a regular LLC
- Draft a comprehensive operating agreement establishing:
  - One member, one vote governance
  - Worker-member eligibility and admission process
  - Patronage-based profit allocation
  - Democratic decision-making processes
  - Member buy-in and exit procedures

**Advantages:**
- Available immediately
- Flexibility in structuring governance
- Pass-through taxation options (can elect S-corp or partnership treatment)
- Familiar structure for lenders and partners

**Considerations:**
- Requires careful legal drafting to ensure cooperative principles are enforceable
- Worker classification may require additional documentation
- No statutory presumptions for cooperative operations

**Resources for LLC operating agreements:**
- [Democracy at Work Institute: Bylaws and Operating Agreements Guide](https://institute.coop/topics/articles-incorporation-bylaws-operating-agreements)
- [Sustainable Economies Law Center Templates](https://www.theselc.org/templates)
- [Sample Worker Cooperative Bylaws (DAWI)](https://institute.coop/resources/sample-worker-cooperative-bylaws)

### Option C: Corporation with Cooperative Bylaws

Worker cooperatives may incorporate as standard Maryland corporations and adopt bylaws that establish cooperative governance. This is less common for new worker cooperatives due to:
- Double taxation concerns (unless S-corp election is made)
- More rigid statutory requirements
- Less flexibility than LLC structure

This option may be appropriate for cooperatives seeking to issue stock or those with specific investor requirements.

### Worker-Coop-Friendly Provisions Worth Noting

- **Annual Report Fee Waiver:** Maryland businesses participating in [MarylandSaves](https://www.marylandsaves.org/claim-fee-waiver/) (state retirement savings program) or operating a qualified retirement plan may receive a waiver of the $300 annual report fee
- **Limited Liability Protection:** Both LLCs and the upcoming LWCA structure provide personal liability protection for members
- **Flexible Tax Treatment:** LLCs can elect various tax treatments (partnership, S-corp) to optimize for cooperative patronage distributions

## 3. Step-by-Step Formation Checklist

### Step 1: Name Search and Reservation

**Search for availability:**
- Use the [Maryland Business Entity Search](https://egov.maryland.gov/businessexpress/entitysearch) to verify your proposed name is available
- Name must be "distinguishable upon the records" of SDAT
- Required designators: "Limited Liability Company" or "LLC" (for LLCs); designator requirements for LWCAs will follow the new statute

**Reserve your name (optional but recommended):**
- File the [Corporate Name Reservation Application](https://dat.maryland.gov/SDAT%20Forms/Name_reservation.pdf)
- **Fee:** $25
- **Duration:** 30 days (renewable for additional 30-day periods at $25 each)
- Submit online via [Maryland Business Express](https://egov.maryland.gov/BusinessExpress/Home/WhatsNew) or mail to SDAT

**Naming restrictions:**
- Cannot imply government affiliation (FBI, Treasury, etc.)
- Certain words (bank, insurance, trust) require regulatory approval
- Cannot be deceptively similar to existing registered entities

### Step 2: Prepare Formation Documents

**For an LLC (current option):**
- Draft Articles of Organization
- Prepare comprehensive Operating Agreement with cooperative provisions

**For an LWCA (after October 1, 2026):**
- Draft formation documents per new statutory requirements
- Prepare Cooperative Agreement addressing:
  - Capital structure
  - Membership classes and rights
  - Profit allocation methodology
  - Voting procedures
  - Member admission and withdrawal processes

### Step 3: File Formation Documents with SDAT

**Filing options:**
- **Online:** [Maryland Business Express](https://egov.maryland.gov/BusinessExpress/Home/WhatsNew)
- **Mail:** State Department of Assessments and Taxation, 301 W. Preston Street, Baltimore, MD 21201
- **In-person:** SDAT Baltimore office (by appointment)

**Filing fees:**
| Service | Fee |
|---------|-----|
| LLC Articles of Organization (online) | $100 + 3% convenience fee |
| LLC Articles of Organization (mail) | $100 |
| Expedited processing (7-10 business days) | +$50 |
| Same-day service (online, by 2:30 PM) | $325 total |
| Same-day service (in-person) | $425 total |

**Processing times:**
- Standard (non-expedited): 6-8 weeks
- Expedited: 7-10 business days
- Same-day: Same business day if submitted by deadline

**Contact:** sdat.charterhelp@maryland.gov

### Step 4: Appoint a Registered Agent (Resident Agent)

**Requirements:**
- Must be one of the following:
  - An adult citizen of Maryland
  - A Maryland corporation, LLC, LP, or LLLP
- Must have a physical street address in Maryland (P.O. Box not acceptable)
- Must be available during business hours to receive legal documents
- The business cannot serve as its own registered agent

**Note:** Sole proprietorships and general partnerships are exempt from this requirement per [Maryland statutes Section 2-108](https://www.peoples-law.org/forming-limited-liability-company-llc-state-maryland).

**Changing your registered agent:**
- File Resolution to Change Principal Office or Resident Agent
- **Fee:** $25
- **Processing time:** 7 business days online

### Step 5: Obtain Federal Employer Identification Number (EIN)

- Apply online at [IRS EIN Application](https://www.irs.gov/businesses/small-businesses-self-employed/apply-for-an-employer-identification-number-ein-online)
- **Fee:** Free
- **Processing:** Immediate (online)
- Required for:
  - Opening business bank accounts
  - Hiring employees
  - Filing federal tax returns
  - State tax registration

### Step 6: Register for Maryland State Taxes

**Comptroller of Maryland registration:**

Register via the [Maryland Combined Registration Online Application](https://interactive.marylandtaxes.gov/webapps/comptrollercra/entrance.asp) or through [Maryland Business Express](https://businessexpress.maryland.gov/start/taxes-and-insurance).

**Common registrations for worker cooperatives:**

| Tax Type | When Required |
|----------|---------------|
| Employer Withholding Tax | If you have employees in Maryland |
| Sales and Use Tax | If selling taxable goods or services |
| Unemployment Insurance | If you have employees |
| Admissions and Amusement Tax | If charging for entertainment, events, etc. |

**Processing time:** Online submissions typically take about two weeks; confirmation arrives by mail.

**Filing frequencies:**
- Monthly filing: Employers withholding $700+ per quarter (due 15th of following month)
- Quarterly filing: Employers with less than $700 quarterly withholding (due last day of month after quarter ends)

**Contact:** (410) 260-7980 or 800-638-2937

### Step 7: Obtain Local Business Licenses

**Trader's License:**
Most Maryland businesses need a trader's license issued by the [Clerk of the Circuit Court](https://jportal.mdcourts.gov/license/index_disclaimer.jsp) in your county or Baltimore City.

**Fees:** Based on average commercial inventory ($15 to $800; up to $2,125 in Baltimore City)

**Additional local requirements:**
- Contact your county's office of inspections, licensing, or permits
- Use the [Maryland Business Express License Research Tool](https://businessexpress.maryland.gov/plan/business-licenses-permits)
- Industry-specific licenses may be required (food service, professional services, etc.)

**State professional licenses:**
- Apply via [Maryland Licensing OneStop Portal](https://www.dllr.state.md.us/license/oplinks.shtml) for regulated occupations

### Step 8: Ongoing Compliance

**Annual Report:**
- **Due:** April 15 each year
- **Fee:** $300 (may be waived through MarylandSaves participation)
- File via [Maryland Business Express](https://businessexpress.maryland.gov/manage/maintain-licenses-and-permits)

**Other ongoing requirements:**
- Maintain registered agent
- Keep operating agreement/cooperative agreement current
- File required tax returns
- Renew local licenses as required

## 4. Governance and Documents You Should Prepare

### Core Governance Documents

**1. Articles of Organization / Certificate of Formation**
- Filed with SDAT to legally create the entity
- Basic information: name, principal office, registered agent, purpose

**2. Operating Agreement / Cooperative Agreement**

This is the most critical document for a worker cooperative. It should address:

**Membership Provisions:**
- Eligibility requirements for worker-membership
- Candidacy/probationary period before full membership
- Member buy-in requirements and payment terms
- Process for admitting new members
- Grounds and process for member removal
- Member exit, retirement, and death provisions
- Transferability of membership interests

**Governance Structure:**
- Voting rights (one member, one vote)
- Member meetings (frequency, quorum, notice requirements)
- Board of directors/managers composition and election
- Officer roles and responsibilities
- Committees (if any)
- Decision-making processes (voting thresholds, consensus options)
- Dispute resolution procedures

**Financial Provisions:**
- Capital contributions and internal capital accounts
- Patronage definition (hours worked, wages earned, etc.)
- Profit and loss allocation methodology
- Distribution policies and timing
- Reserve requirements
- Fiscal year

**3. Member Handbook / Policy Manual**
- Day-to-day operational policies
- Employment policies (if applicable)
- Work expectations and standards
- Benefits information
- Grievance procedures

**4. Membership Agreement**
- Individual agreement signed by each member
- Acknowledgment of rights and responsibilities
- Capital contribution commitment
- Confidentiality provisions

### Template Resources

- [ICA Model Bylaws for Worker Cooperatives](https://institute.coop/sites/default/files/ica-model-bylaws.pdf)
- [DAWI Sample Worker Cooperative Bylaws](https://institute.coop/sites/default/files/SampleWorkerCooperativeBylaws.pdf)
- [Sustainable Economies Law Center Templates](https://www.theselc.org/templates) (includes bilingual English/Spanish templates)
- [Cultivate.Coop Bylaws Resources](https://cultivate.coop/wiki/Cooperative_Bylaws)

### Important Governance Principles

According to [DAWI's guidance](https://institute.coop/news/guide-worker-cooperative-bylaws-and-operating-agreements):
- Bylaws and operating agreements should contain high-level governance information
- More specific operational procedures belong in policy manuals
- Bylaws typically require membership vote to change; policies can be modified by designated bodies
- Documents should be designed to outlast the founding members

## 6. Worker-Ownership Conversions

### Why Consider Converting an Existing Business

Selling a business to its employees offers significant benefits:
- **For sellers:** Potential tax advantages, legacy preservation, rewarding loyal employees
- **For workers:** Job security, wealth-building opportunity, democratic workplace
- **For communities:** Keeps businesses local, maintains economic activity, preserves jobs

According to [BRED](https://www.baltimoreroundtable.org/news/the-case-for-worker-cooperative-conversions), most small businesses never find an outside buyer. A worker buyout keeps businesses operating in their communities.

### Business Readiness Indicators

A business may be a good conversion candidate if it has:
- Minimal debt and strong financial health over the past 3-5 years
- Positive future financial forecasts
- Ability to cover buyout costs even in business downturns
- Employees interested in ownership and democratic governance
- Owners committed to seeing employees succeed
- Revenue sufficient to support $3,000-$5,000+ in annual cooperative maintenance costs

### Conversion Process Overview

**1. Feasibility Assessment**
- Financial analysis of the business
- Employee interest survey
- Preliminary valuation

**2. Deal Structuring**
- Determine purchase price and terms
- Structure financing (often a combination of seller financing, loans, and member equity)
- Plan transition timeline

**3. Legal Conversion**
- Form the cooperative entity (LLC or LWCA)
- Draft operating/cooperative agreement
- Execute purchase agreement
- Transfer ownership

**4. Governance Transition**
- Train worker-members in cooperative governance
- Establish decision-making processes
- Build ownership culture

### Maryland Conversion Resources

- [Baltimore Roundtable](https://www.baltimoreroundtable.org/) offers workshops on selling your business to employees
- [Project Equity](https://project-equity.org/news/employee-ownership-insider/ready-for-worker-cooperative-transition/) provides conversion feasibility assessments and deal structuring
- [DAWI Succession Planning Resources](https://institute.coop/topics/succession-planning)

### Cooperatives vs. Other Employee Ownership Models

| Feature | Worker Cooperative | ESOP | Employee Ownership Trust |
|---------|-------------------|------|-------------------------|
| Minimum employees | 3 (MD LWCA) | Typically 15-20+ | Varies |
| Setup cost | Lower | Higher | Moderate |
| Annual maintenance | $3,000-$5,000 | $15,000-$30,000+ | Moderate |
| Democratic governance | Required | Optional | Varies |
| Best for | Smaller businesses | Larger businesses | Mid-size businesses |

## 7. Common Pitfalls in Maryland

### Formation Pitfalls

**1. Inadequate Operating Documents**
- **Problem:** Using generic LLC templates without cooperative provisions
- **Solution:** Work with an attorney experienced in cooperative law; use [DAWI](https://institute.coop/) or [SELC](https://www.theselc.org/) templates as starting points

**2. Insufficient Capitalization**
- **Problem:** Underestimating startup and operating costs
- **Solution:** Develop detailed financial projections; secure adequate funding before launch; connect with [BRED](https://baltimoreroundtable.org/) for lending options

**3. Skipping the Probationary Period**
- **Problem:** Granting full membership too quickly, leading to poor fit
- **Solution:** Establish a candidacy period (6-12 months common) in your operating agreement

**4. Neglecting State Compliance**
- **Problem:** Missing annual reports, tax filings, or license renewals
- **Solution:** Calendar all deadlines; consider [MarylandSaves](https://www.marylandsaves.org/) participation for annual report fee waiver

### Governance Pitfalls

**5. Confusing Ownership with Management**
- **Problem:** Expecting all decisions to be made by the full membership
- **Solution:** Establish clear delegation of authority; distinguish between membership decisions, board decisions, and management decisions as noted by [Wanderwell Consulting](https://wanderwellconsulting.com/common-cooperative-mistakes/)

**6. "Going Rogue" Members**
- **Problem:** Individual members acting unilaterally without proper authority
- **Solution:** Create accountability structures; clearly define decision-making authority in governance documents

**7. Inadequate Meeting Practices**
- **Problem:** Informal or infrequent meetings leading to poor communication
- **Solution:** Hold regular, structured meetings; document decisions; maintain transparency

### Financial Pitfalls

**8. Unclear Patronage Allocation**
- **Problem:** Disputes over how profits are divided
- **Solution:** Define patronage clearly in operating agreement (hours worked, wages earned, etc.); document methodology

**9. Underfunded Internal Capital Accounts**
- **Problem:** Members leaving with insufficient funds to pay out
- **Solution:** Build adequate reserves; establish realistic payout timelines; consider payout caps

**10. Tax Classification Confusion**
- **Problem:** Choosing inappropriate tax treatment
- **Solution:** Consult with a CPA experienced in cooperative taxation; understand Subchapter T implications

### Maryland-Specific Considerations

**11. Timing the LWCA Statute**
- **Problem:** Uncertainty about whether to form now as LLC or wait for LWCA
- **Solution:** LLCs formed now can potentially convert to LWCA status after October 2026; consult an attorney about your specific timeline

**12. Baltimore City Licensing Costs**
- **Problem:** Higher trader's license fees in Baltimore City (up to $2,125)
- **Solution:** Budget appropriately; factor into startup costs

## 8. FAQs for Maryland

### Q1: Should I wait for the LWCA statute (October 2026) or form an LLC now?

**Answer:** It depends on your timeline. If you need to launch before October 2026, form an LLC with a cooperative operating agreement. LLCs are well-established and flexible. After October 2026, you may be able to convert to an LWCA if the new structure better suits your needs. Consult with an attorney familiar with Maryland cooperative law to evaluate your specific situation.

### Q2: How much does it cost to start a worker cooperative in Maryland?

**Answer:** Budget approximately:
- Formation filing: $100-$150 (LLC)
- Name reservation (optional): $25
- Legal fees for operating agreement: $1,500-$5,000+
- EIN: Free
- State tax registration: Free
- Local business license: $15-$800 (up to $2,125 in Baltimore City)
- Annual report: $300/year (may be waived)

Total first-year costs typically range from $2,000 to $8,000+ depending on legal complexity and local requirements.

### Q3: Can a worker cooperative have non-worker members or investors?

**Answer:** Yes, with proper structuring. The Maryland LWCA statute allows multiple classes of members. Many cooperative LLCs create investor member classes with limited voting rights and fixed returns. However, worker-members should maintain democratic control. Consult with an attorney to structure this appropriately.

### Q4: How are profits distributed in a worker cooperative?

**Answer:** Profits are typically allocated based on "patronage"—a measure of each member's contribution to the cooperative (usually hours worked or wages earned during the fiscal year). This differs from investor-owned businesses where profits are distributed based on capital investment. Your operating agreement should clearly define how patronage is calculated and how distributions are made.

### Q5: Are worker-members considered employees for unemployment and workers' compensation purposes?

**Answer:** Under the new LWCA statute (effective October 2026), worker members are explicitly employees of the association for purposes of state law. For LLCs, the classification depends on how the relationship is structured. Consult with an employment attorney and ensure proper workers' compensation coverage.

### Q6: Where can I find affordable legal help for forming a cooperative in Maryland?

**Answer:** Options include:
- [Baltimore Roundtable for Economic Democracy](https://baltimoreroundtable.org/) for referrals
- [Maryland Volunteer Lawyers Service](https://mvlslaw.org/) for qualifying businesses
- [University of Maryland Carey Law School clinics](https://www.law.umaryland.edu/)
- [Sustainable Economies Law Center templates](https://www.theselc.org/templates) (free/low-cost)
- [DAWI resources](https://institute.coop/) for self-help guidance

### Q7: Can an existing business convert to a worker cooperative?

**Answer:** Yes. Business conversions to worker cooperatives are increasingly common as a succession strategy. The process involves forming a cooperative entity, structuring the buyout, and transitioning ownership to employees. Contact [Baltimore Roundtable](https://baltimoreroundtable.org/) or [Project Equity](https://project-equity.org/) for conversion assistance.

### Q8: What ongoing compliance requirements apply to Maryland worker cooperatives?

**Answer:** Key ongoing requirements include:
- Annual Report to SDAT ($300, due April 15)
- Employer tax filings (withholding, unemployment)
- Sales tax returns (if applicable)
- Local license renewals
- Maintaining registered agent
- Federal tax returns
- Member meetings per your operating agreement
