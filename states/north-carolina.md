# How to Start a Worker Cooperative in North Carolina

A comprehensive guide to forming and operating a worker-owned cooperative business in the Tar Heel State.

---

## Quick Summary: What Makes North Carolina Unique

North Carolina has a **deep-rooted cooperative tradition**, particularly in agriculture, that dates back to the late 1800s when the Farmers' Alliance attracted over 100,000 North Carolinians by 1891. This legacy extends to rural electric cooperatives, which were founded by farmers and continue to serve rural communities as member-owned utilities today.

**Key highlights for worker cooperatives in North Carolina:**

- **Growing worker cooperative movement**: Organizations like [Carolina Common Enterprise](https://commonenterprise.coop/) have been supporting cooperative development across the state since 2012, filling a gap in statewide cooperative support infrastructure.

- **Strong support ecosystem**: The state benefits from institutions like [Self-Help Credit Union](https://www.self-help.org/), which was founded in 1980 specifically to help workers form cooperatives and has since become a major community development financial institution.

- **Active employee ownership network**: The [North Carolina Employee Ownership Center (NCEOC)](https://nceoc.org/) launched in 2019 to educate business owners and advisors about worker cooperatives, ESOPs, and Employee Ownership Trusts.

- **Successful examples**: Notable worker cooperatives include Opportunity Threads (a cut-and-sew factory in Morganton), Caktus Group (a web development firm in Durham), Tangerine Clean (a home cleaning cooperative in Durham), and Electric Violin Shop (a Durham music business that converted to worker ownership).

- **Agricultural and food cooperative strength**: From Weaver Street Market (a multistakeholder cooperative in Carrboro and Hillsborough) to the Sandhills Farm to Table Cooperative, North Carolina has vibrant food-focused cooperative models.

- **Flexible legal structures**: While North Carolina has cooperative statutes under [Chapter 54 of the NC General Statutes](https://www.ncleg.gov/Laws/GeneralStatuteSections/Chapter54), most worker cooperatives choose the LLC structure for its flexibility and simplicity.

## Best-Fit Legal Structures for Worker Cooperatives in North Carolina

### Option 1: Limited Liability Company (LLC) - Most Common Choice

The **LLC structured as a worker cooperative** is the most popular choice for North Carolina worker cooperatives due to its flexibility and straightforward formation process.

**Advantages:**
- Formation filing fee of only $125 with the [NC Secretary of State](https://www.sosnc.gov/divisions/business_registration)
- Highly flexible Operating Agreement can be customized for democratic governance
- Pass-through taxation simplifies tax compliance
- Members can define voting rights, profit distribution, and membership criteria
- Fewer formalities than corporations

**Considerations:**
- Cannot use "Cooperative" or "Co-op" in the business name without authorization
- Operating Agreement must be carefully drafted to preserve cooperative principles
- May not qualify for Subchapter T tax treatment without careful structuring
- Future members could potentially amend the Operating Agreement to remove cooperative elements

**Governing Law:** [NC Limited Liability Company Act (Chapter 57D)](https://www.ncleg.gov/EnactedLegislation/Statutes/PDF/BySection/Chapter_57d/GS_57d-2-30.pdf)

### Option 2: Cooperative Corporation Under Chapter 54

North Carolina's [Chapter 54 of the General Statutes](https://www.ncleg.gov/Laws/GeneralStatuteSections/Chapter54) provides specific frameworks for cooperative organizations.

**Key provisions:**
- **Subchapter IV (Cooperative Associations)**: Covers general cooperative associations with Articles 16-18 addressing organization, stockholders/officers, and powers/duties
- **[G.S. 54-142](https://codes.findlaw.com/nc/chapter-54-cooperative-organizations/)**: Application of NC Business Corporation Act to cooperative associations with capital stock
- **[G.S. 54-142.1](https://codes.findlaw.com/nc/chapter-54-cooperative-organizations/)**: Application of Nonprofit Corporation Act to cooperative associations without capital stock

**Advantages:**
- Clear legal framework with cooperative principles embedded in statute
- May qualify more readily for Subchapter T tax treatment
- Democratic governance requirements help protect cooperative identity
- Recognized cooperative structure provides legitimacy

**Considerations:**
- More complex formation process than LLC
- Less flexibility in governance structure
- Must comply with specific statutory requirements
- $125 filing fee for Articles of Incorporation

### Option 3: Business Corporation with Cooperative Structure

A standard North Carolina business corporation can be structured to operate as a worker cooperative through carefully drafted Articles of Incorporation and Bylaws.

**When this makes sense:**
- When Subchapter T tax treatment is essential
- When the cooperative wants a more traditional corporate structure
- When seeking certain types of investors or grants

**Filing fee:** $125 for Articles of Incorporation

### Comparison Table

| Feature | LLC | Cooperative Corp (Ch. 54) | Business Corporation |
|---------|-----|--------------------------|---------------------|
| Filing Fee | $125 | $125 | $125 |
| Formation Complexity | Low | Medium | Medium |
| Governance Flexibility | High | Medium | Medium |
| "Cooperative" in Name | Requires permission | Allowed | Requires permission |
| Subchapter T Eligibility | Possible with care | More straightforward | Possible with care |
| Member Protection | Via Operating Agreement | Built into statute | Via Bylaws |
| Annual Report Fee | $200 | $200 | $25 |

### Tax Considerations: Subchapter T

[Subchapter T of the Internal Revenue Code](https://www.law.cornell.edu/uscode/text/26/subtitle-A/chapter-1/subchapter-T) (Sections 1381-1388) provides special tax treatment for cooperatives. According to the [NC Bar's analysis](https://www.ncbarblog.com/tax-taxation-of-cooperatives-under-subchapter-t/), key requirements include:

1. **Democratic Control**: One member, one vote
2. **Subordination of Capital**: Prioritize member benefits over capital investment
3. **Equitable Allocation**: Earnings distributed based on patronage (work performed)

Cooperatives qualifying for Subchapter T can deduct **patronage dividends** from taxable income, avoiding double taxation. At least 20% of patronage dividends must be paid in cash, and distributions must occur within 8.5 months after the tax year closes.

## Step-by-Step Formation Checklist

### Phase 1: Planning and Preparation (4-8 weeks)

**Step 1: Conduct a Feasibility Study**
- [ ] Research your industry and market
- [ ] Develop a business plan with cooperative principles
- [ ] Assess potential worker-members and their commitment
- [ ] Consider consulting with [Carolina Common Enterprise](https://commonenterprise.coop/services/) for a free initial consultation

**Step 2: Choose Your Legal Structure**
- [ ] Evaluate LLC vs. Cooperative Corporation vs. Business Corporation
- [ ] Consult with an attorney familiar with cooperative law
- [ ] Consider tax implications and Subchapter T eligibility
- [ ] Review governance needs and member composition

**Step 3: Select and Reserve Your Business Name**
- [ ] Search the [NC Secretary of State Business Registration database](https://www.sosnc.gov/online_services/search/by_title/_Business_Registration) for name availability
- [ ] Ensure name does not contain restricted words ("Cooperative," "Co-op," "Mutual," "Trust") without proper authorization
- [ ] Reserve name for up to 120 days for a $30 fee (optional but recommended)

### Phase 2: Legal Formation (2-4 weeks)

**Step 4: Designate a Registered Agent**
- [ ] Select an individual residing in NC or a registered NC business entity
- [ ] Ensure registered office has a physical NC street address (no P.O. boxes)
- [ ] Registered agent must be available during regular business hours
- [ ] Review [NC registered agent requirements](https://www.sosnc.gov/divisions/business_registration/registered_agent_links)

**Step 5: File Formation Documents with NC Secretary of State**

*For LLC:*
- [ ] Complete Form L-01 (Articles of Organization)
- [ ] Complete BE-01 Cover Sheet
- [ ] Submit filing fee: **$125** (online or mail)
- [ ] Optional expedited processing: $100 (24-hour) or $200 (same-day)

*For Cooperative Corporation:*
- [ ] Prepare Articles of Incorporation per Chapter 54 requirements
- [ ] Include required elements: name, purpose, registered agent, share structure
- [ ] Submit filing fee: **$125**

**Filing Options:**
- **Online**: [NC Secretary of State Business Registration Portal](https://www.sosnc.gov/divisions/business_registration)
- **Mail**: NC Secretary of State, P.O. Box 29622, Raleigh, NC 27626-0622
- **In-Person**: 2 South Salisbury Street, Raleigh, NC 27601

*Processing times*: 7-10 business days standard; 15-20 days during peak periods

**Step 6: Draft Governing Documents**

*For LLC - Operating Agreement:*
- [ ] Define member classes and ownership structure
- [ ] Establish one-member-one-vote democratic governance
- [ ] Specify profit/loss allocation based on patronage
- [ ] Include buy-in and buy-out procedures
- [ ] Address decision-making processes and meeting requirements
- [ ] Consider using [DAWI's Template Operating Agreement](https://institute.coop/) as a starting point

*For Cooperative Corporation - Bylaws:*
- [ ] Detail membership requirements and procedures
- [ ] Establish board of directors structure
- [ ] Define voting procedures and member rights
- [ ] Specify patronage dividend distribution methods

### Phase 3: Federal and State Tax Registration (1-2 weeks)

**Step 7: Obtain Federal Employer Identification Number (EIN)**
- [ ] Apply free through [IRS.gov](https://www.irs.gov/businesses/small-businesses-self-employed/get-an-employer-identification-number)
- [ ] Application available online (instant approval), by fax, or mail
- [ ] Required before opening business bank accounts or hiring employees
- [ ] Beware of third-party sites charging fees for this free service

**Step 8: Register with NC Department of Revenue**
- [ ] Use the [Online Business Registration system](https://eservices.dor.nc.gov/ncbusreg/) for most tax types
- [ ] Register for applicable taxes:
  - [ ] Withholding Tax (if hiring employees)
  - [ ] Sales and Use Tax (if selling taxable goods/services)
- [ ] No fee for online registration
- [ ] Need: EIN, NC Secretary of State ID number, business details
- [ ] Mail option: Form NC-BR to P.O. Box 25000, Raleigh, NC 27640-0100

For more information, visit [NC Department of Revenue Business Registration](https://www.ncdor.gov/registration).

### Phase 4: Local Compliance (1-2 weeks)

**Step 9: Research Local Business Requirements**
- [ ] Contact your city hall or county clerk for local requirements
- [ ] Note: North Carolina does not require a general state business license
- [ ] [Business Link North Carolina](https://www.sosnc.gov/divisions/business_registration) offers free consultation: 1-800-228-8443

**Common local requirements may include:**
- [ ] Zoning permits (verify location is zoned for your business type)
- [ ] Home occupation permit (if operating from home)
- [ ] Health permits (for food service businesses)
- [ ] Building permits (for construction or renovation)
- [ ] Specialty licenses based on your industry

**City-Specific Notes:**
- **Raleigh**: No general business license required; beer/wine license needed for alcohol sales
- **Charlotte**: Home-based businesses need zoning approval
- **Greensboro**: Permits required for peddlers, mobile food vendors, massage businesses

### Phase 5: Operational Setup (Ongoing)

**Step 10: Complete Business Setup**
- [ ] Open a business bank account
- [ ] Obtain appropriate business insurance (liability, workers' compensation)
- [ ] Set up payroll system for worker-members
- [ ] Establish internal capital accounts for patronage tracking
- [ ] Create member education and onboarding program

**Step 11: File Annual Requirements**
- [ ] Annual Report due by April 15 each year ($200 fee for LLCs; $25 for corporations)
- [ ] File via [NC Secretary of State eBusiness Center](https://www.sosnc.gov/)
- [ ] Maintain registered agent information (update within 60 days of any change; $5 fee)

## Governance and Documents You Should Prepare

### Essential Formation Documents

**1. Articles of Organization/Incorporation**
Required filing with NC Secretary of State containing:
- Legal name of the cooperative
- Principal office address
- Registered agent name and address
- Purpose statement
- Management structure (member-managed or manager-managed for LLCs)
- Duration (perpetual or specific term)

**2. Operating Agreement (LLC) or Bylaws (Corporation)**
The heart of your cooperative's governance. Should address:

*Membership:*
- Eligibility requirements (hours worked, probationary period)
- Application and approval process
- Member buy-in amount and payment terms
- Rights and responsibilities of members
- Membership classes (if applicable)
- Resignation and expulsion procedures

*Democratic Governance:*
- One-member-one-vote principle
- Meeting requirements (frequency, notice, quorum)
- Voting thresholds for different decisions
- Board of directors composition and election
- Officer positions and duties
- Committee structure

*Economic Participation:*
- Patronage allocation formula (typically based on hours worked)
- Profit distribution timing and method
- Internal capital accounts
- Retained earnings policy
- Member loan provisions (if applicable)

*Operations:*
- Decision-making processes (consensus, majority vote, supermajority)
- Hiring procedures for new workers
- Work expectations and performance standards
- Compensation structure
- Benefits and leave policies

*Exit Provisions:*
- Voluntary withdrawal procedures
- Involuntary termination process
- Capital account redemption timeline
- Non-compete or confidentiality provisions
- Dispute resolution mechanisms

**3. Membership Agreement**
Individual contract between the cooperative and each member covering:
- Member's capital contribution
- Work commitment expectations
- Acknowledgment of bylaws/operating agreement
- Confidentiality provisions
- Signatures and effective date

**4. Subscription Agreement**
Documents the member's financial investment:
- Amount of membership share purchase
- Payment terms (lump sum or installments)
- Refund conditions
- Tax implications acknowledgment

### Recommended Additional Documents

**5. Member Handbook**
Operational guide covering:
- Day-to-day policies and procedures
- Communication channels
- Work schedules and time tracking
- Safety protocols
- Professional development opportunities

**6. Board and Committee Charters**
If your cooperative has a board:
- Board responsibilities and authority
- Meeting procedures
- Committee descriptions and purposes
- Fiduciary duties

**7. Patronage Records System**
Internal tracking for:
- Hours worked by each member
- Patronage allocation calculations
- Internal capital account statements
- Distribution records

**8. Meeting Minutes Template**
Document all formal decisions:
- Member meetings
- Board meetings
- Committee meetings

### Sample Governance Structure for a Worker Cooperative LLC

```
MEMBERSHIP
    |
    v
GENERAL MEMBER MEETING (Highest authority)
    - Elects Board of Directors
    - Approves major decisions (merger, dissolution, bylaw changes)
    - Meets quarterly or annually
    |
    v
BOARD OF DIRECTORS (Strategic oversight)
    - Sets policy direction
    - Hires/evaluates management
    - Financial oversight
    - Meets monthly
    |
    v
OFFICERS/MANAGERS (Day-to-day operations)
    - President/Coordinator
    - Secretary
    - Treasurer
    - Operations Manager
    |
    v
COMMITTEES (Specialized functions)
    - Membership Committee
    - Finance Committee
    - Personnel Committee
    - Marketing Committee
```

## Worker-Ownership Conversions

### Why Consider Converting to a Worker Cooperative?

Business succession is a growing challenge in North Carolina. According to research cited by NCEOC, the population of North Carolinians reaching retirement age will grow by more than 200,000 over 15 years, potentially reaching 1.5 million by 2030. Yet 80% of businesses never sell, leaving employees and communities at risk.

**Benefits of conversion to worker ownership:**
- Business owner receives fair market value
- Employees keep their jobs and gain ownership
- Community retains local business and tax base
- Seller's legacy continues through worker-owners
- Potential tax advantages for selling shareholders

### The Conversion Process

**Phase 1: Assessment (3-6 months)**

1. **Owner education and commitment**
   - Learn about worker cooperative model
   - Attend NCEOC or CCE informational sessions
   - Understand the timeline (typically 1-3 years for full conversion)

2. **Business evaluation**
   - Financial health assessment
   - Management structure review
   - Employee readiness evaluation
   - Cultural fit for democratic ownership

3. **Feasibility study**
   - Market analysis and business valuation
   - Legal structure options
   - Financing scenarios
   - Worker-member interest survey

**Phase 2: Planning (6-12 months)**

4. **Transaction structure design**
   - Determine sale price and terms
   - Design payment structure (often seller financing over time)
   - Plan for worker buy-in contributions
   - Address outstanding debt and liabilities

5. **Governance development**
   - Draft cooperative operating agreement/bylaws
   - Design board structure and voting procedures
   - Create membership policies
   - Plan for leadership transition

6. **Employee engagement**
   - Education about cooperative model and ownership responsibilities
   - Gauge interest and commitment
   - Identify emerging leaders
   - Develop training programs

**Phase 3: Implementation (3-12 months)**

7. **Legal formation**
   - File new cooperative entity or amend existing entity
   - Execute sale/purchase agreements
   - Transfer ownership interest to cooperative
   - File necessary tax elections

8. **Financing closing**
   - Finalize bank financing (if any)
   - Complete seller financing arrangements
   - Establish member capital contribution schedule
   - Set up internal capital accounts

9. **Operational transition**
   - Transfer management responsibilities
   - Implement democratic governance
   - Begin member education program
   - Establish patronage tracking systems

**Phase 4: Stabilization (1-3 years)**

10. **Ongoing support**
    - Regular member meetings and training
    - Financial management and reporting
    - Board development
    - Culture building and conflict resolution

## Common Pitfalls in North Carolina

### Formation Mistakes

**1. Using "Cooperative" or "Co-op" in the Name Without Authorization**
North Carolina restricts the use of words "Cooperative," "Co-op," "Mutual," and "Trust" in business names. You must receive permission from the proper authority to use these terms. Apply for authorization before filing formation documents.

**2. Inadequate Operating Agreement/Bylaws**
The default NC LLC rules may not align with cooperative principles. Your Operating Agreement must explicitly establish:
- One-member-one-vote governance
- Patronage-based profit distribution
- Democratic decision-making procedures
- Protections against future amendment away from cooperative principles

**3. Failing to Understand Entity Choice Implications**
Each structure (LLC, cooperative corporation, business corporation) has different:
- Tax treatment eligibility
- Governance requirements
- Flexibility for future changes
- Compliance obligations

Consult with an attorney and tax advisor before choosing.

**4. Skipping the Registered Agent Requirement**
All NC business entities must maintain a registered agent with a physical NC address. Failure to update registered agent information within 60 days of changes can lead to administrative dissolution.

### Tax and Compliance Errors

**5. Not Properly Qualifying for Subchapter T Treatment**
If you want cooperative tax treatment:
- Ensure democratic control (one member, one vote)
- Distribute earnings based on patronage, not capital
- Follow patronage dividend timing rules (within 8.5 months)
- Pay at least 20% of patronage dividends in cash
- Maintain proper documentation

**6. Forgetting State Tax Registration**
After forming your entity and obtaining an EIN, you must separately register with [NC Department of Revenue](https://www.ncdor.gov/registration) for:
- Withholding tax (if hiring employees)
- Sales and use tax (if applicable)
- Other industry-specific taxes

**7. Missing Annual Report Deadlines**
- LLCs: $200 annual report due by April 15
- Corporations: $25 annual report
- Failure to file can result in administrative dissolution

### Operational Challenges

**8. Insufficient Member Education**
Worker-ownership requires different skills than traditional employment. Members need training in:
- Financial literacy and reading financial statements
- Democratic decision-making and facilitation
- Conflict resolution
- Business management basics

**9. Unclear Membership Path**
Define clearly:
- Probationary period length
- Buy-in amount and payment options
- Responsibilities that come with membership
- Process for involuntary termination

**10. Underestimating Capital Needs**
Worker cooperatives often struggle with:
- Startup capital (members may have limited resources)
- Working capital for growth
- Member buyout obligations when members leave
- Building reserves for economic downturns

Plan for seller financing, member contributions over time, and loans from mission-aligned lenders like Self-Help Credit Union.

### Governance Issues

**11. Not Documenting Decisions**
Keep minutes of all:
- Member meetings
- Board meetings
- Major operational decisions
Follow your bylaws/operating agreement procedures strictly.

**12. Allowing Governance Drift**
Without vigilance, cooperatives can lose democratic character over time. Protect against this by:
- Requiring supermajority votes to change core cooperative provisions
- Regular member education about cooperative principles
- Periodic governance reviews

**13. Inadequate Conflict Resolution Mechanisms**
Democratic workplaces still have conflicts. Establish:
- Clear grievance procedures
- Mediation resources
- Escalation paths
- Fair termination processes

## Frequently Asked Questions (FAQs) for North Carolina

### 1. Do I need a special license to form a worker cooperative in North Carolina?

No special cooperative license is required. Worker cooperatives form under standard business entity statutes (usually as an LLC or corporation). However, if you want to use "Cooperative" or "Co-op" in your business name, you must obtain authorization from the Secretary of State. This involves demonstrating that your business will operate according to cooperative principles. Contact the [NC Secretary of State Business Registration Division](https://www.sosnc.gov/divisions/business_registration) for specific requirements.

### 2. What is the minimum number of members required to start a worker cooperative in North Carolina?

North Carolina law does not specify a minimum number of members for a worker cooperative LLC. You can form an LLC with a single member, though this would not be a true "cooperative" in practice. For cooperative corporations under Chapter 54, requirements vary by article. Practically speaking, most worker cooperatives function best with at least 3-5 members to enable meaningful democratic governance and distribute workload. The IRS may scrutinize very small cooperatives claiming Subchapter T treatment.

### 3. How much does it cost to form a worker cooperative in North Carolina?

Basic formation costs include:
- **Articles of Organization (LLC) or Incorporation (Corporation)**: $125
- **Name reservation (optional)**: $30
- **Federal EIN**: Free from IRS
- **NC Department of Revenue registration**: Free

Additional costs to budget for:
- **Attorney fees for Operating Agreement/Bylaws**: $1,500-$5,000+
- **Expedited filing (optional)**: $100 (24-hour) or $200 (same-day)
- **Annual report**: $200 for LLCs, $25 for corporations
- **Registered agent service (if not self-serving)**: $50-$300/year

Consider the free initial consultation from [Carolina Common Enterprise](https://commonenterprise.coop/) to understand your specific needs.

### 4. Can worker-members be both employees and owners in North Carolina?

Yes, and this is the standard structure for worker cooperatives. Worker-members are typically:
- **Employees** for employment law purposes (entitled to minimum wage, overtime, workers' compensation, payroll tax withholding)
- **Owners** for business purposes (voting rights, share of profits through patronage)

This dual status is important for tax compliance and labor law. Unlike some other structures, cooperative corporations generally presume worker-members are employees. For LLCs, the employment status depends on how the cooperative is structured. Consult with an employment attorney to ensure compliance with NC labor laws.

### 5. How are profits distributed in a North Carolina worker cooperative?

Profits are typically distributed based on **patronage**-the amount of work each member contributed (usually measured in hours worked or wages earned), not based on capital investment. This distinguishes cooperatives from traditional corporations where profits go to shareholders based on stock ownership.

A typical distribution approach:
1. Set aside funds for taxes and reserves
2. Calculate each member's patronage (e.g., hours worked / total hours)
3. Allocate net income based on patronage percentages
4. Distribute some as cash and retain some in internal capital accounts
5. Pay out retained amounts when members leave or according to a schedule

For Subchapter T qualification, at least 20% of patronage dividends must be paid in cash, and distributions must occur within 8.5 months of the fiscal year end.

- [NC Employee Ownership Center](https://nceoc.org/) - education and referrals

**Financing:**

### 7. Can I convert my existing North Carolina business to a worker cooperative?

Yes, business conversion to worker cooperative ownership is increasingly popular for succession planning. The process typically involves:

1. **Valuation** of the existing business
2. **Feasibility assessment** of worker-ownership
3. **Formation** of the cooperative entity (or restructuring existing entity)
4. **Sale** of ownership interest to the cooperative
5. **Financing** often through seller notes, bank loans, and member contributions over time
6. **Transition** of management and governance

Contact [NCEOC](https://nceoc.org/) or [Carolina Common Enterprise](https://commonenterprise.coop/) for guidance specific to your situation. The timeline is typically 1-3 years for a full transition.

### 8. What are the annual compliance requirements for a North Carolina worker cooperative?

**State filings:**
- **Annual Report**: Due by April 15 each year via [NC Secretary of State](https://www.sosnc.gov/)
  - LLCs: $200
  - Corporations: $25
- **Registered agent updates**: Within 60 days of any change ($5 fee)

**Tax filings:**
- **Federal income tax return** (Form 1120-C for cooperatives or appropriate LLC form)
- **NC corporate income/franchise tax** (if applicable)
- **Form 1099-PATR** to members for patronage dividends over $10
- **Payroll tax deposits and filings** (federal and state)
- **Sales tax returns** (if applicable)

**Internal requirements:**
- **Member meetings** as specified in your governing documents
- **Financial statements** and patronage calculations
- **Capital account statements** to members
- **Meeting minutes** documentation

Ready to start your worker cooperative journey in North Carolina? Here is your action plan:

### Immediate Actions (This Week)

1. **Schedule a free consultation** with [Carolina Common Enterprise](https://commonenterprise.coop/contact/) to discuss your cooperative concept and get personalized guidance from North Carolina's dedicated cooperative development center.

2. **Research your market** and begin drafting a business plan that incorporates cooperative principles of democratic governance and patronage-based economics.

7. **Engage legal counsel** experienced with cooperatives to draft your Operating Agreement or Bylaws. Carolina Common Enterprise has attorneys on staff who specialize in this work.

11. **Establish your governance practices** - hold your first official member meeting, elect any officers or board, and begin documenting decisions.

12. **Begin operations** with clear patronage tracking and member education programs.
